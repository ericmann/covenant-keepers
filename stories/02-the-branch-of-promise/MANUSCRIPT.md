## Chapter 1: Abuelo's Second Entry

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 17, 2018 — Near Mount Ararat, Turkey*
*Everyone on this expedition thinks I'm chasing ghosts. The other scientists say there's nothing on this mountain. "It's been studied for two hundred years," they tell me. "There is nothing up there." I smiled and drank my tea. I've spent my career looking for things everyone says don't exist. Sometimes I wonder if that's the whole point — not finding the thing, but being faithful enough to keep looking.*

---

Three days had passed since the Valley of Elah, and Eli still couldn't stop looking at his hand.

He sat on the floor of Abuelo's hidden study, his back against the desk, turning his right palm toward the window light. The scar from the camping trip was there, thin and familiar. But the warmth was gone. The phantom weight of the stone had faded sometime during the second night, and now his hand was just a hand again.

Elena was upside down in the leather armchair, her legs hooked over the back, flipping through one of Abuelo's journals. She'd been through four of them already, scanning for sketches and ignoring most of the text. "There's a drawing of a boat in this one," she said. "A really big boat."

"Don't bend the spine," Eli said.

"I'm not bending it. I'm reading it. Upside down. It's a skill."

Jordan sat cross-legged near the glass cases, a rubber band stretched between his fingers. He hadn't said much today. He'd come over that morning the same way he had every morning since their adventure — knocking once, letting himself in, walking straight to the study like it was the only room in the house that made sense anymore.

"So are we going to talk about it?" Jordan said. He wasn't looking at either of them. His eyes were on the Pathfinder.

The bronze disc sat on the desk above Eli's head. One symbol glowed — the stone, sharp-edged and amber, pulsing with its slow, quiet heartbeat. The other eleven were still faint scratches in old metal.

"Talk about what?" Elena said.

"About the fact that three days ago we were in ancient Israel watching a kid fight a giant, and now we're just sitting here like it was a movie we watched."

"It wasn't a movie," Eli said quietly.

"I know it wasn't a movie. That's my point." Jordan snapped the rubber band against his wrist. "I still had sand in my shoes when we got back. Sand from three thousand years ago. I put it in a sandwich bag. I don't know why. I just — I needed proof it happened."

Elena flipped right-side up. "It happened."

"Yeah. So when does it happen again?"

The Pathfinder answered him.

The hum came first — low, familiar, rising from somewhere inside the bronze like a voice clearing its throat. Eli felt it in his ribs before he heard it with his ears. He scrambled to his feet and turned.

The disc was glowing. Not the single amber symbol — the whole face, a warm light spreading outward from the center like ripples in still water. And the second symbol, the one that looked like a rough sketch of a branch, was trembling. The faint lines shivered in the bronze, blurring and re-forming, as if the engraving was trying to become something more.

"It's doing the thing," Elena said, already on her feet.

"Nobody touch it yet," Eli said. He pushed his hair back and leaned closer. The journal he'd found last time — Abuelo's sketch of the olive branch, the notes about Genesis 8 — was still open on the desk where he'd left it. He looked at the sketch, then at the trembling symbol on the Pathfinder.

They matched.

"A flood," he whispered. "A dove. A branch."

"Eli." Jordan's voice was tight. He was staring at the Pathfinder with wide eyes, his glasses reflecting the bronze glow. "It's getting brighter."

"I know."

"So either we touch it or we run. And I don't think running is an option."

Elena reached past both of them and put her hand on the disc.

The light swallowed the room.

## Chapter 2: The Builder

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 19, 2018 — Base camp, Mount Ararat region*
*The locals near this mountain all tell flood stories — a good man, a warning no one believed, water that swallowed the world. Some people say these stories borrowed from each other. But maybe the story is just that old. Maybe a man really did build a boat in the middle of dry land, and everyone who watched him thought he'd lost his mind.*

---

The heat was different this time. Wet and heavy, like stepping into a greenhouse in August. The air tasted green — thick with growing things, with soil and sap and the sharp sweetness of overripe fruit.

Eli opened his eyes. They were standing on a dirt road at the edge of a settlement. Low houses of mud brick and timber lined both sides, with flat roofs where laundry dried in the sun. Goats wandered between doorways. Children ran barefoot through the dust. In the distance, terraced hills rose in layers of dark green, and beyond them, the sky was a hard, perfect blue without a single cloud.

Not a single cloud. The sky looked painted.

"Where are we?" Elena said. She was wearing a loose linen tunic, her hair covered by a cloth. She tugged at it immediately.

"When are we," Jordan corrected. He looked down at himself — another rough-woven tunic, sandals, no glasses. He squinted and touched his face. "Great. I can barely see."

"The Pathfinder fixed your vision last time," Eli said. "Give it a second."

Jordan blinked several times. His squint relaxed. "Okay. That's weird. But helpful."

Eli turned slowly, taking in the settlement. It looked prosperous. The houses were well-built, the gardens tended. People moved through the streets with the easy pace of a community that had enough — enough food, enough water, enough of everything. Somewhere nearby, someone was playing a stringed instrument, and laughter floated from an open doorway.

Then he heard the hammering.

It came from beyond the settlement, past the last row of houses, where the road curved around a low hill. A steady, rhythmic pounding — wood on wood — like a heartbeat made of timber.

"That way," Eli said.

They followed the sound around the hill, and when the road opened up, all three of them stopped.

The Ark was enormous.

Not big. Not large. *Enormous* — the way a mountain is enormous, the way something can be so far beyond the expected scale that your brain refuses to process it and just goes quiet. It sat in a clearing that must have been hacked from the surrounding forest over months or years. The hull was half-finished, its ribs rising from a massive keel like the skeleton of something prehistoric. Timber beams thicker than Eli's whole body were stacked in rows nearby, and the smell of cut wood and pitch — a thick, dark tar that kept water out — filled the clearing like a fog.

Men worked along the scaffolding — five, maybe six, hauling beams, driving pegs, smearing dark pitch along the seams with long brushes. They worked without speaking. Their faces were set, their movements efficient and grim, like people performing a task they'd committed to long ago and would not stop now regardless of what anyone thought.

One man stood at the base of the hull, studying a joint where two beams met. He was older — grey-bearded, broad across the shoulders, with thick hands scarred from years of labor. He ran his palm along the seam, pressed it, nodded once. Then he picked up a mallet and drove a wooden peg into the joint with three precise strikes.

"Noah," Eli breathed.

Noah didn't look like a hero. He looked tired. Deep-tired, the kind that sleep doesn't fix — the weariness of a man who had been doing something hard for a very long time with no end in sight and no applause. But his hands were steady. His eyes were clear. And when one of the younger men on the scaffolding called down a question, Noah's answer was calm and certain, the voice of someone who knew exactly what he was building, even if no one else did.

"How long has he been at this?" Jordan asked.

"Years," Eli said. "The Bible doesn't say exactly. But a long time."

Jordan stared at the half-finished hull. At the mountains of timber. At the old man with the mallet and the steady hands.

"He's either crazy," Jordan said quietly, "or the bravest person I've ever seen."

From the road behind them came the sound of laughter — but not the warm kind they'd heard in the settlement. This laughter had edges. Eli turned. A group of men and women had gathered at the curve of the road, watching Noah and his sons work. Some leaned against each other, smirking. One man cupped his hands around his mouth and shouted something that made the others roar.

Noah didn't look up. His sons didn't look up. They kept working. Mallet strikes. The scrape of pitch. The groan of timber settling into place.

The mockers laughed harder.

And in the hard blue sky above them, not a single cloud moved.

## Chapter 3: The Mockery

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 21, 2018 — Base camp, Mount Ararat region*
*Someone asked me at dinner why I keep searching when I never find anything. I told her the searching was the thing. It's hard to explain what it feels like to believe something no one around you believes — not dramatic, just... alone. Year after year. I think I understand Noah better than most people would admit.*

---

The mockery was worse than Eli expected.

Not because it was cruel — though it was. Because it was casual. The people of the settlement didn't rage at Noah. They didn't throw stones or make threats. They laughed. They rolled their eyes. They walked past the clearing on their way to the market and made jokes the way you'd joke about a neighbor who painted his house an ugly color — amused, dismissive, already moving on.

"Still building the boat, old man?"

"When's this flood of yours coming? My garden could use the water!"

"My son needs timber for a new roof. You could spare a beam — you'll never finish that thing anyway."

Elena's fists were clenched. Eli saw the knuckles go white.

"They're so *mean*," she said through her teeth.

"They don't think they're being mean," Jordan said. He was watching the crowd with his head tilted, the way he did when he was sorting through something. "They think they're being reasonable. The sky is clear. The ground is dry. There's no flood. He's been building this thing for years and nothing has happened. From their side, he's the crazy one."

"He's NOT crazy."

"I didn't say he was. I said they think he is. That's different."

Elena turned to Eli. "Can't we do something? Tell them to stop? Tell them he's right?"

"We can't change anything," Eli said. The words tasted heavy. "The story happens how the story happens."

"Then why are we here?"

Eli didn't have an answer for that. Not yet.

They spent the day near the clearing, watching. Noah's sons — Shem, Ham, and Japheth, Eli was fairly sure — worked alongside their father in near-silence. Their wives brought food and water at midday, and the family ate together in the shadow of the half-built hull, surrounded by sawdust and wood shavings and the faint smell of pitch.

Nobody from the settlement joined them. Nobody brought extra food or offered to help. The family ate alone, the way they always ate alone, the way they'd been eating alone for years.

In the afternoon, a group of younger men from the settlement drifted toward the clearing. They weren't mocking — not exactly. They were curious, the way people are curious about a car wreck. They circled the Ark's base, running their hands along the pitch-sealed hull, knocking on beams, making observations to each other in low voices punctuated by laughter.

One of them called up to Japheth on the scaffolding. "How much longer, friend? Another ten years?"

Japheth didn't answer. His jaw tightened. He drove a peg so hard the mallet bounced.

"Come down and have a drink," the young man said. "Your father's dream can wait one evening."

Shem's wife — a small, quiet woman who had been gathering sawdust for bedding — straightened and looked at the young man with an expression that could have cut timber. She didn't say a word. She didn't need to. The young man raised his hands, laughing, and backed away.

"Whole family's gone mad," he said to his friends as they left. "Sad, really."

Jordan watched them go. He was pulling at his lower lip — a habit Eli hadn't noticed before. "That's the worst part," Jordan said. "They feel sorry for him. They're not even angry. They just think he's lost it."

"Wouldn't you?" Eli said. He hated the question as soon as he asked it, but it was honest. "If your neighbor spent years building a massive boat and told you God said a flood was coming — and the weather was perfect, every single day — what would you think?"

Jordan was quiet for a long time. "I don't know," he said finally. "But I'd like to think I wouldn't laugh."

The wrongness crept in around noon.

Elena noticed first. "I'm cold," she said, rubbing her arms. She was standing in full sunlight. Sweat ran down her temples. But she shivered.

Jordan blinked and rubbed his eyes. "Is it just me, or does everything look... too sharp? Like the contrast is turned up."

Eli felt it in his chest — that low hum, not quite a sound, more like a pressure. He looked at the sky. Blue. Empty. Perfect. Not a wisp of cloud, not a haze on the horizon. The sun blazed down, and the settlement gleamed in the light, every surface bright and clean and *fine*.

That was the problem. Everything was fine. Everything looked exactly the way it should look if Noah was wrong. The sky was too clear. The sun was too bright. The world was shouting, with every blade of grass and every blue inch of sky: *There is no flood coming. There has never been a flood. This old man and his boat are a joke.*

"Do you feel that?" Eli said.

"Feel what?" Jordan said. "I feel like my eyes are lying to me, if that's what you mean."

"It's the same thing as the valley. The fog. Except it's not fog this time."

"No," Jordan said slowly. "It's the opposite of fog. It's too clear. Too obvious. Everything's too normal." He paused. Pushed hair out of his face. "Which is exactly what would make you doubt."

Below them, a woman walked past the clearing with a basket on her hip. She glanced at Noah, shook her head with a pitying smile, and kept walking. Her children trailed behind her. One of them — a boy about Elena's age — stopped and stared at the Ark's massive ribs for a long moment, something flickering across his face that might have been wonder.

His mother called him. He turned and ran after her, and the wonder was gone.

Elena was shaking. Not from cold — from anger. "Nobody even *looks*. They walk right past it. It's the biggest thing anyone's ever built and they just — they don't even see it."

"They see it," Eli said. "They just decided it doesn't mean anything."

Noah set down his mallet. He straightened slowly, pressing a hand to his lower back, and looked out over the settlement. For a moment — just a moment — something crossed his face that Eli recognized. Not doubt, exactly. Something heavier. The weight of being the only person in the room who hears the music.

Then Noah picked up the mallet again and drove the next peg home.

## Chapter 4: The Door

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 22, 2018 — Base camp, Mount Ararat region*
*I dreamed about the animals last night. The Bible says they came to Noah on their own — he didn't go out and catch them. Two by two, every kind, just walking up to a boat that wasn't even in any water. You can laugh at a man building a boat on dry land. But when the animals start showing up on their own, the laughter gets harder to hold.*

---

The animals came on the third day.

Eli woke at dawn — or rather, he became aware of dawn, since he wasn't sure he'd actually slept. The kids had bedded down near the edge of the clearing, in a spot between stacked timber where the sawdust was thick and soft. Nobody had questioned their presence. Whatever the Pathfinder did to stitch them into the fabric of the past, it worked as well here as it had in the Valley of Elah.

Elena was sitting up, staring toward the tree line. "Eli. Look."

He looked.

A pair of deer stood at the edge of the forest. Not grazing. Not startled. Just standing, side by side, facing the Ark with the calm patience of passengers waiting for a bus. Behind them, farther back in the shadows of the trees, shapes moved. Large and small, dark and light, an impossible gathering of creatures that had no business being in the same forest, let alone walking in the same direction.

"That's not normal," Jordan said. He was awake too, sitting with his arms around his knees, watching. "Animals don't do that. Predators and prey don't just... walk together."

They came all morning. Pairs of animals emerged from every direction — from the forest, from the hills, from the settlement itself, where dogs left their masters' doorsteps and cats climbed down from warm rooftops and walked, calm and deliberate, toward the clearing. Birds landed on the Ark's unfinished scaffolding in pairs, folding their wings and settling like they planned to stay.

The settlement went quiet.

The laughter stopped. The jokes stopped. The casual dismissal that had been the mockers' favorite weapon dried up and blew away like sawdust. People came out of their houses and stood in the road and watched animals they'd never seen before walk past their gardens and through their streets, heading toward the old man's ridiculous boat.

Nobody was laughing now.

Noah's family moved with sudden, focused energy. The Ark, Eli realized, was finished — or finished enough. The hull was sealed, the decks laid, the compartments built. Noah's sons opened the great door, a ramp of heavy timber that lowered from the side of the hull with a groan that echoed across the clearing.

The animals walked up the ramp.

No pushing. No panic. No herding. They simply walked — the deer first, then goats, then creatures Eli couldn't name, species he'd never seen in any book, each pair moving in sequence as if they'd been given numbers and were filing in to find their seats.

"This is impossible," Jordan whispered.

"Yeah," Elena said. She had tears on her cheeks, though Eli didn't think she knew it. "It is."

Noah stood at the base of the ramp, watching. His wife stood beside him, and his sons and their wives gathered close. The old man's face was unreadable — not triumphant, not vindicated, not even relieved. He looked like a man standing at the edge of the world, watching it end and begin at the same time.

The last animal entered. A pair of doves, white against the grey timber, fluttering up the ramp and disappearing into the dark interior.

"Inside," Eli said suddenly. "We need to be inside."

"What?" Jordan said. "Inside the boat? The giant boat that's about to be in a flood? You want us to get *on* the boat?"

"The relic is the olive branch," Eli said. "The dove brings it back after the flood. We have to be here for that. We have to be inside."

Elena was already moving toward the ramp. "Come on. Let's go."

"I didn't agree to this," Jordan said. But he was following, because the alternative was standing alone in a settlement full of people who were starting to look at the sky with expressions that had nothing to do with sunshine.

They climbed the ramp. The interior of the Ark was dark and warm and smelled of hay and animals and fresh-cut wood. Stalls lined both sides, two and three levels deep, already filling with the quiet sounds of creatures settling in — rustling, breathing, the click of hooves on timber.

Noah's family entered behind them. And then — Eli didn't see how — the door closed.

Not slammed. Not pushed. It rose, the heavy ramp lifting from the ground and sealing against the hull with a deep, final thud that Eli felt in his bones. No one had touched it. Noah's sons were all inside. Noah himself stood ten feet from the door, his hands at his sides.

God closed the door.

The silence that followed was the loudest thing Eli had ever heard. No birdsong from outside. No voices from the settlement. No wind. Just the creak of timber and the breathing of animals and the heartbeat in Eli's ears.

Jordan stood very still in the half-dark, his face pale, his hands clenched at his sides. "We're locked in," he said. "We're locked in a boat with a man who says God told him the world is about to end."

"Jordan —"

"And I can't leave. The door is shut and I can't leave and I don't even know if I believe —"

The first drop of rain hit the hull like a fist.

## Chapter 5: The Rain

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 3, 2019 — Home office, late*
*I had a nightmare about water — not the ocean, but water with no end and no horizon. I lay in the dark thinking about what it must have been like inside the Ark — not the adventure, but the not-knowing. Trapped in a floating box with no idea how long it would last or what would be left when it ended. That's not adventure. That's endurance.*

---

The rain didn't fall. It *attacked*.

Within minutes of the first drop, the sky opened — Eli couldn't see it, but he could hear it, a hammering on the hull so loud that the timbers shook. Not the steady drum of a rainstorm. Something deeper. Something that sounded like the world was being torn apart and the water was pouring through the cracks.

Elena pressed her hands over her ears. "It's so LOUD."

It was. The Ark groaned and creaked around them, the timbers flexing under the assault of water like a living thing trying to hold itself together. Animals stirred in their stalls — a low of cattle, the nervous bleating of goats, the flutter of wings in the upper levels. But even they seemed muted, overwhelmed by the sheer volume of the rain.

Then the Ark moved.

Not rocking — *lifting*. Eli felt his stomach drop as the hull shifted beneath his feet. The timbers screamed. For one terrible moment, everything tilted sideways, and Elena grabbed a support beam with both hands, and Jordan fell against a stall partition, and Eli's knees buckled as the floor swung under him like something alive.

Then it leveled. Slowly, with a long, shuddering groan, the Ark found its balance. The floor still moved — a slow, deep roll, like breathing — but the terrible tilt was gone. They were floating.

"We're on the water," Eli said. His voice sounded small.

"I noticed," Jordan said from the floor. His face was grey.

The darkness was nearly total. A few oil lamps hung from hooks along the central corridor, swaying with the ship's roll, casting shadows that moved like living things. The air was thick — animals, hay, timber, pitch, and underneath it all, the wet mineral smell of rain finding its way through every crack and seam.

Noah moved through the Ark like a man walking through a dream. Eli watched him check the stalls, adjust ropes, calm a horse with a low word and a steady hand on its neck. His sons worked beside him, methodical and silent. Whatever they were feeling, they'd locked it away behind the work.

But Noah's wife sat in the family's quarters near the stern, her hands in her lap, her eyes fixed on nothing. She wasn't crying. She was listening. To the rain. To the water that was climbing the hull, inch by inch, foot by foot, swallowing the world she'd known.

The wrongness pressed in.

It came differently this time. Not as cold or blurred vision but as a creeping doubt — a voice in the back of Eli's mind that said *this boat is too small, the water is too deep, no one is coming.* Elena went quiet, which was worse than shivering. Jordan's hands clenched and unclenched at his sides, as if he was trying to hold onto something that kept slipping away.

The lamps dimmed. Not all at once — slowly, as if something was leaning on the flames, pressing them lower. Shadows deepened. The creaking of the hull grew louder, more insistent, each groan sounding less like the flexing of strong timber and more like the complaint of something about to break.

In the corridor ahead of them, Noah stopped walking.

He stood with one hand on a support beam, his head bowed, his shoulders hunched. In the swaying lamplight, the old man looked every year of his age and then some. His lips moved. Not prayer — not yet. Something rawer. A question.

"Did I hear correctly?"

Eli barely caught the words. Noah whispered them to the beam, to the darkness, to the water that hammered the hull like it wanted in.

"Did I hear correctly? Was it You? Was it really You?"

The lamps flickered. The shadows surged. Elena made a small sound and pressed closer to Eli. The darkness was thickening at the edges of the corridor — not fog, not smoke, but an absence. A place where light should be and wasn't. It crept along the walls and pooled in the corners and made the Ark feel smaller, tighter, as if the whole massive vessel was contracting around them.

Noah lifted his head. His eyes were wet. His hands trembled against the beam.

Then he closed his eyes. Took a single, shuddering breath. And when he opened them again, the trembling had stopped.

"I heard correctly," he said. Quiet. Firm. Not a question anymore.

The lamps steadied. Not brighter — just steadier. The shadows stopped their creeping. And the hum in Eli's chest loosened, just slightly, from a knot into a thread.

Outside, the rain kept falling. And falling. And falling.

## Chapter 6: Forty Days

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 8, 2019 — Home office*
*The rain lasted forty days and nights, but the Ark floated for nearly a YEAR. Imagine being stuck on a boat for a year with no way of knowing when it ends. The boredom would be worse than the danger. That kind of patience isn't waiting for a bus. It's planting a tree you might never sit under.*

---

Days blurred into each other in the belly of the Ark. The rain stopped eventually — one morning Eli woke and realized the hammering was gone. The silence was almost worse. At least the rain had been something to count.

The kids found their routine. Elena threw herself into work — feeding animals, hauling water, mucking stalls. She discovered she had a gift for calming the larger animals. A nervous horse that wouldn't let anyone near it would settle under her hand, and Japheth's wife started coming to find her whenever an animal was spooked. Elena needed to be needed. Sitting still in the dark made her twitchy and sharp-tongued.

Jordan helped with the animals too — he turned out to be surprisingly good with them, his careful movements and quiet voice calming the nervous ones faster than anyone else. A pair of small brown goats took to following him through the corridors, their hooves clicking on the timber behind him like a tiny escort.

"They're doing it again," Jordan said one morning, trying to shoo them away. One of the goats butted its head against his knee. The other one chewed on the hem of his tunic.

"They like you," Elena said, grinning.

"They like *anyone* who gives them food. It's not personal."

"You named them."

"I did *not* name them."

"I heard you call the little one Patches."

Jordan's face went red. "That was a description, not a name. It has patches. I was being observational." The goat bleated and pressed closer to his leg. Jordan sighed and scratched behind its ears. "Fine. But the other one's name is Not My Problem."

But when the work was done, Jordan retreated to a spot near the hull where a crack between two planks let in a thin line of grey light. He sat there for hours, staring at that sliver of the outside world, his face unreadable.

It was on one of those quiet days — the fourteenth or the fortieth, Eli had lost count — that Jordan and Noah ended up alone together.

Eli hadn't planned it. He'd gone to help Elena with the goats, and when he came back, Jordan was sitting in his usual spot by the hull, and Noah was nearby, mending a rope. They weren't talking. They were just existing in the same space, the way two people do when neither feels the need to fill silence.

Eli stopped around the corner, not wanting to interrupt. Elena bumped into his back.

"What —"

"Shh." He held up a hand.

Noah's fingers worked the rope. Jordan stared at his crack of light. A goat bleated somewhere in the lower level.

"You heard a voice," Jordan said. Not a question.

Noah glanced at him. Then back at the rope. "Yes."

"And you just... believed it. You heard a voice tell you the whole world was going to flood, and you started building a boat."

"I did."

"Weren't you scared?"

Noah's hands stopped. He looked at Jordan — really looked, with the full attention of a man who had spent years being dismissed and had learned to recognize when someone was asking a real question instead of setting up a joke.

"Every day," Noah said.

Jordan blinked. "What?"

"I was afraid every day. Before the rain. During the rain. Right now, sitting here." Noah held up the rope. His hands were steady, but his voice was not. "My wife thinks I'm brave. My sons think I'm certain. I am neither. I am a man who heard something and decided to act on it. That's all."

"But if you're afraid, how do you keep going?"

Noah tied off the rope. Set it down. Rested his hands on his knees. "Because the voice was real. I know it was real. Not because I can prove it. Not because the rain confirmed it — I believed before the rain. I believed when the sky was blue and the ground was dry and everyone I knew said I was a fool." He paused. "Fear and faith are not opposites, boy. They sit in the same room. I am afraid, and I believe. Both at the same time."

Jordan said nothing. He turned back to his crack of light. But his hands, which had been fidgeting with the rubber band that wasn't there anymore, were still.

Elena tugged Eli's sleeve. He let her pull him back down the corridor, away from the conversation.

"Did you hear that?" she whispered.

"Yeah."

"He's afraid. Noah's afraid."

"Yeah."

"That doesn't make me feel better."

Eli leaned against the hull and felt the Ark roll beneath him — slow, patient, carrying its impossible cargo across a world made entirely of water. "I think it's supposed to," he said.

## Chapter 7: The Dove

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 14, 2019 — Home office*
*Everyone remembers the dove with the olive branch. But look at the order: first the raven, which never came back. Then the dove, which came back with nothing. Then the dove again — and this time, the branch. The first try failed. The second try failed. The third try worked. How many of God's promises work like that? Not the first time, not the second, but always eventually.*

---

The morning Noah opened the window, light fell into the Ark like something that had been locked out and was desperate to return.

Eli squinted against it. Real light — not the thin grey that leaked through the hull's seams, but actual, golden sunlight, warm on his face. He hadn't realized how much he'd missed it until it was there. Elena turned toward it like a flower, her eyes closed, a smile breaking across her face.

Even the animals felt it. A low murmur ran through the stalls — not agitation, but something gentler. Anticipation.

Noah stood at the window — a square opening near the top of the hull, sealed until now with pitched timber that his sons had pried loose. The old man leaned out and breathed. Eli watched his shoulders drop, his chest expand, his whole body soften as fresh air hit his lungs for what must have been the first time in months.

Then Noah reached into a cage near the window and pulled out a raven. The bird was large and glossy-black, its eyes bright and restless. Noah held it for a moment, then opened his hands.

The raven flew. It shot through the window and into the sky and didn't look back. It circled the Ark once — a dark shape against the blinding blue — and then it was gone. Flying, flying, never returning.

They waited.

"It's not coming back," Jordan said after an hour.

"No," Eli said. "It won't. The raven can eat dead things floating on the water. It doesn't need dry land."

Noah seemed to know this too. He didn't watch the sky for the raven. Instead, he reached into a smaller cage and gently lifted out a dove.

She was white. Pure white, small enough to cup in two hands, with dark eyes and a rapid heartbeat that Eli could see fluttering in her breast. Noah held her close to his chest and whispered something — words Eli couldn't hear, private words, the kind a man says to a small, fragile thing he's about to send into the unknown.

Then he let her go.

The dove flew. Not like the raven — not in a straight, confident line, but in wide, searching circles, rising and falling, scanning the water below for something solid to land on. She flew until she was a white dot against the blue. Then she turned and came back.

She landed on Noah's outstretched hand. Empty. No branch. No leaf. Nothing.

Noah's face didn't change. He brought the dove back inside, set her gently in her cage, and closed the window.

"Seven days," he said to no one in particular. "We wait seven days."

They waited. Seven days in the belly of the Ark, the light from the sealed window reduced again to thin cracks, the routine of feeding and cleaning and enduring resuming its patient rhythm. Elena paced. Jordan sat by his crack in the hull. Eli helped Noah's sons and tried not to count the hours.

On the seventh day, Noah opened the window again.

The dove was calm this time. She sat in Noah's hands without fluttering, her small head turning, her dark eyes bright. Noah lifted her to the window. She spread her wings and flew.

The wait was different this time. Eli couldn't explain how, but the air in the Ark felt tighter, more charged, like the moment before a held breath breaks. Elena stood beside him, her hands clenched, her eyes on the window. Jordan was on the other side, arms crossed, but his jaw was tight and his eyes were locked on the square of blue sky.

Hours passed. The sun moved. The light from the window shifted from gold to amber to the deep orange of late afternoon.

"There," Elena said. She pointed.

A white shape against the darkening sky. Small, growing larger, wings beating in a steady rhythm. The dove, returning.

She landed on the window ledge. And in her beak, she carried a branch.

It was small — six inches, maybe less, with a few oval leaves still attached, green and fresh and impossibly alive. An olive branch, torn from a tree that had survived the flood, carried across miles of water by a bird the size of a child's fist.

And it glowed.

Faint — barely visible in the amber light of the setting sun, like the memory of a candle flame. But the glow was there, a soft greenish-gold that pulsed along the branch's length, strongest at the leaves, as if life itself was shining through them.

The hum in Eli's chest surged — not warning, not sour. Pure. A clear note that rose and filled his body and made his eyes sting.

Noah took the branch from the dove's beak. He held it in both hands and stared at it, and tears ran down his weathered face into his grey beard.

"He remembered," Noah whispered. "He remembered us."

Noah held the branch, and tears ran into his beard, and Eli understood. Not because someone explained it. Because he was there. The branch meant God remembered them — even in the dark, even in the silence, even when it felt like the water would never end.

"The relic," Jordan said. His voice was hoarse. "It's the branch."

"It won't come to us yet," Eli said. He was sure of this, though he didn't know how. "Not until Noah is done with it."

Noah held the branch for a long time. Then, slowly, he set it down on the ledge beside the dove's cage. He pressed his hands together, bowed his head, and stood in the golden window-light with his lips moving in silent prayer, a man who had spent a year on the water because he believed something he couldn't see.

The branch glowed brighter. The green-gold light spread along the leaves, filling the grain of the wood, until the whole thing shone like a small, living lantern.

Eli reached for it. The moment his fingers touched the branch, the glow flared — warm, fierce, filling the window, filling the Ark, filling his chest with a note so clear and high it was almost singing.

Elena's hand found his arm. Jordan's shoulder pressed against his. The branch dissolved in Eli's grip — not crumbling, not breaking, but melting into light, its weight and shape and texture becoming warmth and then becoming nothing, until Eli was holding empty air and the brightness swallowed everything.

## Chapter 8: The Rainbow

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 15, 2019 — Home office, sunrise*
*I've been thinking about the rainbow. The Hebrew word for it also means a war bow — like God is hanging His weapon in the sky, putting it down after a battle. And the promise isn't "you will remember." It's "I will remember." God is the one making the promise. God is the one who won't forget. I find that both beautiful and terrifying.*

---

Old books and cinnamon.

Eli's knees buckled as solid ground replaced the Ark's rolling floor. He caught himself on the edge of the desk, blinking against the familiar dimness of Abuelo's study. Late afternoon light slanted through the window at the exact angle it had when they'd left. Same dust motes. Same creak of the old house settling. Same moment.

His right hand was empty. The ghost of the branch lingered — the smooth bark, the waxy leaves, the living warmth — but there was nothing between his fingers except fading light.

Elena sat down hard on the floor. Her hair was dry. Her tunic was gone — she was back in her red t-shirt and leggings, a bandage on her left elbow from a bike fall last week. She looked at her hands, turned them over, then looked at Eli.

"A year," she said. "We were there for a year."

"It felt like it," Jordan said. He was leaning against the doorframe, glasses back on his face, hoodie pulled tight around his shoulders. He looked wrung out, like someone had taken everything inside him and squeezed. But his eyes were clear.

The Pathfinder sat on the desk.

Eli straightened and looked at it. The first symbol still glowed — the stone, amber and warm, its single note humming beneath the bronze. But beside it, the second symbol had changed.

The rough scratch that had been a suggestion of a branch was gone. In its place, sharp lines traced every detail — the curve of the stem, the spread of the leaves, the tiny veins running through each one. The engraving was polished smooth, filled with color like enamel inlay, and the color was green. Not painted green. *Living* green — the deep, bright green of a leaf held up to sunlight, impossible and beautiful against the old bronze.

A faint glow pulsed from within the symbol. Green-gold. Steady. Alive.

And the hum had changed. It wasn't one note anymore. It was two — the first, the amber note from the stone, and now a second, higher, sweeter, blending with the first into something that was almost harmony. Almost music. Not a song yet. The beginning of one.

"Two," Elena whispered. She traced the air above the symbols with her finger, not quite touching. "Two down. Ten to go."

Jordan hadn't moved from the doorframe. He was staring at the Pathfinder, and for once, his arms weren't crossed.

"He waited a year on that boat," Jordan said quietly. "A whole year. No signal. No proof. No guarantee it would ever end."

"And he was scared the whole time," Elena added. "That part got me. He wasn't, like, calmly sitting there knowing everything would be fine. He was terrified."

"But he kept going," Eli said.

"He kept building stalls and feeding goats and checking the hull," Jordan said. "For a *year*." He paused. Pushed his glasses up. "I'm not saying I believe what he believed. I'm saying — I don't think he was crazy. Also, I miss Patches."

Elena snorted. "You are *not* allowed to be sad about leaving imaginary ancient goats."

"They were real goats. They were historically accurate goats. And Patches had great personal qualities."

"He wasn't crazy," Elena said.

"No. He wasn't."

Eli let the silence hold for a moment. Then he turned to the desk and picked up the journal he'd set down three days and a lifetime ago — Abuelo's leather-bound notebook, still open to the sketch of the olive branch. He flipped the page.

The next entry was in Abuelo's tight, careful handwriting, the ink a different color — blue instead of black, written on a different day, maybe weeks later. At the top of the page was a sketch, quick and confident: a coat. Not a plain coat. A coat covered in color — stripes and swirls of red and gold and blue, drawn with the exuberance of someone who loved what they were seeing. It looked like a painting you could wear.

Below the sketch, Abuelo had written: *Genesis 37. Joseph, son of Jacob. Seventeen years old. His father made him a coat of many colors, and his brothers hated him for it. Sold into slavery by his own blood. But God was with him — in the pit, in the prison, in the palace. The thread holds.* And circled twice, in the same faded ink: *A coat. A pit. A boy betrayed. The promise bends but does not break.*

Eli closed the journal and looked at the Pathfinder. The two notes hummed together — amber and green, stone and branch, courage and promise. Ten faint symbols waited in their circle, rough and patient, like seeds that hadn't yet found their season.

Outside, the evening was settling over Abuelo's house. Crickets sang in the overgrown garden. The wind chimes made of old keys clinked softly from the eaves. Somewhere down the street, a car door closed and a dog barked and the world went on being ordinary.

But inside the hidden study, in the last light of a summer day, a bronze disc hummed its two quiet notes — patient as a prayer, steady as a promise kept.
