## Chapter 1: The Phone Call

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 7, 2017 — Sinai Peninsula, Day 3*
*I've walked the base of this mountain three times, looking for anything — a scorch mark, a split in the rock. I keep thinking about the man who stood here barefoot, arguing with God. Not a warrior. Not a king. A runaway shepherd with a stutter. God could have chosen anyone. He chose the man who said "not me." I find that both encouraging and unsettling.*

---

The phone rang at 6:47 in the morning.

Eli was already awake. He'd been lying in bed in Abuelo's guest room, staring at the ceiling, thinking about a coat dipped in goat's blood and a man who chose forgiveness when revenge would have been easier. Three adventures now. Three relics dissolved in his hands. Three symbols glowing on the Pathfinder like small fires that refused to go out.

The phone rang again. The house line — the old rotary phone in the hallway that Abuelo kept because he said digital phones had no soul.

Elena's door banged open. Footsteps on the stairs. She was faster, always faster.

"Hello?" Her voice carried up the stairwell. Then silence. Then, smaller: "Abuelo?"

Eli was out of bed before his brain caught up. He took the stairs two at a time, bare feet slapping hardwood, and found Elena standing in the hallway with the heavy receiver pressed against her ear. Her eyes were wide. Not scared. Something else — the look of someone hearing a voice they'd started to worry they might forget.

"He wants to talk to you," she whispered, holding out the phone.

Eli took it. The receiver was warm from Elena's hand. He pressed it to his ear and heard breathing — slow, steady, a little ragged at the edges.

"Mijo." Abuelo's voice was thin. Like a radio signal from far away, fading in and out. But it was him. The warmth underneath the weakness was unmistakable.

"Abuelo. Are you okay? How are you —"

"Listen to me." A pause. The sound of effort, of a man spending energy he didn't have much of. "You've been in my study."

It wasn't a question. Eli's throat tightened.

"The staff was never magic, mijo." Another pause, longer. Eli could hear machines beeping in the background. "It was just a stick. Remember that."

"What staff? Abuelo, what do you —"

"Just a stick. Until he trusted." A breath that sounded like it cost something. "Te quiero. Both of you. I'm proud."

The line went quiet. Not dead — Eli could still hear the hospital sounds, the beeping, the hiss of something mechanical. But Abuelo was done. He'd spent what he had.

Eli hung up. His hand was shaking.

Elena stood three feet away, arms crossed over her pajama shirt, watching him with those sharp brown eyes. "What did he say?"

"He said the staff was never magic. It was just a stick."

"What staff?"

"I don't know."

Jordan's knock came twenty minutes later. He let himself in the way he did now — one knock, then the door, because somewhere during the last three adventures they'd stopped being neighbors and started being something else.

"You guys look weird," Jordan said. He was wearing his charcoal hoodie despite the July heat. His glasses caught the morning light. "Weirder than normal."

"Abuelo called." Elena said it like a headline. Like breaking news.

Jordan's eyebrows went up. "From the hospital? He's talking?"

"Barely," Eli said. He was sitting at the kitchen table, turning a spoon in a bowl of cereal he hadn't eaten. "He said something about a staff. That it was just a stick."

"That's... cryptic."

"That's Abuelo."

They went upstairs. The hidden study smelled the same as always — cedar and dust and the weight of decades of searching. The Pathfinder sat on its leather cloth, three symbols sharp and glowing in the morning light. A stone. A branch. A thread. Each one polished smooth, filled with colored enamel, pulsing with a faint light that was visible even in daylight now. Three notes hummed together — not quite a chord, not quite a melody. Something in between. Something building.

Eli picked up the Pathfinder. The warmth flooded his hands immediately, stronger than the first time, more sure of itself. The hum rose from his chest into his arms, and the fourth symbol — a rough scratch that might have been a staff or a rod, barely visible in the bronze — began to vibrate.

"Here we go," Elena said. She grabbed Eli's arm with one hand and Jordan's sleeve with the other.

Jordan sighed. "I really should stop coming over in the morning."

The light swallowed the room.

## Chapter 2: The Bush That Burned

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 9, 2017 — Sinai Peninsula, Day 5*
*I watched a shepherd today carrying a staff — just olive wood, worn smooth by years of use. When God asked Moses what was in his hand, Moses just said, "A staff." The most ordinary answer in the world. God said throw it down. And the ordinary became something else entirely. I wonder how often we hold the answer in our hands and don't recognize it because it looks too plain.*

---

Heat. Sand. Silence so deep it had texture.

Eli opened his eyes and squinted against light so bright it bleached the world white. They were standing on a rocky slope dotted with scrub brush and thin grass that had given up trying to be green. Mountains rose around them — not the sharp peaks of postcards but worn, rounded shapes, ancient beyond measuring, the color of rust and bone.

The air smelled like dust and something faintly sweet. Wild thyme, maybe. And goats. Definitely goats.

"Where are we?" Jordan was already scanning the horizon, one hand shading his eyes, the other fidgeting with the rubber band on his wrist.

"Sinai," Eli said quietly. The word felt heavy in his mouth. "The wilderness of Sinai."

Elena crouched and picked up a handful of reddish sand, letting it sift between her fingers. "There's nothing here. Just rocks and —"

She stopped.

Below them, on a flat stretch of ground between two ridges, a man sat on a rock. He was watching a scattered flock of sheep and goats pick their way across the slope. He held a long staff across his knees. He was alone.

He looked old. Not ancient — strong in the shoulders, dark-skinned from years of sun — but worn in a way that had nothing to do with age. His face was the face of someone who had been carrying something heavy for a very long time. His eyes moved across the flock with a patience that looked like it had been learned through exhaustion, not choice.

"Is that —" Elena started.

"Moses," Eli said. His voice dropped without meaning to. "Before everything. Before Egypt. He's a shepherd here. He ran away."

"Ran away from what?"

"He killed a man. An Egyptian who was beating a Hebrew slave. He ran into the desert and never went back."

Jordan pushed his glasses up. "So the guy God picked to free an entire nation of slaves... was a fugitive hiding in the desert."

"Yeah."

"Interesting hiring criteria."

They watched Moses for a while. He moved through his flock with a quiet competence, tapping a goat back into line with his staff, checking the hoof of a limping ewe. Nothing dramatic. Nothing special. Just a man doing a small job in a vast, empty place.

Then Moses looked up.

Eli followed his gaze. On the slope above them, tucked between two large boulders, a bush was on fire.

Not burning. On fire. There's a difference. Burning means smoke and crackling and branches curling to ash. This bush was wrapped in flame — orange and gold and white at the center — and the leaves weren't blackening. The branches weren't breaking. The fire poured over and through the bush like water over stones, and the bush simply... held.

Moses set his staff against the rock and stood. Eli watched the shepherd's face cycle through confusion, then curiosity, then something deeper. Moses started walking toward the bush. Not running. Walking, the way a man walks toward something he knows will change everything if he gets close enough.

"I need to see this," Moses said, half to himself. "Why doesn't it burn up?"

He climbed the slope. The kids followed, staying behind the boulders, close enough to hear. The heat from the bush was real — Eli felt it on his face, dry and pressing — but it wasn't the heat of destruction. It was the heat of presence.

A voice came from the fire. Not from the sky. Not from the ground. From the bush itself, and also from everywhere, and also from the silent center of Eli's chest where the hum usually lived. The voice was not loud. It didn't need to be.

"Moses."

Moses stopped walking. His sandals scraped on rock.

"Moses."

"Here I am," Moses said. His voice cracked on the second word.

"Take off your sandals. The ground where you're standing is holy."

Moses knelt and pulled off his sandals with hands that were shaking. Eli could see the tremor from fifteen feet away. This was not a man performing a ritual. This was a man who understood, suddenly and completely, who he was talking to.

The voice continued. It spoke of suffering — the suffering of an entire people, enslaved for generations. It spoke of crying out, of being heard. And then it said the thing that changed Moses' face from awe to something closer to terror.

"I am sending you to Pharaoh. Bring my people out of Egypt."

The silence that followed was enormous.

Moses stood there, barefoot on holy ground, with fire reflecting in his eyes, and Eli watched him shrink. Not physically. Something inside Moses pulled back, contracted, like a hand withdrawing from a flame.

"Who am I," Moses said, "to go to Pharaoh? Who am I to bring the Israelites out of Egypt?"

Eli's breath caught. He knew that voice. Not the sound of it — the shape of it. The way the words curled inward, making themselves small. He'd heard it in his own head a hundred times. In class, when he knew the answer but couldn't make himself raise his hand. In the hallway, when someone needed help and his feet wouldn't move. *Who am I to do this?*

Moses wasn't being humble. He was being honest. And the honesty was terrifying, because Eli recognized it.

God answered. He gave Moses signs — the staff that became a snake, the hand that turned leprous and healed. But Moses kept objecting. His voice got quieter with each one, not louder. Eli knew that pattern too. The quieter Moses got, the more certain he was that he couldn't do this.

"I'm not a good speaker," Moses said. Almost whispering now. "I never have been. I stumble over words. Please. Send someone else."

*Send someone else.* Eli felt those words land in his stomach like stones.

Elena looked at him. She didn't say anything. But her hand found his arm, and she squeezed once — the way she did when she could tell he was somewhere inside his own head that she couldn't reach.

The voice from the bush did not give up on Moses. It didn't choose someone else. It gave him his brother Aaron to speak for him. It gave him the staff in his hand. And then it said: "Go."

Moses picked up his staff. Just a stick. Olive wood, worn smooth. Nothing special about it at all.

He started walking toward Egypt.

## Chapter 3: The Weight of Empire

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*February 22, 2018 — Cairo, Egypt*
*You cannot understand the Exodus until you've stood in the ruins of Egypt. The columns are sixty feet tall. The stones weigh tons, and there are millions of them. This wasn't a kingdom. It was a machine — designed to make you feel small, to make fighting back feel impossible. That's what the Hebrews were up against.*

---

Egypt hit them like a wall.

The Pathfinder had jumped them forward — past the journey, past the reunion with Aaron — straight into the heart of the thing Moses was most afraid of. The city was enormous. Mud-brick buildings packed tight along roads that teemed with people, and above it all, a temple complex rose against the sky — columns taller than any tree Eli had ever seen, painted in reds and golds that blazed in the sun.

But what made Eli's stomach drop wasn't the size of the buildings. It was the people on the edges.

They were everywhere, once you noticed. Men and women with dust-caked skin and sunken eyes, hauling bricks on wooden frames strapped to their backs. Children — children Eli's age, Elena's age — carrying water jars that bent them double. A line of workers stretched along a half-built wall, lifting mud-bricks into place under the watch of overseers who carried short whips coiled at their belts.

The workers didn't look up. They didn't talk. They moved in a rhythm that was mechanical and joyless, like parts of a machine that had forgotten they were human.

"Those are the Hebrews," Eli said. His voice came out flat. He didn't recognize it.

"Slaves," Jordan said quietly. He pushed his glasses up, but his hand lingered at his face, and Eli realized Jordan was pressing his palm against his cheek like he was trying to feel something solid.

Elena's fists were clenched. Her jaw was tight. "How long? How long have they been like this?"

"Generations," Eli said. "Hundreds of years."

She turned away. When she turned back, her eyes were wet and furious. "Hundreds of *years*?"

A shadow passed over them that had nothing to do with clouds. Eli felt it first — the hum in his chest dropping low, becoming thick and sluggish, like honey poured into his lungs. The air changed. Not cooler, not warmer. Heavier. The colors around them dimmed half a shade, as if someone had turned down the brightness on the world.

Elena hugged herself. "I'm cold again."

Jordan blinked rapidly and rubbed his eyes behind his glasses. "Everything just went fuzzy. Is it — is that the thing? The grey thing?"

They didn't have a name for it. Three adventures, and they still just called it "the thing" or "the fog" or "the wrongness." But Eli knew what it felt like by now. It was here, woven into the fabric of Egypt like thread through cloth. Not creeping in from the edges the way it had in the Valley of Elah or on the Ark. Here, it was already settled. Comfortable. At home.

Because here, the Fade didn't need to create despair. Egypt had done that work for generations. The Fade just had to sit back and let the weight of the empire do its job.

They found Moses in the Hebrew quarter — a cramped, stinking section of the city where the buildings leaned against each other like exhausted men. Moses and Aaron stood before a group of Hebrew elders in a dim courtyard. Aaron was speaking. Moses stood behind him, gripping his staff with both hands, knuckles pale.

"God has seen your suffering," Aaron said. His voice was strong, practiced. A speaker's voice. "He has sent us to bring you out."

The elders listened. Some leaned forward. Others sat with their arms crossed, faces carved from stone. One old woman in the back shook her head slowly, not in disagreement but in the tired way of someone who had stopped believing that good news could be real.

"Out," one of the elders said. "Out where? There is no 'out.' There is Egypt. There has always been Egypt."

Eli watched Moses' face. The shepherd heard those words and flinched. Not from the skepticism. From the recognition. Because Moses had said the same thing at the burning bush. *Who am I? I can't do this. Send someone else.*

And now here he was, standing in the place he'd run from, holding a stick, trying to convince people who'd been broken for centuries that freedom was possible.

The air grew heavier. The hum in Eli's chest thickened. But it wasn't cold or fog this time. It was suffocating *inevitability* — the crushing feeling that nothing could ever change, that Egypt was too big and too old and too permanent to break. The old woman's head-shake spread through the group like a disease. Shoulders dropped. Eyes dulled. One by one, the elders' faces closed like doors shutting. Not because they were angry at Moses. Because they had stopped believing that hope was something real people could have.

"They don't believe him," Elena whispered. The fury was still in her voice, but it was smaller now, pressed flat by something too big to punch.

"Would you?" Jordan said. He wasn't being sarcastic. His voice was careful, almost gentle. "If you'd been a slave your whole life, and your parents were slaves, and their parents were slaves — would you believe some guy who showed up with a stick and said God sent him?"

Eli didn't answer. He was watching Moses. The shepherd's grip on his staff had gone white. His shoulders were hunched. He was getting quieter. Pulling inward. Becoming smaller in the shadow of the empire.

And Eli thought: *That's me.* The kid who knows the answer but can't raise his hand.

Moses looked up. For one second — so brief Eli almost missed it — the shepherd's eyes swept across the courtyard and landed on the three kids standing in the shadow of a crumbling wall. Moses' gaze passed over them the way it had passed over difficult terrain on the mountain slopes. Measuring. Uncertain.

Then Aaron touched his arm, and Moses turned back to the elders, and the moment was gone.

But Eli's heart was hammering. Because in that one second, he'd seen something in Moses' eyes that he hadn't expected.

Not courage. Not faith. Not the fire from the bush.

Fear. Plain, ordinary, human fear.

And Moses was going to do it anyway.

## Chapter 4: Plague and Stone

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*February 25, 2018 — Cairo, Egypt*
*The plagues weren't just punishments. Each one targeted something Egypt worshipped — the river, the sun, the harvest. One by one, the things they trusted were shown to be powerless. Every plague was an invitation to see the truth. Pharaoh refused. Ten times, the door opened. Ten times, he closed it.*

---

The plagues came like a storm breaking in slow motion.

The Pathfinder moved them through time again — not one clean jump but a series of lurches, like a boat carried by waves that grew taller with each swell. Days compressed into hours. Eli felt the shifts like the turning of pages in a book read too fast.

The river turned to blood first.

They stood on the bank of the Nile and watched Moses lift his staff over the water. Aaron stretched out his hand. And the river — wide, brown, the lifeblood of an empire — turned dark red from bank to bank. Not gradually. All at once, like a wound opening. The fish died within the hour. The smell reached them before the sight did — thick, copper-sweet, unbearable.

Egyptians gathered at the riverbank, staring. Some wept. Some raged. The Hebrew workers looked up from their bricks for the first time in Eli's memory, something flickering behind their exhausted eyes.

But Pharaoh's magicians did the same trick. Not as well, not as completely — but enough. Enough for Pharaoh to shrug and turn away.

"He doesn't care," Elena said. "His own river is poison and he doesn't *care*."

Then came the frogs. They erupted from the dead river in a living carpet — in the streets, in the houses, in the bread ovens. Eli watched a palace guard shake three frogs out of his helmet with a look of dignified disgust.

"Okay," Jordan said, watching a frog hop off a dignitary's lap. "This one's almost funny."

"Don't laugh," Elena said, but her mouth was twitching.

"I'm not laughing. The frog on that guy's head is laughing. I'm just observing."

Pharaoh called for Moses. "Remove the frogs. I'll let your people go." Moses prayed. The frogs died. Pharaoh changed his mind.

"Of course he did," Jordan muttered. "You know what this reminds me of? My dad promising to show up for my sister's recital. Three times. Every time, something came up."

Elena looked at him. "That's not the same thing."

"No. But the feeling is. When someone breaks a promise enough times, you stop hearing the promise. You just hear the breaking."

The plagues kept coming. Gnats that choked the air. Flies that blackened the sky over Egyptian neighborhoods but didn't cross into the Hebrew quarter. Livestock that dropped dead by noon. Boils. Hail. Locusts. Each time, the same pattern: plague, promise, relief, betrayal. Over and over, like a door opening and closing.

Elena stopped watching after the seventh one. "He's never going to let them go. Is he?"

"He does," Eli said. "Eventually."

"When? After how many more?"

The weight of the Fade had burrowed into Egypt like roots into soil. It lived in the feeling that this would never end — that the system was too big, too old, too powerful to break. That Moses was a fool holding a stick in front of a machine that had been grinding people to dust for four hundred years.

"Tell me they get out," Elena said.

"They get out," Eli said. But his voice was small.

Then darkness fell. Three days of it — not night but true darkness, the kind you could feel on your skin, heavy and thick as wet cloth. The Egyptians couldn't see their own hands. The Hebrews had light in their homes, but it was thin and uncertain, like candles in a hurricane.

In that darkness, Eli heard Moses speaking to Aaron. The two brothers sat in a Hebrew house, a single oil lamp between them. Moses' staff lay across his knees. His hands were still.

"He won't let them go," Moses said. Not angry. Exhausted. "Nine times. Nine."

Aaron said nothing. What was there to say?

Moses looked down at the staff in his hands. Just a stick. Olive wood, worn smooth. And Eli remembered Abuelo's voice on the phone, thin and rasping: *The staff was never magic, mijo. It was just a stick.*

Moses closed his eyes. His lips moved without sound. And then — slowly, like a man lifting something heavier than his body should be able to bear — he stood.

"One more," Moses said. "God says one more."

The darkness pressed against the walls. The lamp flickered. And somewhere in Eli's chest, beneath the weight and the grey and the exhaustion, the hum held steady.

## Chapter 5: The Night of Passing

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 1, 2018 — Cairo, Egypt*
*This wasn't a ritual when it happened. It was the longest night of these people's lives. They stood in their houses with sandals on and staffs in hand, eating bread that hadn't had time to rise, listening. Waiting. The angel of death was passing over their doors because of the blood of a lamb. That's a family crouched in the dark, trusting a promise with their children's lives.*

---

The night came without stars.

Moses had given the instructions. Every Hebrew family was to take a lamb — a year-old lamb, without blemish — and slaughter it at twilight. They were to take the blood and paint it on the doorposts and the lintel — the wooden beam above the door. Top and sides. Then go inside. Eat the lamb with bitter herbs and unleavened bread. Don't leave until morning.

Eli watched the preparations and felt the weight of what was coming settle over the Hebrew quarter like a held breath. The families moved quickly. No one argued. No one asked why. After ten plagues and ten broken promises, these people had run out of skepticism. They were past the point of weighing evidence. They were at the raw, ragged edge of hope — the kind that doesn't feel like hope at all. The kind that feels like surrender.

A man across the narrow street lifted his youngest child onto his shoulders and carried her inside. The doorposts behind him were dark with blood. His wife stood in the doorway, scanning the street once — counting neighbors, checking doors — before she pulled the curtain closed.

"What happens tonight?" Jordan asked. His voice was very quiet. He was standing close to Eli, closer than he usually stood.

Eli looked at him. Jordan's face was open in a way Eli had only seen a few times — the guarded expression gone, replaced by something younger and more vulnerable. Jordan knew the answer. He'd been paying attention. He was asking anyway, because some things need to be said out loud before you can make yourself believe them.

"The firstborn," Eli said carefully. "Every firstborn in Egypt. But the houses with the blood on the doors — they're passed over. Protected."

Jordan nodded slowly. He looked at the painted doorways. "So the blood is..."

"A sign. For the angel to pass over."

Elena was quiet. That, more than anything, told Eli how heavy this night was. Elena, who filled every silence with action or words, stood with her hands at her sides and said nothing. Her eyes moved from door to door, reading the dark marks on the wood like a language she was learning to understand.

A Hebrew woman appeared at the end of the street and beckoned to them — waving urgently, the way you wave at children who've wandered too far. She didn't seem surprised to see three kids standing alone in the street. She simply pulled them inside her small house, where a family of seven sat around a low table.

The lamb was roasted. The bread was flat and plain. Bitter herbs sat in a clay bowl. The family ate standing up, sandals on their feet, cloaks gathered at their waists. Ready to move. Ready to run. Eating a meal and waiting for the worst night of their lives to end.

The father of the family looked at his children — two boys and a girl, the oldest maybe eight — and Eli saw his jaw clench. The blood was on the door. The promise was spoken. But promises had been broken nine times before.

Eli understood then — really understood, in the way the Pathfinder required — what faith meant in this room, on this night. It wasn't confidence. It wasn't certainty. It was a family standing in their shoes, eating a meal they could barely taste, trusting a God they couldn't see to keep a promise they couldn't verify, because the alternative was despair too deep to survive.

The hum in his chest was different tonight. Lower. Steadier. Like a heartbeat that wasn't his.

Outside, the night deepened. No sounds from the Egyptian quarter. No dogs barking. No wind. The silence was total and absolute, and it carried a weight that pressed against the walls of the small house like water against a dam.

Then — distant, on the far side of the city — a sound. A cry. Then another. And another. Rising, spreading, a wave of grief that rolled through the Egyptian streets like a flood of its own.

The Hebrew family froze. The mother gathered her children against her, pulling them close with both arms, her face buried in the youngest one's hair. The father stood facing the door, his staff in his hand, his lips moving in silent prayer. Eli saw the muscles in his arm trembling.

The youngest girl — maybe four, maybe five — looked up at her mother. "Ima, why are people crying?"

The mother didn't answer. She held on tighter.

Elena reached out and took Eli's hand. He took Jordan's. Jordan didn't pull away. His grip was fierce, his knuckles hard against Eli's fingers, and Eli could feel the rubber band on Jordan's wrist pressing into his own skin. The three of them stood together in the small, lamp-lit room while the sound of mourning washed over Egypt like a tide.

Jordan's face was very still. Not blank — the opposite. So full of feeling that it had gone past expression into something deeper. His lips moved once, and Eli barely caught the words: "They're safe."

He wasn't talking about the family. Or maybe he was. Eli didn't know. But the way Jordan said it — quiet, almost reverent, like he was testing the weight of the word — made Eli think that something had shifted in the place where Jordan kept his doubts locked up tight.

The blood held.

The angel passed over.

And when the first grey light of dawn seeped under the curtain, the family was still standing. Still whole. The father looked at his children, counted them, touched their faces one by one. Then he sat down on the floor and wept.

Not grief. Something past grief and past relief and past understanding. The tears of a man who had trusted a promise in the dark and found it true.

## Chapter 6: Going Out

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 3, 2018 — Cairo, Egypt*
*Two million people who had never known anything but slavery, walking out of the only world they'd ever known, following a man with a stick. Going out. As if they were just stepping through a door. In a way, they were — a door painted with blood and held open by a promise.*

---

The morning after Passover, Egypt broke.

Eli had seen crowds before — county fairs, airport terminals, the time Mom took them to New York City and Times Square swallowed them in a river of bodies. None of it prepared him for this.

The Hebrew quarter emptied like a dam bursting. People poured out of houses, out of alleys, out of the cramped and stinking corners where they'd lived for generations. They carried everything they owned — bundles of cloth, cooking pots, jars of grain, bread dough still in the kneading bowls because it hadn't had time to rise. Children rode on shoulders. Old women walked with staffs and the stunned, blinking look of people emerging from a cave into daylight.

And they walked.

Not running. Not marching. Walking — hundreds of thousands of people moving in a direction that wasn't back. For the first time in four hundred years, their feet were carrying them somewhere they chose to go.

"Look at them," Elena said. She was standing on a low wall, scanning the river of humanity with her hand shading her eyes. Her voice had the rough edge it got when she was trying not to cry. "They're actually going."

A little girl stumbled in the road, losing her grip on a clay jar that shattered at her feet. She started to cry. Elena jumped off the wall before Eli could blink, scooped up the girl, and carried her back to her mother. The mother stared at Elena, then clutched her daughter tight and kept walking. Elena climbed back up on the wall, her jaw set.

"Nobody gets left behind," she said. It wasn't a suggestion.

"The Egyptians are giving them things," Jordan said. He sounded baffled. "Look — that woman just handed over a gold bracelet. That man gave them silver. They're *paying* them to leave."

He was right. Egyptian families stood in their doorways, pressing jewelry and clothing and silver vessels into Hebrew hands. Their faces were grey with grief and fear. *Go. Go quickly. Before we're all dead.*

Eli found Moses in the thick of it. The shepherd stood at the junction of two roads, staff raised above the crowd like a signal flag, directing the flow of people the way he'd directed his flock on the mountain slopes. His voice was steady — not loud, but carrying, the voice of a man who had found something stronger than his stutter in the hours since the darkness lifted.

"Keep moving. Stay together. Don't stop."

The grey weight was still there. Eli felt it — thinner now, beaten back by the sheer impossible momentum of what was happening — but hanging at the edges like storm clouds on a horizon. The Fade hadn't left. It was waiting.

The journey began. The Pathfinder lurched them forward through the first days — flashes of movement, dust, the endless shuffle of feet on sand. Eli caught fragments:

A woman singing. The first song in the Hebrew quarter in — how long? Years? Decades? Her voice was cracked and uncertain, like a door opening on rusty hinges, but it was a song.

A boy Elena's age running alongside a donkey, laughing. Just laughing. No reason. No joke. The sound of someone discovering that their mouth could make a shape it had forgotten.

An old man — very old, his beard white and his back bent — kneeling in the sand and pressing his forehead to the ground. Praying or weeping or both. His family waited for him. No one told him to hurry.

Two women walking arm in arm, their faces cracked and sunburned, singing a song Eli didn't know the words to but understood anyway. It was a song about going home. About a place none of them had ever seen but all of them remembered, carried in stories told in whispers over four centuries of someone else's bricks.

"They don't even know where they're going," Jordan said. He was walking beside Eli, his hoodie transformed into a linen wrap, his glasses somehow still on his face. "Two million people walking into a desert with no map, no supply chain, no infrastructure. This is —" He stopped. Pushed his glasses up. "This should be terrifying. But look at them."

He was right. The faces weren't terrified. They were something past terror and past courage and past logic. They were free. For the first time in four hundred years, and they didn't need a map for that.

But underneath the joy and the chaos and the singing, Eli could feel it. The weight. Not gone. Growing.

Because behind them, Egypt was changing its mind.

"He's coming," Moses said on the second evening, staring back the way they'd come. His voice was flat and tired. Aaron stood beside him, and both brothers looked toward the horizon where dust was beginning to rise in a thin brown line.

"Who?" Elena asked, though she already knew. Eli could see it in the way her shoulders squared, the way her hands became fists at her sides.

"Pharaoh," Jordan said. "He changed his mind again."

"He always changes his mind," Elena snapped. "That's literally all he does."

The news moved through the camp like fire through dry grass. Eli watched it happen — the singing stopped, the laughter died, and the old familiar weight settled back onto shoulders that had briefly, miraculously straightened. A mother pulled her children close. A man looked down at his hands and then back at the horizon, measuring the distance to a freedom he'd held for two days before the fist closed again.

The grey thickened. Not fog — not the visible kind. But the air felt dense, the way it feels before a storm that never arrives. The hum in Eli's chest dropped to a low drone that vibrated in his teeth.

"They're going to panic," Jordan said.

They did. The screaming started at the back of the multitude and rolled forward like a wave. People turned. People pointed. On the horizon, the dust cloud had become a line of chariots — hundreds of them, bright metal flashing, horses running in formation. The most powerful army in the world, bearing down on two million unarmed former slaves.

And ahead of the Hebrews, flat and infinite and utterly impassable, the Red Sea stretched to the horizon.

Moses stood between the water and the army. His staff was in his hand. His shoulders were shaking.

Eli looked at the man who'd argued with God at the burning bush, who'd stood before Pharaoh nine times, who'd led two million people into the desert on nothing but a promise. And he saw the fear again. Raw and real and completely human.

The empire behind. The sea ahead. And in Moses' hand, a stick.

## Chapter 7: The Sea

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 12, 2017 — Sinai Peninsula, last day*
*I stood at the coast today and tried to imagine it — six hundred chariots behind you, the sea in front, and nothing in your hand but a stick. Moses raised that stick over an ocean. That's a man choosing to do what God said when doing it looked insane. The staff was just a stick. It was always just a stick. But the hand that raised it was trusting something bigger than wood.*

---

The screaming was everywhere.

Behind the multitude, Pharaoh's chariots churned across the desert floor, wheels cutting furrows in the sand, horses frothing, bronze armor blazing in the late afternoon sun. The sound was thunder — not from the sky but from the earth itself, six hundred chariots pounding the ground into submission.

Ahead, the sea sat still and deep and utterly indifferent to the two million people trapped against its shore.

People were shouting at Moses. Not praying. Shouting. The words hit Eli like thrown stones.

"Did you bring us out here to die?"

"Weren't there enough graves in Egypt?"

"We told you to leave us alone! It was better to serve the Egyptians than to die in this desert!"

Moses faced them. The staff in his hand trembled. His jaw was clenched so tight Eli could see the muscles jumping. And for one terrible, suspended moment, Eli saw the possibility of collapse. Saw Moses' grip loosen. Saw the staff begin to lower.

The grey surged. Not around the edges anymore — it thickened the air itself, turning everything heavy and slow. The shouts seemed muffled, the light dimmer. Elena's energy drained out of her. Jordan went very still, his eyes unfocused.

The Fade was feeding. On the panic, on the despair, on the four-hundred-year-old certainty that Pharaoh always won. The crowd's terror thickened the air like smoke. Hope drained from face after face — Eli watched it go, watched people's eyes go flat and empty, watched shoulders sag under a weight that had nothing to do with the desert heat.

Eli felt it pressing against the hum in his chest — not attacking him, but surrounding everything, making the whole world feel impossible. Making the sea look too wide, the army too close, the stick in Moses' hand too small.

Moses stood at the waterline with his back to the sea and two million terrified people pressing toward him and an army closing like a fist behind them, and he said:

"Don't be afraid."

His voice cracked on the word *afraid*. It was not the voice of a general. It was not the voice of a hero. It was the voice of a man who was afraid and knew it and chose to speak anyway.

"Stand firm. Watch. The Lord will fight for you. You only need to be still."

Then Moses turned to face the sea. And Eli saw his lips move, silent. Not speaking to the people. Speaking to God. The words were too quiet to hear, but Eli could read the shape of them on Moses' lips, and they looked like: *I can't do this.*

And then, without waiting for an answer — without waiting for confidence or certainty or the fear to go away — Moses raised the staff.

Just a stick. Olive wood. Worn smooth by a shepherd's grip. Nothing special about it. Nothing at all.

He raised it over the water.

Eli felt it happen before he saw it.

The hum in his chest detonated. Not louder — *clearer.* Like a bell struck in a padded room, where the padding suddenly disappears and the sound rings free. The grey weight cracked. Not shattered — cracked, like ice on a river in spring, and through the cracks poured something warm and bright and undeniable.

The wind came.

Not a breeze. Not a gust. A wind so strong it came from the east like a living wall, slamming into the surface of the sea with a sound like a thousand sails filling at once. Eli's tunic whipped against his body. Sand stung his eyes. Elena grabbed his arm and Jordan grabbed hers and the three of them leaned into the gale as the sea began to move.

The water drew back. Not like a tide going out — too fast, too deliberate, as if something vast and invisible had reached down and pulled the water apart with both hands. The surface split down the center and the halves drew away from each other, revealing wet sand and rock and shells and the dark mud of the seabed, glistening under a sky that was suddenly, impossibly, brilliantly blue.

A path. Straight through the sea. Walls of water standing on either side, tall as buildings, held in place by nothing Eli could see. Fish moved inside the walls like shadows in glass.

The screaming stopped.

Two million people stared at the impossible path and said nothing. The silence lasted three seconds. Five. Ten.

Then Moses walked forward. Staff in hand. Feet on wet sand. Into the sea.

And Eli saw it happen. Saw the moment. Not the miracle — the moment before the miracle. The moment Moses chose to raise a stick over an ocean. The moment between the fear and the obedience, when the answer to *I can't do this* was not *yes you can* but *do it anyway.*

That was when it happened.

A sound. Small. Almost lost in the roar of the wind. A sharp *crack* — like a twig snapping — and a sliver of wood separated from Moses' staff. A splinter, no longer than Eli's finger, spinning in the wind.

Not at the moment the sea parted. Not at the moment of miracle.

At the moment Moses raised the stick.

The splinter caught the light and blazed — a brief, bright pulse of amber warmth, the same glow Eli had seen in a stone in a streambed and a branch in a dove's beak and a thread on a courtyard floor. It tumbled through the air and landed in the wet sand at the edge of the path, glowing softly, waiting.

"There," Eli breathed.

People were moving now. The front ranks stepped onto the sea floor, hesitant at first, then faster, then running. Families. Children. The old man Eli had seen kneeling in the desert — walking between walls of water with his eyes closed and his arms raised and his face turned up toward a sky he could see for the first time without Egypt's shadow on it.

The chariots were close. The ground shook. Pharaoh's army reached the shore just as the last of the Hebrews stepped onto the seabed.

Eli ran. His sandals slapped wet sand. Elena was beside him, fast and sure. Jordan was right behind, breathing hard. The splinter lay at the edge of the path, half-buried in mud, glowing with a steady amber light that pulsed like a heartbeat.

Eli knelt. The walls of water rose on either side, enormous and impossible. The wind howled. Behind them, the chariots were driving onto the seabed, wheels sinking into the mud.

He reached down.

The splinter was warm. The same alive warmth. The same heartbeat. But this time, Eli understood what he was holding. Not a relic of miracle. A relic of obedience. A piece of the moment when a terrified man chose to trust.

The grey shattered.

Not cracked — shattered, like a window hit by a stone, fragments of heaviness flying apart and dissolving in the wind. The colors blazed. The sky was blue. The water was green and deep and alive. The sand was gold. And through the path between the waters, two million free people walked toward the other side.

The hum rose. Clear and strong and joined now by three other notes — the stone's note, the branch's note, the thread's note — harmonizing into something that was almost a chord. Almost a song. The light spread from the splinter in Eli's hand, warm and total, and the sea and the sky and the sand began to dissolve into brightness.

The last thing Eli saw before the light took everything was Moses, standing on the far shore, staff raised, watching as the walls of water thundered back together behind the last Hebrew family, swallowing Pharaoh's chariots, swallowing the empire's long reach, swallowing four hundred years of *there is no out.*

The sea closed.

Egypt ended.

And Eli's hand was full of light.

## Chapter 8: Just a Stick

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*December 31, 2018 — Home office, midnight*
*"What is in your hand?" God asked. "A staff," Moses said. Just a staff. God didn't give Moses a weapon. He gave him a job and told him to use what he already had. I think the Pathfinder works the same way. It's waiting for the right hand. And these old hands — their job was to find it and put it where the right hands could reach. Mijo, if you're reading this: it was always enough.*

---

Old books and cinnamon.

Eli opened his eyes and he was standing in Abuelo's study with the afternoon light falling through the window at the same angle it had fallen when they left. The same dust motes. The same moment. As if the sea and the fire and the four hundred years had fit inside a single breath.

His right hand was open. Empty. The warmth was fading from his palm like the last heat of a held cup of coffee. The splinter was gone.

"Every time," Jordan said quietly. He was staring at his own hands. "Every time, it dissolves. We bring back nothing."

"We bring back everything," Elena said. She was pacing the room, too wired to sit. She kicked the leg of the desk gently, then climbed onto the armchair and crouched there like a cat.

"Did we just walk through the Red Sea?" she said.

"We did."

"Between walls of *water*."

"With fish in them," Jordan added. "I saw a fish. Inside the wall. Just swimming around like it was a normal Tuesday."

Elena grinned. "That was the craziest thing we've ever done. And we watched a kid fight a giant."

"The giant was bigger," Jordan said. "But the sea was wetter. Marginally."

She was looking at the Pathfinder.

Eli looked too. And his breath stopped.

The fourth symbol — the rough scratch that might have been a staff, barely visible in the bronze — had transformed. The lines were sharp and precise now, engraved with a clarity that looked almost alive. The surface within the symbol was polished to a deep, warm brown, the color of olive wood, and the enamel inlay caught the light like something that had its own fire. A faint glow pulsed from within, steady as breath.

Four symbols now. A stone, amber and bright. A branch, green as a living leaf. A thread, rich and deep as a woven coat. And a staff, brown and warm and solid as the wood it remembered.

The hum had changed. Four notes now. Not a chord exactly — something richer, something that moved between harmony and melody like a song trying to find its shape. Eli felt it in his chest, in his arms, in the palms of his hands. It was fuller than before. More certain. Like a voice that had found more of its range.

Eight symbols remained. Faint scratches in old bronze. Waiting.

Jordan sat on the edge of Abuelo's desk. He'd taken off his glasses and was cleaning them on his hoodie — the thing he did when he needed time to think, when his hands needed to be busy while his brain caught up. When he put them back on, his eyes were different. Not the guarded look. Not the skeptical eyebrow. Something more open and more honest and harder to name.

"He didn't want to go," Jordan said. "Moses. He actively, specifically, repeatedly did not want to go. He said 'send someone else.' And God sent *him*."

"Yeah," Eli said.

"And the staff was just a stick."

"Yeah."

"So the miracle wasn't the staff. Or the sea. Or the plagues." Jordan paused. He was working through something, building it carefully, the way he always did — not leaping to conclusions but constructing them one brick at a time. "The miracle was that he raised it."

Elena sat cross-legged on the floor, her chin in her hands. "Abuelo said that. On the phone. 'The staff was never magic. It was just a stick. Until he trusted.'"

"Until he trusted," Eli repeated. The words settled into him like stones into water, sinking deep, finding the bottom. He thought about Moses on the shore of the sea with two million people screaming behind him and an army closing in front and nothing in his hands but a piece of wood. He thought about the gap between *I can't do this* and raising the staff anyway.

He thought about himself. The kid who knew the answers. The kid who froze.

"I do that," he said quietly. "What Moses did. The freezing. The 'send someone else.' I do that all the time."

"I know," Elena said. Simply. Like stating a fact.

"But he raised the stick," Jordan said. "He was terrified, and he raised it anyway."

Eli nodded. His throat was tight. "Because God said to. And it was just a stick. And it was enough."

"I want to call Abuelo," Eli said.

Elena was already reaching for the phone. She dialed the hospital number from memory — Eli hadn't even known she'd memorized it — and held the receiver between them so they could both hear.

It rang twice. Three times. Then a click, and breathing. Slow and ragged and warm.

"Abuelo," Eli said. "We found it. The staff. You were right."

A pause. The sound of machines. Then Abuelo's voice, thin as paper, strong as roots: "Just a stick, mijo?"

"Just a stick."

Eli could hear the smile. Couldn't see it — but he heard it, the way the breath changed, the way the silence softened. "Four down," Abuelo whispered. "You're doing it, nietos. You're really doing it."

"Are you getting better?" Elena asked. Her voice had the crack in it that meant she was holding back something big.

"Getting there. Slowly." Another breath. "The next one... the walls come down, but not the way you think. Listen to the horn."

The line went quiet again. Not dead. Just resting.

Eli hung up and stood in Abuelo's study with his sister and his friend and the Pathfinder humming its four-note song, and he felt something he hadn't felt before. Not courage exactly. Not confidence. Something smaller and more honest.

Willingness.

The willingness to raise the stick. Even when the stick was just a stick. Even when the sea was too wide and the army was too big and his own hands were shaking.

Jordan was reading the spines of Abuelo's journals on the shelf, tilting his head to see the labels. He pulled one down and opened it, and Eli saw a sketch — a curved horn, drawn in Abuelo's quick pencil strokes. Notes in the margins: *Joshua 6. Seven priests, seven days, seven times on the seventh day. The walls fell outward. Not physics. Obedience.*

"Jericho," Jordan said. He looked up. A year ago, his face would have been skeptical. Six months ago, guarded. Now it was something else — curious in a way that went deeper than intellect, that reached into the place where questions become real because the answers actually matter.

"The walls come down," Jordan said. "But not the way you think."

Outside, the summer evening was settling over Abuelo's neighborhood. Crickets sang in the overgrown garden. The wind chimes made of old keys clinked in a breeze that smelled like warm grass and coming rain. Jordan's house sat next door, still with boxes in the garage, still unsettled — but less so. Less than it used to be.

The Pathfinder hummed. Four notes. Almost a chord. Almost a song. Building toward something that none of them could hear yet but all of them could feel — a melody taking shape in the bronze, patient as a promise, waiting for the next note.

Eight symbols. Eight scratches in old metal. Eight doors waiting to be opened.

Eli set the Pathfinder down on its leather cloth and looked at his friends. Elena grinned — the full grin, the gap-toothed dare-the-world grin. Jordan closed the journal carefully and slid it back onto the shelf.

"Same time tomorrow?" Jordan said. He was already heading for the door. His hand was on the rubber band at his wrist, but he wasn't fidgeting with it. Just touching it. A habit that was changing from anxiety to anticipation, though he'd never admit it.

"Same time tomorrow," Eli said.

The door closed. The house settled. Eli stood alone in Abuelo's study with the smell of old books and cinnamon and the soft, steady glow of four symbols in the fading light.

He looked at his hands. Empty. Ordinary. The hands of a twelve-year-old kid who froze when things got hard and knew the answers but couldn't make himself speak.

But Moses' hands had been ordinary too. And he'd raised the stick.

Eli closed the study door. Four notes hummed behind it — quiet, steady, almost a song — carrying the memory of a shepherd who said *not me* and went anyway.
