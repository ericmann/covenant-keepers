## Chapter 1: The Weight of Seven

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 9, 2017 — Mount Carmel, northern Israel*
*The ridge was green today. That surprised me. I expected desert — bare rock, windswept nothing. But Carmel is lush, thick with oak and pine, and the Mediterranean glittered below like hammered bronze. Hard to reconcile this quiet, beautiful place with the story that happened here. One man. Four hundred and fifty opponents. A God who answered with fire. I climbed to the traditional site of the altar and sat there for an hour. The wind was warm. I kept thinking: Elijah was terrified. The text doesn't say that outright. But read between the lines — a man who ran for his life immediately after his greatest victory? That's not confidence. That's a man who spent everything he had on a single moment of faith and had nothing left.*

---

The Pathfinder sat on the desk, humming its six-note chord.

Eli stood in Abuelo's study with his hands in the pockets of his field jacket and tried to breathe normally. Six symbols glowed around the bronze disc — stone, branch, thread, staff, horn, seal — each one sharp and polished and faintly luminous in the late afternoon light. Six adventures. Six moments of God's faithfulness carved into ancient metal.

Six times the Fade had been waiting for them.

"You're staring at it again." Elena leaned in the doorway, arms crossed, a popsicle stick clamped between her teeth. "You've been standing there for twenty minutes."

"I'm thinking."

"You're worrying. There's a difference."

She was right. Eli could feel it — the tight knot below his ribs that had been tightening for weeks. Each adventure had been harder. The Fade arrived earlier, pressed closer, pushed harder. In Babylon, it had crept into Daniel's story before they'd even found their footing. The grey fog that drained color and muffled sound and turned faith into forgetting — it was getting stronger.

Or they were getting weaker.

Eli pulled his notebook from his jacket pocket and flipped it open to the page he'd been rereading all week. His own handwriting, tight and slanted, listing what they knew. *Rule 1: Can observe, can't change the outcome. Rule 2: Relic appears when we understand the meaning. Rule 3: Fade can't touch us directly.* Below the rules, he'd written a question and underlined it twice: *What happens when the Fade stops following the rules?*

He didn't have an answer. That was the problem. He was the thinker, the planner, the one who read Abuelo's journals and figured out the pattern. But the Fade was changing faster than he could map it, and the thing Eli feared most — more than the fog, more than the cold, more than the doubt — was being the one everyone looked to for answers and having none.

Jordan came up the stairs two at a time, his dark hoodie unzipped over a t-shirt with a pixelated spaceship on it. His glasses were slightly crooked. "Your grandpa called. He's doing better today. Physical therapy went well. He said —" Jordan paused. "He said to trust the next one. That's it. 'Trust the next one.' And then he coughed for like thirty seconds and your mom took the phone."

Eli closed his eyes. Abuelo's guidance had been like this for months — cryptic, brief, broken by the limits of his recovery. A sentence here. A word there. Enough to point them in a direction. Never enough to explain.

"Trust the next one," Elena repeated. She pulled the popsicle stick from her mouth and pointed it at the Pathfinder. "So let's do the next one."

"Just like that?"

"Eli. We've done this six times. We know the rules. We know what to expect."

"The Fade is getting worse."

"So we're faster."

Jordan leaned against the bookshelf, arms crossed. "She's not wrong. Waiting doesn't make it easier. But —" He pushed his glasses up. "I want it on record that I think whatever's coming is going to be worse than Babylon. The pattern isn't subtle."

Eli looked at the Pathfinder. The seventh symbol — rough, barely visible, the faint outline of a square stone — waited dull and unfinished among its glowing neighbors. He thought about Abuelo's voice on the phone, thin and tired but steady. *Trust the next one.*

He reached out and picked up the Pathfinder.

The warmth hit first. Then the hum — rising from the six completed symbols into a chord that climbed through his chest and filled the room. The light came from everywhere, golden and total, erasing the study, the desk, the maps on the walls. Elena grabbed his arm. Jordan's shoulder pressed against his.

The world went white, then hot, then bright.

And then they were somewhere else entirely.

## Chapter 2: The Mountain Between Two Kingdoms

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 10, 2017 — Mukhraka, Mount Carmel*
*The local Druze community maintains the traditional site of Elijah's confrontation. The view is staggering — you can see the Jezreel Valley, the hills of Galilee, the sea. It's a natural amphitheater. Whatever happened here happened in full view of the world. No privacy. No escape. The Carmelite monks built a small church at the summit, but under the stone floor there are older foundations. Much older. Some stones have burn marks that no one can explain. I spent the afternoon sketching them in my journal and pretending my hands weren't shaking.*

---

Dust. Heat. The smell of dry pine and animal dung and smoke from a hundred cook fires.

Eli blinked hard against white sunlight. They were standing on a ridge — a long, green ridge that dropped away sharply on one side toward a glittering blue sea and on the other toward a wide, golden valley. The air was thick with heat and the hum of insects.

He looked down. Sandals. A rough, undyed tunic. The Pathfinder was gone from his hands, replaced by the familiar empty-palmed tingle that meant it was back on Abuelo's desk, waiting.

"I will never get used to the wardrobe change," Jordan muttered. He was tugging at his tunic like it was personally offending him.

Elena didn't seem to notice her clothes. She was staring at the valley below, eyes wide. "Where are we?"

Eli turned slowly. The ridge was dotted with scrubby oaks and low brush. To the north, a road wound along the spine of the mountain. People were moving on it — hundreds of people, maybe thousands, streaming up from the valley in loose, disorganized crowds. Some carried bundles. Some led goats. Many carried nothing at all. Their faces looked the way the Israelite soldiers' faces had looked in the Valley of Elah — tight, uncertain, waiting for something they hoped wouldn't come.

But this was different from Elah. Different century. Different mountain. And the tension in the air had a different flavor — not military fear, but something more fractured. Like a family in the middle of a fight that had been going on so long nobody remembered how it started.

"Mount Carmel," Eli said quietly. "Northern Israel. We're in the time of the divided kingdom."

"Divided how?" Jordan asked.

"Israel split in two after Solomon died. The northern kingdom kept the name Israel. The southern kingdom became Judah. The north —" Eli swallowed. "The north went bad. Really bad. King Ahab married a foreign queen named Jezebel, and she brought her gods with her."

"Her gods," Elena said.

"Baal. The rain god. The fertility god. Jezebel set up temples and altars everywhere. She imported hundreds of prophets of Baal and gave them official positions. And she hunted down the prophets of the true God." Eli's voice dropped. "Killed them. Drove them into hiding. The whole northern kingdom was worshiping Baal, and anyone who refused was a target."

Jordan was quiet. His eyes swept the crowds moving up the mountain. "And we're here for —"

"Elijah," Eli said. "The last prophet standing."

A murmur moved through the crowd like a wave. People stopped walking. Heads turned toward a point further up the ridge where the road crested a small rise. Eli couldn't see what they were looking at yet, but he could feel the shift — a collective holding of breath, the way a room goes still when someone important walks in.

Then a second murmur, louder, sharper. From the opposite direction — down the valley road — a procession was climbing. This one was organized. Soldiers in polished bronze flanked a line of figures in elaborate robes, their garments dyed deep purple and crimson. At the center of the procession, a golden litter carried a man with a thin crown and a face like a person who hadn't enjoyed anything in a very long time.

"That's the king," Elena said.

"Ahab," Eli confirmed. "He's coming because he was told to. Elijah sent him a message: 'Gather all of Israel on Mount Carmel. Bring the prophets of Baal.' This was Elijah's idea."

Jordan stared. "One guy challenged the entire religious establishment of a kingdom? And the king just... showed up?"

"Israel was in the middle of a three-year drought. No rain. Crops failing. People starving. Elijah had announced the drought as judgment. Ahab hated him — called him the 'troubler of Israel.' But he was desperate."

Behind the king's procession, in clusters of five and ten, came the prophets of Baal. Eli counted until he lost track. Their robes were ornate, their heads shaved in patterns, and they moved with the confidence of men who had power, resources, and numbers on their side.

The kids fell in with the crowd moving up the road. Nobody questioned them — the Pathfinder's camouflage held, as it always did, stitching them into the fabric of the place like they'd always been there.

Eli listened as they walked. The people around them talked in low, worried voices, and the conversations told a story uglier than any history book.

"My goats died last month," a woman said to her neighbor. "All of them. The well went dry."

"The Baal priests say the drought will break if we sacrifice more. My husband gave our last two lambs." The neighbor's voice was bitter. "The drought didn't break."

A man with sun-cracked hands and hollow cheeks spat on the road. "Elijah brought this. The troubler of Israel. If Ahab catches him —"

"Ahab couldn't catch a lame donkey," someone else muttered.

Elena leaned close to Eli. "They don't know whose side they're on."

"That's the whole problem," Eli said. "They've been going back and forth for years. A little Baal worship when times are good, a little prayer to God when times are bad. Hedging every bet. And now everything is falling apart and they don't know who to blame."

Jordan's eyes moved across the crowd with the observational precision that made him useful in every adventure. "Half of them are wearing Baal amulets. See the little bronze sun discs? But that woman there —" He nodded toward an older woman clutching a clay jar to her chest. "She's praying under her breath. In Hebrew. Not to Baal."

"Both sides in the same crowd," Elena said.

"Both sides in the same *people*," Eli corrected. "That's what makes this different. They're not divided into two camps. They're divided inside themselves."

The road crested the ridge, and the summit of Carmel spread before them. Elena's hand found Eli's arm. Her fingers were cool.

Behind the king's procession, in clusters of five and ten, came the prophets of Baal. Eli counted until he lost track. Their robes were ornate, their heads shaved in patterns, and they moved with the confidence of men who had power, resources, and numbers on their side.

"How many?" Elena whispered.

Eli watched the last of them crest the ridge.

"Four hundred and fifty."

## Chapter 3: The Challenge

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 11, 2017 — Haifa, hotel room*
*I keep rereading 1 Kings 18:21. Elijah's opening line to the crowd: "How long will you waver between two opinions?" The Hebrew word is "pasach" — it means to limp, to hobble, to hop back and forth. He's calling them crippled. Not because they chose wrong, but because they refused to choose at all. They were hedging their bets — a little Baal, a little Yahweh — and Elijah looked at them and said: Stop limping. Pick a side. The crowd's response? Silence. Not a single voice. That silence tells you everything about the state of their faith.*

---

The summit of Carmel was broad and flat — a natural platform of exposed rock ringed by wind-bent oaks. The crowd gathered in a rough semicircle, pressing in from every side. Soldiers stood at intervals. The prophets of Baal occupied one end of the clearing like a wall of purple and crimson.

And at the other end, alone, stood a man.

He was not what Eli expected.

Elijah was lean to the point of gauntness. His face was burnt dark by years of sun. He wore a rough garment of animal hide belted with leather, and his hair was long, matted, and wild. He looked like a man who had been living in caves and eating whatever he could find — because he had.

But his eyes. Eli noticed them from thirty paces away. Dark, burning, exhausted, and absolutely certain. They were the eyes of someone who had been afraid for a very long time and had decided to stop letting fear make his decisions.

His hands, though — his hands were shaking.

"He's terrified," Elena whispered. She sounded surprised.

"He's been running for three years," Eli said. "Living in hiding. Jezebel put a price on his head. He's probably the last public prophet of God in the entire northern kingdom."

"And he called *them* here?" Jordan said. "He's on the most-wanted list and he sends the king an invitation?"

"Yeah."

Jordan was quiet for a moment. "That's either insane or the bravest thing I've ever heard of."

Elijah stepped forward. The crowd noise died like a candle in wind.

"How long will you limp between two opinions?" His voice was raw but it carried — not loud, just clear, the way David's voice had been clear in the Valley of Elah. "If the Lord is God, follow Him. But if Baal — follow him."

Silence.

Not a murmur. Not a cough. Thousands of people and not a single voice answered. Eli watched their faces — shame, confusion, the stubborn blankness of people who had been hedging their bets for so long they'd forgotten what conviction felt like.

Elijah's jaw tightened. He'd expected the silence. That didn't make it easier.

"I am the only prophet of the Lord left," he said. "But Baal has four hundred and fifty prophets." He gestured toward the mass of robed figures. "Bring two bulls. Let them choose one. Let them cut it in pieces and lay it on wood, but put no fire to it. I will prepare the other bull and put no fire to it. Then you call on the name of your god, and I will call on the name of the Lord. The God who answers by fire — He is God."

The crowd stirred. A ripple of voices. Eli heard it — the first sound from the people — and it wasn't faith. It was curiosity. Entertainment. The promise of a spectacle in the middle of a drought.

Elena was grinning. Not the entertained grin — her fierce grin, the one that showed the gap in her teeth and meant she'd found something worth fighting for. "Four hundred and fifty against one. I love this."

"You love this?" Jordan said. "The odds are insane."

"That's why I love it. If he wins, the odds make it mean something."

"If he *loses*, the odds make him dead."

"He's not going to lose."

"You don't know that."

Elena looked at Jordan with a patience she didn't usually have. "Yes I do. We've read the story."

"We've read the story of a book that some people believe is —" Jordan stopped himself. He pushed his glasses up and pressed his lips together. "Okay. Fine. I'm watching."

Below on the summit, the prophets of Baal had already selected their bull. They worked quickly — practiced, organized, confident. An altar of stacked stones stood ready. They prepared the animal and laid it on the wood.

Elijah stepped back. He found a rock and sat down.

"What's he doing?" Elena asked.

Eli almost smiled. "Waiting. He told them to go first."

The prophets of Baal arranged themselves around their altar. Four hundred and fifty voices rose together, calling on the name of their god. The sound was enormous — chanting, singing, crying out in a language Eli didn't fully understand. They threw their arms toward the sky. They bowed to the ground. They shouted until their voices cracked.

And nothing happened.

## Chapter 4: The Silence of a Dead God

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 22, 2018 — Home office, late*
*The text says the prophets of Baal called on their god "from morning till noon." Hours. Not minutes. Hours of screaming at the sky and hearing nothing back. I've been in empty temples. I've stood in the ruins of pagan shrines where priests once cried out to gods of stone and wood. There is a specific kind of silence in those places. Not peaceful silence. Empty silence. The silence of a room where no one has ever been home. Elijah understood this. That's why his taunt wasn't cruel — it was diagnostic. He was pointing at the silence and saying: Do you hear it? That emptiness? That's your answer.*

---

One hour. Two hours. Three.

The prophets of Baal did not stop. They circled their altar in a frenzy, their voices rising and falling in waves. Some tore at their robes. Some beat the ground with their fists. The sun climbed to its peak and the heat pressed down on the summit like a weight, and still they cried out, and still nothing came.

Eli sat on a flat stone near the edge of the crowd, watching. Elena sat beside him, her legs pulled up to her chin, her earlier grin gone. Jordan stood behind them, arms crossed, glasses reflecting the white sky.

The crowd was getting restless. The initial curiosity had burned off like morning fog. People shifted on their feet. Some sat down. A few at the back turned away, disappointed.

"How long do we just sit here?" Elena asked. Stillness was always her enemy. She'd torn a piece of dried grass into strips and was shredding the strips into smaller strips.

"As long as it takes," Eli said. "The text says they did this from morning until noon. And then kept going."

"Hours of nothing." Jordan sat down next to them, finally, his back against a boulder. "You'd think after the first hour of total silence from your god, you'd start to wonder."

"Would you?" Eli asked, and it wasn't a challenge. It was a genuine question. "If you'd spent your whole life believing something, and everyone around you believed it too, and the king and the queen and every official in the country said it was true — would one hour of silence be enough to make you question it?"

Jordan didn't answer right away. He pulled at the rubber band on his wrist, thinking. "No," he said finally. "Probably not."

And the prophets kept screaming.

Around midday, Elijah stood up.

He cupped his hands around his mouth and called out across the summit. His voice was dry, almost conversational. "Shout louder. Surely he is a god. Maybe he's deep in thought. Or maybe he's busy. Or traveling. Maybe he's sleeping and needs to be woken up."

Elena let out a short, startled laugh.

"Did he just —" Jordan started.

"He's mocking them," Eli said. "He's pointing at the silence and making sure everyone hears it."

The prophets of Baal shouted louder. They cut themselves with knives, a practice of their worship, blood running down their arms as they begged their god to answer. The frenzy built and built and built until the noise was almost unbearable — a wall of desperate sound hurled at a sky that gave nothing back.

Eli's stomach twisted. Not because the spectacle was frightening, but because it was sad. These men believed. They believed with everything they had. They cut their own skin and screamed until their throats bled and threw themselves at the feet of an altar where nothing waited. Their faith was real. Their god wasn't.

"This is hard to watch," Jordan said quietly.

Elena nodded. She wasn't laughing anymore.

The afternoon shadows lengthened. The shouting grew hoarse, then ragged, then desperate. One by one, the prophets' voices gave out. Some fell to their knees. Some stood with their arms raised, mouths open, no sound left.

The silence didn't arrive all at once. It crept in. Voice by voice, the screaming faded until the only sound on the summit was the wind through the oak trees and the harsh breathing of four hundred and fifty exhausted men.

Nothing had answered.

No fire. No voice. No sign. Just the bull on the altar, the wood underneath, and the vast, empty, indifferent sky.

The crowd was utterly still. Eli could feel it — the weight of that silence pressing down on every person on the mountain. This wasn't the silence of awe. It was the silence of people watching faith crash against nothing and shatter.

And at the edges of the summit — Eli felt it before he saw it — the air went wrong.

The hum rose in his chest like a warning bell. Elena shivered, hard, and wrapped her arms around herself. Jordan rubbed his eyes, blinking.

A grey mist was creeping along the ground. Not from any direction — it seemed to seep out of the silence itself, feeding on the emptiness, thickening the air. Colors dimmed. The green of the oaks went flat. The blue sky turned pale.

The Fade.

It had arrived earlier than any adventure before. Earlier and thicker and more deliberate, rolling across the summit like something that had been waiting for exactly this moment — the moment when the silence of a false god was loudest, when doubt was thickest, when the crowd was most vulnerable to forgetting that real answers existed.

"It's here," Elena whispered. Her teeth were chattering.

Jordan's vision was narrowing. He could tell — he kept turning his head, trying to see past edges that were going grey. "It's worse than Babylon. Way worse."

Eli gripped his knees and stared at Elijah, who stood alone at his end of the summit, watching the silent prophets. The prophet's face was unreadable. His hands had stopped shaking.

Eli knew that feeling. He'd felt it six times — the moment between knowing what you had to do and actually doing it. The moment when fear stops being a warning and becomes just noise. You don't get braver. You just run out of reasons to hesitate.

He looked at Elijah and saw himself. Not the prophet's power, not his authority — his loneliness. The bone-deep exhaustion of being the only person in the room who believed what he believed and having to act on it anyway.

Because it was his turn now.

## Chapter 5: Twelve Stones and Water

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 12, 2017 — Mount Carmel, sunrise*
*I came back at dawn to watch the light hit the summit. I wanted to understand the altar. The text is specific: Elijah used twelve stones, one for each tribe of Israel. Not ten for the northern kingdom. Not two for the south. Twelve. The full number. The undivided promise. In the middle of a kingdom torn in half, Elijah built an altar that said: God remembers all of you. Even the ones who forgot Him. Then he drenched it in water. Three times. In a drought. Water was more precious than gold, and he poured it over the sacrifice like it was nothing. Because either God would answer or He wouldn't. And if He would, the water didn't matter. And if He wouldn't — nothing else mattered either.*

---

Elijah walked to a bare patch of ground near the center of the summit.

He moved slowly, deliberately. The crowd parted for him — not out of respect, exactly, but out of the nervous uncertainty of people who didn't know what to expect and weren't sure they wanted to find out.

He knelt. And he began to gather stones.

Eli watched him select each one with care — testing the weight, the shape, the fit. Not random rocks. Chosen ones. He carried them to the bare ground and began to build.

"Twelve," Eli whispered.

"What?" Elena said.

"He's going to use twelve stones. One for each tribe of Israel."

"Israel has ten tribes in the north," Jordan said. "And two in Judah. They split."

"Exactly. But he's using twelve. All of them. The whole nation. The altar represents everyone — even the ones who walked away."

Jordan's mouth opened slightly, then closed. He didn't say anything, but his eyes stayed on Elijah's hands as the prophet set each stone into place.

The altar took shape — rough, unpolished, a simple platform of stacked stones. Elijah arranged the wood on top, then the pieces of the bull. His movements were unhurried. Methodical. The whole mountain was watching, and the grey mist was crawling closer, and Elijah built his altar like a man who had all the time in the world.

Then he did something that made the crowd murmur.

"Fill four large jars with water," he called out. "And pour it over the offering and the wood."

People stared. In the third year of a drought. Water was life. Water was scarce and precious and desperately needed. And this gaunt, wild prophet was asking them to waste it.

Servants brought the jars. The water splashed over the bull, soaked the wood, ran down the sides of the altar and pooled in a trench Elijah had dug around the base.

"Do it again," Elijah said.

They did.

"Again."

Three times. The altar was drenched. Water filled the trench, glistening in the afternoon light. The wood was soaked through. Even the stones were dripping. If anyone had held a torch to that altar, the flame would have hissed and died.

"He just made it impossible," Jordan said. His voice was tight. "You can't light wet wood. You definitely can't light stone. What is he doing?"

A woman near the front of the crowd was crying. Not from fear or faith — from the waste. Eli could see her face, sunburnt and cracked from the drought, watching twelve jars of precious water soak into a pile of stones. In three years without rain, that water could have kept a family alive for a week. Elijah had poured it out like it meant nothing.

*Because to him it didn't.* Either God would answer, and the water was irrelevant. Or God wouldn't, and nothing was enough. There was no middle ground. No hedge. No careful both-ways bet.

"Making a point," Elena said. She was shivering badly now. The Fade was thick around them — the air tasted flat, metallic, like breathing through a screen. Colors were bleeding out of the scene. The crowd looked grey. The sky looked grey. Even Elijah looked faded, his edges soft, his figure blurring.

But the altar. The altar stayed sharp.

Eli stared at it. Twelve stones, soaking wet, holding up a sacrifice that had no business burning. It was the most impractical, unreasonable, absurd thing he'd ever seen. And something about it made his throat ache.

Because this was what standing alone looked like.

Not standing alone with confidence. Not standing alone with a plan and a backup plan and a reasonable expectation of success. Standing alone with twelve wet stones and a God who hadn't spoken in three years and four hundred and fifty opponents who'd just spent six hours proving that divine silence was the only thing the sky had to offer.

Elijah wasn't brave. He was desperate. He was gambling everything — his life, his reputation, the faith of an entire nation — on a God who might not show up. Who hadn't spoken audibly in years. Who had let His prophets be hunted and killed and scattered while Baal's temples multiplied.

Eli's hands were shaking. He shoved them in the folds of his tunic.

"You okay?" Elena asked.

"No," he said honestly. "I'm trying to imagine doing what he's about to do. Standing up in front of everyone and saying 'my God is real, and I'll prove it,' when you have no control over whether He answers."

"That's faith, Eli."

"I know. That's what scares me."

The Fade pressed closer. The hum in Eli's chest was a steady, vibrating pressure now, like standing too close to a bass speaker. The edges of the crowd were going indistinct — people forgetting why they were here, attention drifting, the enormity of the moment thinning like fabric held up to light.

Elijah knelt before the altar.

His lips moved. Eli stepped closer, straining to hear, pushing through air that felt thick as water.

The prophet was going to pray.

## Chapter 6: The Fire

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 12, 2017 — Mount Carmel, noon*
*I counted the words of Elijah's prayer in Hebrew today. Sixty-three words. The prophets of Baal screamed for six hours. Elijah prayed sixty-three words. No ritual. No frenzy. No self-harm. Just a direct, simple, honest request: "Answer me, Lord, so these people will know that You are God, and that You are turning their hearts back again." That's it. Not a demand. Not a performance. A conversation. The contrast is the sermon. Everything you need to know about real prayer versus counterfeit worship is in the difference between six hours of screaming at nothing and sixty-three words spoken to Someone who is listening.*

---

Elijah's prayer was short.

That was what hit Eli hardest. After six hours of screaming and cutting and desperate, frantic ritual — after all that noise and spectacle and blood — Elijah knelt on the hard ground in front of a soaking wet altar and spoke like a man talking to someone standing right next to him.

"Lord, God of Abraham, Isaac, and Israel." His voice was quiet. No shouting. No theatrics. Just a man and the words. "Let it be known today that You are God in Israel, and that I am Your servant, and that I have done all these things at Your word."

The Fade surged.

Eli felt it like a wall of cold air — a wave of grey that rolled across the summit and slammed into the moment like a fist. Elena gasped and doubled over, arms wrapped tight around herself. Jordan staggered, his hands going to his glasses like he was trying to hold his vision together.

The crowd wavered. People blinked, confused, as if they'd suddenly forgotten where they were. The prophets of Baal, still slumped around their silent altar, lifted their heads with dull, empty eyes.

The Fade was trying to kill the moment before it happened. Drowning it in apathy. Smothering it in the weight of all that silence — six hours of silence that screamed *no one is listening, no one has ever been listening, the sky is empty and prayer is talking to yourself.*

Eli's knees buckled. Not from the cold. From the doubt.

Because that was what the Fade did. It didn't create fear from nothing. It found the doubt already living inside you and turned up the volume. And Eli's doubt was this: *What if Elijah prays and nothing happens? What if God doesn't answer? What if the fire doesn't come?*

He knew the story. He'd read it a hundred times. But standing here, with the Fade pressing in and the silence of a dead god still ringing in his ears and a wet altar steaming in the afternoon heat — knowing the story and trusting the story were two different things.

"Answer me, Lord." Elijah's voice barely carried above the fog. "Answer me, so these people will know that You, Lord, are God, and that You are turning their hearts back again."

Sixty-three words. Then silence.

One heartbeat. Two.

The fire came.

It didn't fall like lightning. It didn't streak from the clouds like a thrown spear. It came down like a hammer — a column of white-gold flame that dropped from the sky and struck the altar with a sound that was less like an explosion and more like a word spoken so loudly it became heat and light.

The bull vanished. The wood vanished. The water in the trench flashed to steam so fast it sounded like a scream. The stones — the twelve stones Elijah had chosen and stacked with such care — blazed white at the edges as the fire consumed everything it touched.

Not burned. *Consumed.* The fire didn't leave ashes. It left nothing. It ate the sacrifice and the wood and the water and the dust underneath and kept burning, kept devouring, a holy hunger that would not stop until every last trace was gone.

The heat hit Eli like a wall. He threw his arms up, stumbled backward, felt Elena grab his tunic. The light was blinding — not golden like the Pathfinder's glow but white, pure, the color of something too real for human eyes.

The Fade screamed.

Not out loud — it couldn't speak. But Eli felt it. A soundless shriek, a convulsion in the grey fog as the fire tore through it like sunlight through smoke. The mist shredded. Colors erupted back into the world — the green of the oaks, the blue of the sky, the brown of the earth — brighter than before, oversaturated, as if the Fade had been dimming them for so long that their true intensity was almost too much to bear.

And the crowd.

Every person on the mountain was on the ground. Not kneeling — *fallen.* Face-down in the dirt, prostrate, the way you drop when you've been slapped by something so real it breaks every argument you've ever built against it. Their voices came in a ragged chorus, not organized, not rehearsed — raw and broken and honest:

"The Lord — He is God. The Lord — He is God."

Eli was on his knees. He didn't remember falling. Tears burned hot tracks down his dusty face. Beside him, Elena was crying too — not sad tears, fierce tears, the kind that come when you see something so big it cracks you open. Jordan was on his hands and knees, his glasses knocked sideways, his face white.

"Jordan?" Eli reached for him.

Jordan's mouth worked. No sound came out for a long moment. Then, barely audible: "That was real."

Not a question. Not qualified. Not hedged with "I mean" or "but what if."

Two words, stripped bare.

"Yeah," Eli said. "It was."

The fire burned. The altar was almost gone — just a few stones left, white-hot at their cores, collapsing inward as the flames consumed them. Steam rose from the empty trench in long, twisting columns.

And in the center of the fire, where the altar had stood, something glowed.

## Chapter 7: The Stone That Remembers

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 13, 2017 — En route to airport*
*I leave Carmel today, and I cannot stop thinking about what comes after the fire. The crowd falls on their faces. The prophets of Baal are dealt with. And then — then Elijah climbs to the top of the mountain and prays for rain. Seven times he sends his servant to look toward the sea. Six times: nothing. The seventh time: a cloud the size of a man's hand. After three years of drought. After fire from heaven. After the most dramatic display of God's power in recorded history — Elijah still has to pray seven times for rain and wait for a cloud the size of a fist. God's power is overwhelming. But His timing is still His own.*

---

The fire dimmed but did not die.

The white-gold blaze that had consumed the altar and everything on it had shrunk to a low, steady glow — a pool of heat and light where twelve stones and a soaked sacrifice had stood moments before. The ground around it was scorched black. The air smelled like ozone and hot metal and something older, something that didn't have a name.

The crowd remained face-down. Some were weeping. Some were praying. Some were simply still, pressed against the earth like people holding on to the only solid thing left in a world that had just been turned inside out.

And then the grey crept back.

Not the fog itself. Not the thick, rolling mist that drained color and muffled sound. Something subtler. Eli felt it first as a whisper in his own mind — not words, not a voice, but an impulse. A reframing.

*Lightning. That was lightning. A freak strike from the upper atmosphere. It happens — dry lightning in drought conditions. There are documented cases. The water on the altar created a conductive surface. The altitude of Carmel, the thermal updrafts from the valley — it's unusual, but it's not impossible. It's not —*

"Stop," Eli said out loud.

Elena looked at him. "What?"

"Can you feel it? The Fade — it's not gone. It's just — it changed."

Jordan's face was tight. He was pressing his palms against his temples, squinting. "Something's — I keep thinking about atmospheric conditions. Electrical discharge patterns. I don't even know atmospheric science, and suddenly I'm an expert? That's not me. Something is feeding me explanations."

"Coincidence," Elena said, but her voice was strange. She shook her head hard, like shaking off water. "No. No, that's it talking. It's trying to make me think it was just coincidence. Weather. Bad luck for the other side, good luck for ours."

The Fade had changed tactics. Not erasure now — reinterpretation. It couldn't undo what had happened. The fire had been too real, too complete, too total. So instead, it was doing what it always did, but more precisely: it was targeting the meaning. Not the event. The *meaning.*

*It was just lightning. It was just weather. It was just —*

Eli stood up. His legs were unsteady. The scorched ground was still warm under his sandals. He walked toward what was left of the altar.

"Eli, wait —" Elena started.

He didn't stop. The heat pushed against his face. The low glow of the remaining fire pulsed like a heartbeat. Most of the stones were gone — dissolved, consumed, reduced to nothing by a fire that ate stone and water like paper.

But one stone remained.

It sat at the edge of the scorch mark, half-buried in ash and blackened earth. It should have been destroyed. Every other stone was gone. The wood was gone. The water was gone. The bull was gone. The *ground* was scorched for three feet in every direction. But this stone — small, rough, the size of Eli's fist — sat in the ashes and glowed.

Not with fire. With the warm amber light he'd seen six times before. The light of a relic.

The Fade pressed harder. Eli felt the explanations crowding his mind — *volcanic rock, heat-resistant mineral, an optical illusion caused by thermal distortion* — rational, reasonable, relentless.

He knelt beside the stone.

"It's trying to explain it away," he said. His voice was shaking but he didn't care. "The fire, the stone, all of it. It wants us to think there's a normal reason. A scientific reason. A reason that means God wasn't here."

He looked up at Jordan. Jordan, whose vision was narrowing, whose edges were going grey, who was standing with his arms crossed and his jaw clenched in the posture of a person fighting something he couldn't see.

"Jordan. You saw the fire."

"I saw the fire."

"Was it lightning?"

Jordan was quiet for a long time. The Fade pushed. Eli could almost see it in Jordan's face — the war between what his eyes had witnessed and what something was trying to make him believe instead.

"No," Jordan said. His voice was rough. "Lightning doesn't eat stone. Lightning doesn't consume water. Lightning doesn't burn from the top down." He uncrossed his arms. His hands were shaking. "I don't know what it was. But it wasn't weather."

The Fade thinned. Not gone — it never went fully away during an adventure. But it thinned, the way morning fog thins when the sun hits it. The explanations quieted. The doubt retreated to its corner, still present but no longer in charge.

Eli reached into the ashes and picked up the stone.

It was warm. Alive warm. Heartbeat warm. The amber glow brightened in his palm, and the hum — the deep, resonant hum he'd carried in his chest since they arrived — rose into a note that harmonized with the six notes already living in the Pathfinder.

Seven notes. Almost a melody.

Elena was beside him. Jordan was beside him. The light spread from the stone — outward, upward, filling the air with golden warmth. The summit of Mount Carmel began to blur. The crowd, the scorched earth, the exhausted prophet kneeling in the ash — all of it softened at the edges, going bright, going warm, going home.

The stone dissolved in Eli's hand. He felt it go — the weight, the warmth, the solidity — melting into light like sugar in water, leaving only the ghost of its heat in his palm.

The world went white.

## Chapter 8: The Seventh Note

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*December 2, 2020 — Hospital room, afternoon*
*The kids called today. Eli asked me something I wasn't ready for: "Abuelo, how do you stand up for something when you're the only one standing?" I told him I'd spent my whole career doing exactly that — defending research that my colleagues dismissed, chasing artifacts that other archaeologists said didn't exist. But that wasn't what he was asking. He wasn't asking about professional courage. He was asking about the kind of faith that has no backup plan. The kind where you pour water on the altar because either God shows up or nothing matters. I didn't have a good answer. I told him: "Mijo, I think that's the kind of courage you can't understand until you need it. And I think you're closer to needing it than you know." He was quiet for a long time after that.*

---

Old books and cinnamon.

Eli opened his eyes. Abuelo's study. Late afternoon light through the single window. Maps on the walls. Journals stacked in their labeled columns. The creak of the old house settling around them like a sigh.

His right hand was open. Empty. The phantom warmth of the altar stone faded slowly from his palm, a ghost of heat that lingered like the last ember of a fire.

He was shaking. Not from cold. From something deeper — the full-body tremor that comes after adrenaline burns out, after the moment passes and your body finally admits how afraid you were.

Elena sat on the floor, her back against the desk, knees drawn up. Her eyes were red. The fierce tears had dried into salt tracks on her dusty — no, clean — face. Sneakers. T-shirt. The braided bracelet on her wrist. Home.

Jordan leaned against the bookshelf by the door. His glasses were back in place, straight on his face. His arms were crossed, but loosely — not the defensive posture, the thinking posture. He was staring at nothing, his expression the open, unguarded look that Eli had only seen a handful of times. The look that meant Jordan's walls were down and he hadn't figured out how to put them back up yet.

The Pathfinder sat on the desk.

Seven symbols glowed.

The seventh — the altar stone — had transformed. Where a faint, rough scratch had been, the engraving was now sharp and deep. The surface was polished smooth, and a warm amber color filled the lines like enamel worked by a master jeweler. A faint glow pulsed from within, steady as a heartbeat.

And the sound. The Pathfinder's hum had changed. Six notes had become seven, and the seven together formed something that was almost a melody — not complete, not resolved, but close enough that Eli could feel the shape of the music it was becoming. Five more notes. Five more relics. Five more promises to preserve.

"Seven," Elena said softly. "More than half."

Eli nodded. He couldn't speak yet. His throat was tight with something that wasn't quite emotion and wasn't quite understanding — something in between, something that had been building since he watched Elijah kneel before a wet altar and pray sixty-three words to a God who answered with everything.

"I don't know if I could have done what he did," Eli said finally. His voice came out rough, cracked. "Standing up there alone. Pouring water on the altar when it already looked impossible. Praying a sixty-three-word prayer when the other side screamed for six hours and it didn't matter."

He turned the Pathfinder in his hands. The seven glowing symbols caught the light.

"He didn't have a backup plan. He didn't hedge. He just — stood there and believed, and if God didn't show up, that was it. Everything was over."

"But God did show up," Elena said.

"But he didn't *know* that. Not the way we know it. We've read the story. Elijah was living it. He poured water on the altar because he was betting everything on a God he couldn't see. In front of everyone. Alone."

Elena pulled herself up and sat on the edge of the desk next to him. She was quiet for a moment — Elena, quiet — and when she spoke, her voice was gentler than usual.

"Eli. You keep saying you couldn't do what he did. But you already have."

He looked at her.

"Every time you pick up the Pathfinder. Every time the Fade shows up and you stay anyway. Every time you walk into a story where you don't know what's going to happen and you can't control the outcome and you do it anyway because you believe it matters." She pointed at the seven glowing symbols. "That's your altar. You've been building it for seven adventures and you didn't even notice."

Eli stared at the Pathfinder. Seven symbols. Seven moments when God showed up and the Fade tried to erase it and three kids from the twenty-first century stood in the gap and said *no, we were there, we saw it, we remember.*

His hands had stopped shaking.

Jordan pushed himself off the bookshelf. He walked to the desk and looked at the Pathfinder for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was careful — the way Jordan's voice always was when he was saying something that cost him.

"The fire wasn't lightning."

"No," Eli said.

"And the Fade — the thing that kept trying to explain it away, to make it feel like coincidence — that was real too. I felt it in my head. Feeding me explanations I didn't ask for." Jordan's fingers went to the rubber band on his wrist. He snapped it once. "I don't know what all of this means yet. The relics. The Pathfinder. The fire. I'm not — I'm not where you guys are. But I can't pretend anymore that it's just coincidence. Seven times isn't coincidence. Seven times is a pattern."

He looked at Eli. "And patterns mean something."

Elena tilted her head. "That might be the most not-skeptical thing you've ever said."

"Don't get used to it." But he didn't take it back.

"The Fade did something new this time," Eli said. "In Babylon, it tried to make people doubt. On Carmel, it tried to make us explain. Like — rewrite the story while we were still standing in it. Replace what we saw with something safe."

"Coincidence as a weapon," Jordan said. "That's smarter than fog."

"It's getting smarter," Elena said. She wasn't smiling.

They were all quiet for a moment. The weight of that settled over the room like the evening shadows.

Eli felt the tightness in his chest loosen. Not all the way. The Fade was still out there. Five symbols still waited, rough and unfinished. The adventures were getting harder and the stakes were climbing and he was still, at his core, the kid who wanted to think his way through everything and froze when thinking wasn't enough.

But he'd picked up the Pathfinder seven times. He'd stood in the gap seven times. And seven times, the fire had come — not always literal fire, but the real, unmistakable, impossible-to-explain presence of something bigger than coincidence.

Maybe Elena was right. Maybe he'd been building his altar all along.

The Pathfinder hummed its seven-note chord in the fading light. Almost a melody. Not complete. But closer.

Outside, the summer evening settled over Abuelo's house. The wind chimes made of old keys clinked softly from the eaves. Somewhere in the distance, the low roll of thunder moved across the sky — real thunder, natural thunder, the kind that meant rain was coming.

Eli set the Pathfinder down on its leather cloth. Seven symbols glowed. Five waited.

He was already thinking about the eighth.
