## Chapter 1: Something Different

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 14, 2019 — Tell es-Sultan, Jericho*
*The walls here are older than anyone wants to admit. Bronze Age, certainly, but the foundation stones feel earlier. I spent the morning arguing with Dr. Feldman about the acoustic properties of the tell — whether concentrated sound at specific frequencies could destabilize mudbrick construction. She thinks I'm reaching. Maybe I am. But I keep thinking about those trumpets. Seven priests. Seven days. Seven times on the seventh day. There's a precision to it that doesn't feel ceremonial. It feels engineered. Sometimes God's blueprints look like nonsense until the building is finished.*

---

The Pathfinder was humming before Eli touched it.

That had never happened before.

He stood in Abuelo's study with his hand hovering over the bronze disc, listening. Four notes rose from the device in a quiet chord — one for each symbol that had transformed. The stone. The branch. The thread. The staff. Four promises preserved, four glowing engravings in a circle of twelve. The other eight were still rough and faint, barely scratches in old metal.

But tonight the hum was different. Louder. More insistent. Like the Pathfinder was tired of waiting.

"You feel that?" Elena said from the doorway. She had her arms crossed, leaning against the frame the way she leaned against everything — like standing still was a temporary compromise with gravity.

"Feel what?"

"I don't know. Like something's... tighter." She rubbed her arms. "I've been cold all afternoon. Not regular cold. The other cold."

Eli knew what she meant. The Fade cold. The bone-deep chill that crawled through Elena like ice water in her veins whenever the grey fog was near. But they were home. They were in the present. The Fade existed in the past, in the Biblical stories where it crept and dimmed and eroded.

Didn't it?

Jordan appeared behind Elena, pushing his glasses up his nose. "Hey. Your front door was open. Also, is it just me, or is my vision doing the thing? The edges are going blurry."

Eli looked at both of them. Elena, cold. Jordan, blurred. And in his own chest, the hum — not from the Pathfinder this time, but from that low place behind his ribs where he always felt the Fade first.

"Something's changing," Eli said.

"Yeah, no kidding." Elena pushed off the doorframe and crossed the room in three quick steps. "Four adventures. Four relics. And every time the Fade showed up a little earlier. A little stronger. So what happens on number five?"

Nobody answered.

The Pathfinder's hum climbed. The four completed symbols pulsed — amber, green, crimson, and the deep brown of the staff — their light filling the dim study like embers in a dying fire. The fifth symbol, faint and rough, began to vibrate. Eli could see it trembling in the bronze: a tiny horn, barely an outline, shivering like a tuning fork.

"It's ready," he said.

Elena grinned. The gap between her front teeth showed. "Finally."

Jordan snapped the rubber band on his wrist. "I want it noted that I have reservations."

"Noted," Elena said. "Grab on."

Eli picked up the Pathfinder. The warmth flooded his hands, his arms, his chest. The four notes swelled into something bigger — not a chord anymore but the beginning of a melody, climbing and reaching toward a fifth note that wasn't there yet. Light poured from the bronze disc, golden and total, erasing the walls and the maps and the bookshelves.

Elena's hand locked onto his arm. Jordan's shoulder pressed against his. The study dissolved.

And then heat. Blinding, white heat that pressed against his skin like a hand. The smell of dust and cook fires and animals and something sharp and mineral — the scent of a desert that had been baking since before memory. Beneath it all, the low hum of thousands of voices, murmuring in the distance.

Eli opened his eyes. The light was so bright he had to squint. His sneakers were gone. Rough sandals scraped against dry ground. His jeans and T-shirt had been replaced by an itching tunic of undyed wool. Beside him, Elena was pulling her hair out of a head covering, scowling. Jordan looked down at himself and made a sound like a tire slowly deflating.

"Every time," Jordan muttered. "Every single time with the outfits."

## Chapter 2: The Walls

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 15, 2019 — Tell es-Sultan, Jericho*
*I measured the foundation today. The original walls were double — an outer wall six feet thick, an inner wall twelve feet thick, with houses built across the gap on top. The whole structure rose five stories in some sections. Standing at the base looking up, the word that comes to mind isn't "fortification." It's "statement." These walls said: We are permanent. We are immovable. Nothing gets in. The people of Jericho had every reason to believe that was true. Walls like these had never fallen.*

---

The walls were enormous.

Eli had read about them. He'd seen Abuelo's sketches, studied the archaeological diagrams, memorized the measurements. None of that prepared him for standing at the base and looking up.

They rose from the desert floor like cliffs — rough stone and packed mud brick, thick as a house, tall as a five-story building. Guard towers jutted from the top at regular intervals. Soldiers paced the ramparts, small as insects against the white sky. The walls curved away in both directions, encircling the city of Jericho in a ring that looked permanent. Eternal. Like the earth had grown them.

"That's not a wall," Jordan said flatly. "That's a mountain someone organized."

They were standing at the edge of a vast camp. Tents made of animal hide stretched across the plain in ordered rows. Cook fires sent smoke spiraling into the hot air. Thousands of people — families, soldiers, elders, children — moved through the camp with the restless energy of people who had been waiting too long.

The Israelites. Eli recognized the camp from Abuelo's descriptions of the exodus encampments. These were the people who had crossed the Jordan River, who had wandered forty years in the wilderness, who had finally arrived at the edge of the Promised Land.

And standing between them and everything they'd been promised was a wall that could not be broken.

Elena was already bouncing on her toes, scanning the camp, cataloging everything. Eli recognized the look — the one that meant she'd already decided they were behind schedule.

"So what's the plan?" she asked. "Siege towers? Battering rams? Are they digging tunnels?"

A trumpet sounded from the center of camp. Not a battle trumpet — something deeper, more resonant, like a voice made of brass. People began moving toward the sound.

The kids followed.

At the center of the camp stood a man who looked like he'd been carved from the same stone as the desert. Broad shoulders, weathered skin, a jaw set so firm it might have been welded shut. He wore a simple tunic, no armor, but he carried himself like a man accustomed to command. His eyes swept the crowd that gathered around him.

"Joshua," Eli whispered.

Joshua spoke. His voice was steady and clear, carrying over the crowd without shouting.

"The Lord has given us Jericho," he said. "Here is what we will do."

Elena leaned forward. Eli could feel her coiling like a spring.

"The armed men will march around the city. Once. Every day. Seven priests will carry seven ram's horns before the Ark of the Covenant. The rest of you will follow behind. You will not shout. You will not speak. You will march in silence."

The crowd stirred. Whispers rippled through the ranks.

"For six days," Joshua continued, "you will circle the city once and return to camp. On the seventh day, you will march around the city seven times. When you hear a long trumpet blast, then — and only then — will every person shout with all their strength."

He paused. His eyes moved slowly across the crowd, as if daring someone to object.

"And the walls will fall."

Silence. The kind of silence that falls when people are trying to decide whether they heard correctly.

Nobody moved. The silence stretched like a rubber band pulled too tight.

A soldier near Eli turned to the man beside him. "March?" he whispered. "Around walls like *that*?"

The other man shook his head slowly. Not disagreeing. Just unable to process what he'd heard.

Elena turned to Eli. Her mouth was open. Her eyes were wide.

"Wait," she said. "That's it? Just... walk?"

## Chapter 3: The Silence

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 16, 2019 — Tell es-Sultan, Jericho*
*I tried to walk the perimeter of the ancient tell today. Took me forty-five minutes at a steady pace. The Biblical account describes a complete circuit of the city walls — armed men, priests, the Ark, and a procession of thousands. In total silence. No war cries. No chanting. Nothing. Imagine the discipline that required. Imagine the doubt it invited. You've just spent forty years getting to this place, and God's battle plan is a parade. I think the silence was the hardest part. Not the walking. The not talking.*

---

Day one was strange.

The armed men formed a column at dawn. They wore leather armor and carried bronze weapons, but they didn't look like soldiers going to war. They looked like soldiers going to a funeral — solemn, confused, walking in a direction they didn't choose with no explanation they understood.

Behind them, seven priests carried seven curved ram's horns. The horns were enormous, each one the length of a man's arm, polished smooth, catching the morning light. Behind the priests, four men carried the Ark of the Covenant on wooden poles, its golden surface blazing in the sun. And behind the Ark, thousands of people followed.

In silence.

The kids walked near the back of the procession, sandals scuffing through the dust. They wore rough tunics that itched at the neck — the same transformation as every adventure. Elena's hair was covered by a cloth that she kept pushing back impatiently.

The quiet was unnatural. Thousands of feet shuffling through dry ground, and nobody speaking. No orders shouted. No marching songs. Just the crunch of sandals, the low moan of the ram's horns — not music, just a sustained note, eerie and resonant — and the sound of breathing. All that breathing, from all those people, walking in the same direction for no reason anyone could explain.

Elena lasted about ten minutes before she leaned toward Eli. "Are we seriously not going to —"

A soldier in front of them turned and pressed a finger to his lips. His eyes were hard. The command had been clear: silence. Total silence.

Elena's mouth clamped shut. Her jaw worked like she was chewing gravel.

The march took them around the entire city. The wall loomed above them, massive and unbroken. The shadow it cast was cold — the kind of cold that came from standing next to something that dwarfed you, that made you feel small without trying. From the ramparts, the people of Jericho watched. At first they watched in silence too, tense and uncertain. An army circling your city was not a good sign.

Then someone laughed.

It started small — a single voice from the top of the wall, sharp and mocking. Then more voices joined. The people of Jericho leaned over the ramparts and shouted down at the marching Israelites. Eli couldn't understand every word, but the tone was clear. Ridicule. Contempt. *This is your army? This is your plan? Walking?*

Elena's jaw tightened. Her fists clenched at her sides.

"Don't," Eli said quietly.

"I'm not doing anything."

"You're about to."

She shot him a look that could have peeled paint. But she kept walking.

They completed one circuit. Then they marched back to camp. That was it.

Day two was worse.

The same march. The same silence. The same mocking from the walls. But now the mockers were bolder. They threw scraps of food. They made obscene gestures. One man leaned over the rampart and pretended to sleep, snoring loudly as the procession passed below.

Elena's whole body was vibrating with the effort of staying quiet.

"This is pointless," she hissed to Eli as they walked back to camp. "We walked in a circle. For an hour. And tomorrow we're going to do it again. And the day after that. WHY are we just walking?"

"It's what Joshua said to do."

"Joshua said God told him to do it. But does this *look* like a battle plan to you? Does this look like anything?"

Eli didn't answer. It didn't look like a battle plan. It looked like a parade with no audience. A performance with no point.

Day three was the worst yet.

The mocking from the walls had organized. The people of Jericho lined the ramparts like spectators at a sporting event. They had drums now, and they beat out a mocking rhythm — a parody of a march — as the Israelites circled below. Children threw pebbles. Women called down insults. A group of men near the main gate held up a crude banner showing a broken horn.

Elena walked with her head down, fists jammed in the folds of her tunic. Eli could see the tendons standing out in her neck.

Jordan fell into step beside Eli. He pushed his glasses up and looked at the walls, then at the silent procession ahead of them.

"Can I say something?" Jordan said.

"You're going to anyway."

"This is either the dumbest military strategy in history or..." He stopped.

"Or what?"

Jordan's mouth worked, but nothing came out. He shook his head. "I don't know. I keep waiting for the part where it makes sense. And it's not happening."

They walked back to camp in silence. The late afternoon light fell thick and orange across the tents, and the smell of cook fires and roasting grain drifted through the camp. Somewhere a baby cried. Soldiers sat in clusters, not talking, staring at the ground or the walls or nothing.

Elena kicked a stone so hard it sailed over three rows of tents.

"Three days," she said to nobody. "Three days of walking in circles while people throw garbage at us. Four more to go." She turned to Eli, and her eyes were bright with something that wasn't tears but was close. "What if nothing happens? What if we march for seven days and the walls just... stay?"

Eli thought of David in the valley. Moses at the sea. The dove circling over endless water.

"It'll happen," he said.

"How do you know?"

He didn't. Not really. He trusted, which was different from knowing and harder in every way.

## Chapter 4: The Whispers

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 3, 2020 — Home office*
*I've been reading Rahab's account again — the woman who hid the Israelite spies in Jericho before the siege. She told them something remarkable: "The terror of you has fallen on us. All who live in this country are melting in fear because of you." The people of Jericho were afraid. The mocking was a mask. I think the most dangerous moment in any act of faith is the middle — when the beginning excitement has faded and the ending isn't visible yet. That's where doubt has the most room to grow.*

---

Day four. The crack appeared in the silence.

Not in the wall. In the army.

Eli heard it first — a murmur near the front of the column, where the armed men marched. Not loud. Not defiant. Just a low grumble, the sound of tired men asking questions they'd swallowed for three days.

"This isn't working."

"Joshua's lost his mind."

"We crossed the Jordan for *this*?"

The whispers spread like smoke through the ranks. By the time the procession reached the northern face of the wall, the murmur had grown. Soldiers were shaking their heads. A group near the center of the column slowed their pace, dragging their feet, making the whole column stretch and stagger.

And at the edges of the camp, in the low places between the tents where morning shadows still lingered, a grey mist was gathering.

Elena stopped walking. "There," she said. She pointed at the mist, and her voice had gone flat and tight. "You see it?"

Eli saw it. The fog was thinner than in previous adventures — no thick grey curtain rolling across the landscape. Instead it seeped up from the ground like groundwater, pooling in the shadows, threading between tents, touching the edges of things and draining them pale. A red banner near the command tent faded to the color of old rust. The cook fires, still burning, seemed to cast less light.

The hum hit Eli's chest like a fist. Not gradual, not building — it was just there, sudden and loud, vibrating his ribs.

"It's early," he said. "The Fade. It's never come this early."

In their first four adventures, the Fade had always arrived late — slipping in during the final act, pressing against the climactic moment. They'd had time to settle in, to understand the story, before the grey fog pushed at the edges. But this was day four of seven. The crisis was still days away.

Jordan pulled his hood up, even in the heat. He was squinting hard, rubbing his temples. "My vision's been off since yesterday. Edges are grey. Getting worse." He looked at Eli with an expression that might have been fear if Jordan ever admitted to fear. "It's here, isn't it? Already."

"Already," Eli confirmed.

The effect on the army was immediate. The whispers turned darker. Eli overheard a cluster of soldiers near the supply wagons, speaking in hushed, urgent tones.

"Joshua didn't hear God. He heard his own ambition."

"We should have attacked on day one. With ladders. With rams. Like a real army."

"Seven days of this? The city will hear us coming. They'll be ready."

"They're already laughing at us."

An older soldier, grey-bearded and scarred, shoved his spear into the ground and sat down. He didn't say anything. He just stopped. Two younger soldiers looked at each other, then sat down beside him. Then three more.

Elena watched the sitting soldiers with her jaw locked. "They're giving up."

"They're confused," Eli said.

"Same thing."

She wasn't wrong.

That night, Eli lay in the tent they'd found at the edge of camp and stared at the goatskin ceiling. The hum in his chest wouldn't let him sleep. He could feel it pulsing, steady and wrong, like a second heartbeat that didn't belong to him.

Beside him, Elena tossed and turned. Even in sleep, her arms were wrapped around herself. Cold.

Day five was harder.

The Fade had thickened overnight, pooling in every shadow, creeping along the ground like a living thing. The morning light, which should have been fierce and white, came through a haze that made everything look washed out. Flat. Like a photograph left in the sun.

The march continued — Joshua demanded it, his voice cutting through the murmurs like a blade — but the ranks were ragged. Soldiers stumbled. Priests carrying the horns walked with their heads bowed, not in reverence but in exhaustion. The Ark bearers' shoulders sagged.

On the walls, the people of Jericho had stopped mocking. That was worse. They watched in silence now, arms crossed, faces calm. The panic Rahab had described — the terror that had melted their hearts — was fading. Each day of silent marching replaced fear with contempt. They weren't afraid of this army anymore.

Elena was shivering despite the heat. She walked with her arms wrapped around herself, teeth clenched against the cold only she could feel.

"Five days," she said as they returned to camp. Her voice was flat. Defeated. Eli hadn't heard that tone from her before — not in any of their four adventures. Elena didn't do defeated. She did angry. She did frustrated. She did let's-go-right-now-before-I-lose-my-mind. But this was different. The fight was draining out of her, and that scared Eli more than the Fade.

"Two more. And nothing. Not one crack. Not one stone loose. Nothing."

Jordan sat on a rock outside their tent and stared at the distant walls. "I keep running the numbers," he said. "Force, resonance, structural integrity. There's no version of this where walking in circles collapses a wall like that. Even if you factor in vibration from the footsteps, even if the trumpets hit some kind of resonant frequency — the math doesn't work. That wall is solid stone and mud brick five stories high. It's not coming down because people yelled at it."

He took off his glasses and cleaned them on his tunic. His hands were shaking slightly.

"But," he said, very quietly, and stopped.

"But what?" Elena demanded.

Jordan put his glasses back on. "But nothing I've seen on these trips follows the math either." He paused. "David shouldn't have won. The flood shouldn't have been survivable. The sea shouldn't have parted. None of it should have worked. And it all did."

He didn't finish the thought. He didn't have to.

The Fade pressed closer in the gathering dark, and the camp felt smaller than it had the day before.

## Chapter 5: Breaking Rank

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 5, 2020 — Home office*
*I called the kids today. Brief call — the nurses are strict about phone time. Eli asked about Jericho. I told him what I've told every student who ever asked about the siege: the hardest part wasn't the waiting. The hardest part was that waiting felt like failure. Every day those walls stood, it looked like proof that God's plan wasn't working. But "not yet" isn't the same as "no." I wish I'd understood that forty years ago. It would have saved me a lot of sleepless nights.*

---

Day six. Elena broke.

It happened during the march — the sixth circuit of the sixth day, the procession dragging around the eastern wall under a sky bleached pale by the Fade. The mocking had started again, halfhearted now, more bored than cruel. A group of boys on the rampart were throwing fig seeds at the marching Israelites, counting hits and keeping score.

Elena had been walking with her fists clenched for three days. The cold had burrowed deep into her bones, into the place where her patience had once lived — the small, grudging store of it that she'd built up over four adventures. Four times she'd watched God's plan unfold in ways that didn't make sense until the end. Four times she'd waited when every nerve in her body screamed to act.

She was done waiting.

A fig seed bounced off her head. The boys on the wall whooped.

Elena stepped out of the procession. She planted her feet and looked up at the wall, and her face was red and her eyes were blazing.

"HEY!" she shouted.

The word cracked across the silence like a whip. Heads turned — Israelites, priests, soldiers. The boys on the wall froze mid-throw. Joshua, thirty yards ahead, turned his iron gaze back down the column.

"Elena!" Eli lunged and grabbed her arm. "What are you doing?"

She yanked free. "Something! I'm doing SOMETHING! Because that's what somebody should be doing instead of walking in circles like a bunch of —"

"Stop." Eli stepped in front of her. He put both hands on her shoulders and held on. "You have to stop."

"WHY?" The word came out so loud that nearby soldiers flinched. Elena's eyes were bright and fierce, and her voice cracked at the edges. "Why, Eli? Tell me why. Tell me one good reason we're doing this. Because I've been walking for six days and my feet hurt and I'm freezing cold and those kids up there are throwing fruit at us and NOTHING IS HAPPENING."

Eli held her shoulders. He could feel her trembling — not with cold this time, but with something deeper. Frustration and fear and the terrible weight of trusting a plan that looked like nonsense.

"The plan isn't ours," he said quietly.

"I know the plan isn't ours! You think I don't know that? You think I haven't sat through every single one of these waiting games? Moses at the bush. Noah on the boat. Joseph in the pit. I sat and watched and trusted and waited and it ALWAYS works out but it always takes FOREVER and I can't —" Her voice broke. She pressed the heels of her hands against her eyes. "I can't just keep walking, Eli. I can't."

"You can."

"You don't know what it feels like."

"I know exactly what it feels like." His voice was low and steady, the way it got when he was most sure of something. "I froze in front of Moses. Remember? I couldn't move. I couldn't think. I had to learn that trusting doesn't mean having answers. You have to learn that trusting doesn't always mean doing."

Elena dropped her hands. Her eyes were red. "That's easy for you. You're the thinker. You like waiting. You like figuring things out."

"I hate waiting. But that's not the point."

"Then what IS the point?"

"The point is that sometimes the hardest thing God asks isn't the big thing. It's the boring thing. It's the walk-around-the-wall-again thing. And doing it anyway is —"

"Don't say it's brave. Don't you dare call walking in a circle brave."

"It is, though."

They stared at each other. Brother and sister, standing in the dust outside an ancient city, the procession flowing around them like water around stones. The Fade pressed in from every direction, draining the light, muffling the sound, turning the world grey at the edges.

Elena's chin trembled. "I hate this," she whispered.

"I know."

"I really, really hate this."

"I know."

Jordan appeared beside them. He didn't say anything. He just stood there, hands in pockets, a quiet presence.

Elena wiped her eyes with the back of her hand. She looked at the wall. She looked at the marching column. She looked at Eli.

"Fine," she said. Her voice was rough and small. "One more day."

She stepped back into the procession. Her shoulders were stiff. Her jaw was set. She didn't look at the boys on the wall.

She walked.

Jordan watched her go, then looked at Eli. "For what it's worth," he said quietly, "she's not wrong that it's hard. And you're not wrong that it matters." He paused. "I just wish I understood why."

Eli watched his sister's stiff back disappear into the column of marchers. The Fade curled around the procession like a cat around a sleeping bird.

"Tomorrow," Eli said. "Whatever happens, it happens tomorrow."

## Chapter 6: The Seventh Day

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 18, 2019 — Tell es-Sultan, Jericho*
*The seventh day. Seven circuits. By my calculations, that's more than eight hours of continuous marching. And then the shout. I've been thinking about what it must have felt like — that moment when the trumpet sounds the long blast and Joshua finally gives the order to raise your voice. Six days of silence. Six days of swallowed words and clenched jaws and feet aching in the dust. And then permission to release everything you've been holding. I think the shout wasn't a weapon. It was a declaration. It was every silent step converted into sound.*

---

They rose before dawn on the seventh day.

The camp was different. Eli could feel it the moment he stepped out of the tent — a tension in the air, like the sky before a thunderstorm. The Fade was everywhere. Not fog exactly, but a wrongness that infected the light itself, turning the predawn glow a sickly yellow-grey. Colors had leached from the camp overnight. The red banners looked brown. The brass fittings on the Ark's carrying poles had gone dull.

Elena stood outside the tent with her arms wrapped tight around herself. She wasn't shivering anymore. She was past shivering. The cold had settled into her like a second skeleton, something she carried inside her bones.

"Ready?" Eli asked.

She didn't answer. She just started walking toward the assembly point.

Jordan fell into step beside Eli. His glasses sat crooked on his nose, and he kept tilting his head, trying to find an angle where the blurred edges cleared. "This is the worst it's been," he said quietly. "My peripheral vision is basically gone. Everything past about twenty degrees is just... grey."

"It's thicker than any time before," Eli agreed. The hum in his chest had become a permanent thing, a vibration he couldn't shake, couldn't tune out. Like standing next to a machine that never turned off.

The procession assembled for the seventh time. But today was different. Today the armed men stood straighter. The priests gripped their ram's horns with white-knuckled hands. Joshua walked the length of the column, his face carved from granite, and spoke three words that silenced every whisper.

"Seven times today."

A murmur ran through the ranks. Seven circuits. Not one. Seven.

Joshua raised his hand. "March. In silence. Until I give the command."

They marched.

Dawn broke while they walked, the sun climbing over the eastern ridge and pouring pale, watery light across the plain. Even the sunrise looked wrong — filtered through the Fade's haze, it was the color of old parchment instead of gold.

The first circuit felt endless. The wall loomed above them, oppressive, immovable. The people of Jericho lined the ramparts in force — the entire city seemed to have turned out to watch. But the mocking was muted today. Something in the air had changed, and even behind their massive walls, the people of Jericho could feel it. They watched in uneasy silence as the procession passed below.

Second circuit. Eli's legs burned. The dust caked his throat.

Third circuit. Elena walked like she was made of iron — stiff, mechanical, jaw locked so tight Eli worried about her teeth.

Fourth circuit. The Fade pressed closer. The hum in Eli's chest was so loud it buzzed in his teeth. The sky was the color of dishwater. The walls looked less like stone and more like shadows — dark masses against a colorless sky.

Jordan stumbled. Eli caught his arm.

"Can't see," Jordan muttered. "Edges are gone. It's like looking through a tunnel."

"Stay close."

Fifth circuit. Soldiers in the column were swaying. The whispers had stopped — not because the doubt was gone, but because the doubt was so thick that words couldn't carry it anymore. It sat in people's chests like stones.

Sixth circuit. Elena was crying. Silently, jaw clenched, tears cutting tracks through the dust on her face. She didn't wipe them. She didn't stop walking. She put one foot in front of the other like each step was a war.

Seventh circuit. The world had gone grey. The Fade had drained nearly everything — the tents in the distance were colorless, the soldiers' armor was dull as old tin, even the golden Ark seemed to have lost its shine. The hum in Eli's chest was deafening.

Elena's feet were bleeding. She could feel the blisters broken and raw inside her sandals, each step a small fire. Her legs shook. Her arms were numb from the cold. She counted her steps — one, two, three, four — because counting was something to do, and doing something, anything, was the only way she knew how to survive.

*One more step. Just one more.*

She'd been saying that since the fifth circuit. Hundreds of steps ago. And she was still walking.

Then Joshua stopped.

The column halted. Thousands of people, frozen in the dust, in the silence, in the grey nothing of a morning that shouldn't have been so dark.

Joshua turned to face the city. He drew a breath that seemed to pull the air from the whole valley.

And the priests raised their horns.

The sound was unlike anything Eli had heard. It wasn't a trumpet blast. It wasn't music. It was a roar — a deep, bone-rattling roar that rose from seven curved horns and merged into a single note that vibrated the ground under their feet. The sound climbed and swelled and filled the grey sky until the air itself seemed to ring like a bell.

The Fade shuddered. Eli felt it — a tremor in the wrongness, a flinch in the fog.

Joshua's voice split the morning:

"SHOUT! FOR THE LORD HAS GIVEN YOU THE CITY!"

And the silence broke.

Every voice. Every soldier, every priest, every woman, every child, every person who had walked in silence for seven days opened their mouth and screamed. Not a battle cry. Not a command. A release — raw and ragged and enormous, six days of swallowed doubt and clenched jaws and aching feet and WHY ARE WE WALKING converted into pure, thundering sound.

Elena screamed. Her voice cracked and she screamed again, louder, all the frustration and the cold and the fear and the walking, all of it, pouring out of her in a sound that was not words but was truer than words.

Eli shouted. Jordan shouted. The ground shook.

And the walls of Jericho fell.

## Chapter 7: The Horn

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*March 19, 2019 — Tell es-Sultan, Jericho*
*I found a crack in the foundation today that runs the full width of the excavation trench. The collapse pattern is wrong for an earthquake — the stones fell outward, not inward. Dr. Feldman has no explanation. Neither do I, if I'm being honest with my archaeology. But I have one if I'm being honest with my faith. Some walls aren't brought down by force. They're brought down by obedience. And obedience that doesn't make sense is the hardest kind there is. I think that's the point. If it made sense, it wouldn't require trust.*

---

The sound came first — a crack so deep it seemed to come from inside the earth. Not a snap. Not a crumble. A splitting, like the planet itself had broken a bone.

Then the wall moved.

Not crumbled. Not tilted. The wall *fell* — the entire face of it dropping outward like a door thrown open, massive blocks of stone tumbling down the slope in a cascade of dust and thunder. The ground heaved. Eli grabbed Elena, Elena grabbed Jordan, and the three of them staggered backward as the earth bucked under their feet.

The noise was beyond anything Eli could have imagined. Stone struck stone in a chain reaction that raced along the perimeter — section after section collapsing outward, each fall triggering the next, the walls peeling open like the rind of a fruit split by the sun. Blocks the size of wagons bounced and rolled down the slope. The dust cloud rose fifty feet, a hundred feet, swallowing the sky.

Dust exploded upward in a wall of its own — brown and thick, blotting out the sun. The sound was enormous, so vast it stopped being sound and became a physical thing, a pressure against the chest, a vibration in the bones.

And through it all, the Fade shattered.

Eli saw it. The grey fog that had cloaked the world for two days tore apart like wet paper. Color slammed back into the landscape — the sky went blue, fierce and deep. The dust cloud turned golden in the sudden sunlight. The soldiers' armor blazed. The banners snapped red and clean in the wind. The wrongness, the hum, the muffled silence — gone. Burned away in an instant, like frost on a window hit by morning sun.

Elena was staring at the fallen wall. Tears still tracked through the dust on her face, but her mouth was open and her eyes were wide and she wasn't shivering anymore. She was warm. The cold that had lived in her bones for six days had vanished with the Fade, and the heat of the desert sun hit her like an embrace.

"It fell," she whispered. "It actually fell."

The Israelite army surged forward. Soldiers poured over the rubble, scrambling across the massive stones, flooding into the city through the gap where the wall had stood. The rams' horns kept blowing — not the single sustained note now but a series of sharp, triumphant blasts that echoed off the remaining walls and bounced across the valley.

Jordan stood very still. His vision had cleared — the grey edges were gone, the tunnel had opened back into full, vivid sight. He looked at the rubble, at the soldiers, at the priests with their horns, and his mouth moved but nothing came out.

"Jordan?" Eli said.

Jordan shook his head slowly. "I had... I had a whole speech prepared. About resonant frequencies and structural failure and how maybe there's a scientific explanation." He looked at Eli. His face was completely open, unguarded — the expression he got when something moved him too deeply for sarcasm. "I don't think there's a scientific explanation."

"No," Eli said.

"Okay." Jordan took a breath. "Okay."

Elena was already moving. The shivering girl from five minutes ago was gone. The girl who had cried through the sixth circuit, who had counted her bleeding steps through the seventh — gone. She was Elena again, the spark, the doer, but different now. Tempered. The fire was the same but the fuel had changed.

"There." She pointed toward the rubble field, where the priests were climbing through the wreckage, horns still raised. "The relic. The horn. It has to be one of those horns."

They scrambled over the fallen stones. The blocks were massive — each one taller than Eli, scattered across the slope like a giant's toys. The kids climbed and jumped and squeezed between them, following the priests.

One of the seven priests had stopped. He was an old man, lean and sun-dark, and he held his ram's horn in both hands. The horn had cracked. A long split ran from the bell to the mouthpiece, and as Eli watched, a piece of the horn's rim broke free and fell to the stone at the priest's feet.

The piece glowed.

Not bright. Not blinding. The same warm, amber glow Eli had seen in the streambed at Elah, in the branch from the dove, in the thread from Joseph's coat, in the splinter from Moses' staff. A light that came from inside the object, from the meaning held within it.

The old priest looked down at the glowing shard. His eyes widened. He looked at Eli — looked straight at him, the first person in this entire adventure to truly see any of them — and his face softened into something like recognition.

He stepped back. His lips moved — not words Eli could hear, but the shape of them looked like a prayer. Or a blessing. Or a farewell to something sacred that had served its purpose.

Elena reached the shard first. She crouched on the rubble, dust settling in her hair, and stared at the piece of ram's horn glowing on the stone. It was small — no bigger than her palm, curved and smooth, warm-colored bone with a light inside it that pulsed in time with something she could feel but not hear.

"I get it now," she said.

Eli knelt beside her. "Yeah?"

Elena picked up the shard. The glow spread through her fingers, warm and steady.

"The waiting wasn't nothing," she said. Her voice was quiet — quieter than Eli had ever heard her. "It felt like nothing. It felt like we were wasting time while people laughed at us. But every step mattered. Every silent lap around this wall was part of the plan. And if anyone had broken rank — if anyone had shouted early or stopped walking or tried to do it their own way —"

"It wouldn't have worked," Jordan finished.

Elena nodded. The glow in her hand brightened. "Patience wasn't giving up. It wasn't being passive. It was the hardest thing I've ever done." She looked at Eli. Her eyes were still red, but clear. "It was the bravest thing I've ever done."

The hum rose. Not from Eli's chest this time — from everywhere. From the fallen stones and the clear sky and the piece of horn in Elena's hand. The four-note chord from the Pathfinder joined it, and together they climbed toward a fifth note that had been missing, a gap in the melody that had been waiting to be filled.

The glow expanded. Golden light poured from the shard, washing over the rubble, the soldiers, the ruined walls, turning everything bright and warm and present. Elena felt the horn dissolving in her hand — the edges softening, the weight melting away like ice in a warm palm — and she held on until there was nothing left but light between her fingers.

"Hold on," Eli said. He reached for Elena. Jordan pressed close.

The world went golden. And they went home.

## Chapter 8: The Fifth Note

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*December 1, 2020 — Hospital, room 412*
*The nurse let me have my notebook today. Small victory. I've been thinking about obedience — real obedience, the kind that costs something. Not the easy kind where God's instructions make sense and the path is clear. The hard kind. The kind where you walk in circles for a week while people mock you and nothing visible changes. I think that's where faith lives — not in the miracle but in the march. Not in the shout but in the silence. Elena would hate hearing that. But I think she might be the one who understands it best, someday.*

---

Old books and cinnamon.

Elena blinked. The study materialized around her — maps on the walls, journals in columns, late afternoon light through the single window. Everything exactly as they'd left it. The same moment. The same dust motes floating in the air.

Her hand was empty. The horn shard was gone. She could still feel the warmth of it, like holding a cup of hot chocolate on a winter day — the memory of the heat lingering after the source was gone.

The Pathfinder sat on the desk.

Elena looked at it before the others did. Five symbols now — five marks in a circle of twelve. The first four were sharp and glowing, the way she'd seen them before: the amber stone, the green branch, the crimson thread, the brown staff. Each one polished, precise, filled with color like stained glass.

As she watched, the fifth symbol transformed.

The rough scratch of the horn — barely a suggestion in the bronze before — sharpened. Lines cut themselves into the metal as if carved by an invisible hand. The surface polished smooth, and a deep golden color flooded the engraving, rich and warm as the desert sun. A faint glow settled into it, steady and permanent.

The Pathfinder hummed. Five notes now. Not a chord — a melody. The first five notes of a song that wasn't finished, climbing and reaching toward something bigger, something that needed seven more notes to complete.

"Five," Eli said softly.

"Seven to go," Jordan said. He was staring at the Pathfinder with an expression that had nothing guarded in it. Pure curiosity. Pure attention. "It's building something. The notes, the symbols — it's not random. It's a sequence."

"A song," Eli said.

"Maybe. Or a map. Or both." Jordan caught himself and pulled back slightly, crossing his arms, but the gesture was habit now, not armor. "I mean, I'm not saying I know what it is. I'm saying I can't explain it."

Elena was quiet.

Both boys looked at her. Elena, who was never quiet. Elena, who always had the first word and the last word and most of the words in between. She stood with her empty hand at her side and looked at the Pathfinder's five glowing symbols.

"I hate waiting," she said.

Eli nodded. "I know."

"I hate it more than anything. More than losing. More than being wrong. More than that time Jordan ate the last granola bar and blamed the raccoons."

"In my defense," Jordan started.

"But I think —" Elena stopped. She pressed her lips together, and for a moment she looked not like the girl who charged into everything, but like someone standing at the edge of a cliff, deciding whether to jump. "I think that's the point. It's supposed to be hard. If it wasn't hard, it wouldn't be trust. It would just be... following directions."

Eli put his hand on her shoulder. He didn't say anything. There was nothing to add.

Elena reached out and touched the Pathfinder. The five notes pulsed under her fingertip, warm and alive. She could feel the seven empty spaces around the circle — seven rough, faint engravings waiting to be filled. Seven more promises. Seven more walks into the impossible.

The room was quiet for a moment. The kind of quiet that follows something important — not empty, but full. Holding something.

"It's different now," Jordan said. He was looking out the study window at the ordinary street, the ordinary evening. "The detection thing. Elena's cold, Eli's hum, my vision — we felt it here. In the present. Before we even left."

Eli's stomach tightened. Jordan was right. They'd felt the Fade before the Pathfinder activated. Before they traveled. That had never happened in the first four adventures.

"It's getting stronger," Eli said.

"Or closer," Jordan said.

"What's the difference?"

Jordan looked at him. "I don't know. But I think there is one."

Elena pulled her hand back from the Pathfinder. She looked at her brother, then at Jordan, then at the seven empty spaces in the circle.

"Whatever's coming," she said, and a little of the old fire crept back into her voice, "we've got five already. It's had four shots at us and we're still here."

"Five shots," Jordan corrected. "It came early this time."

"Right." Elena's jaw set. The familiar half-grin — the one that dared the world to keep up — flickered at the corner of her mouth. "Five shots and we're still here. So let it come."

The Pathfinder hummed its five-note melody in the quiet room. Outside, the summer evening settled over Abuelo's house, ordinary and warm. The wind chimes made of old keys clinked softly on the porch. Somewhere down the street, a dog barked.

But in the study, the air felt different than it had an hour ago. Charged. Alert. Like the house itself knew that something had shifted — that the easy part was over and what came next would test them in ways the first four adventures hadn't.

Eli picked up Abuelo's journal from the desk. He opened it to the next marked page and found a sketch: a circular seal with a lion engraved in its center. Below it, in Abuelo's tight handwriting: *Daniel 6. The lion's den. A seal that couldn't be broken — except by faithfulness.*

He closed the journal and looked at the Pathfinder. Five notes, humming. Seven spaces, waiting.

Elena looked at the sketch. A lion. A seal. A den.

"Next one's going to be harder, isn't it?" she said.

"They all are now," Eli said.

Elena squared her shoulders. The half-grin widened. "Good."

The escalation had begun.
