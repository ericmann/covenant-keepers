## Chapter 1: The Vizier's Shadow

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 9, 2017 — Avaris, Tell el-Dab'a, Egypt*
*The excavation is slower than anyone wants. Sand and heat and the usual bureaucratic tangles. But the pottery fragments from Stratum D/2 are remarkable — Canaanite forms in an Egyptian context. A Semitic household in the heart of the Nile Delta, and a wealthy one at that. Someone from Canaan rose very high in this place. The local team keeps calling him "the foreign vizier," and I keep finding myself thinking about a boy with a colorful coat and a family that threw him away. Archaeology gives you bones and sherds and foundations. But sometimes the silence between the artifacts tells you more than the objects themselves. Something terrible happened to that boy. And something extraordinary happened after.*

---

The journal was the third one from the left, second shelf from the bottom.

Eli had been looking for it all morning. Abuelo's study had its own kind of logic — field notebooks organized by year, leather spines labeled in faded ink, stacked in columns on the long table and shoved into every available shelf space. But the logic only made sense if you were Abuelo.

Elena sat cross-legged on the floor, surrounded by maps she'd pulled from a drawer. She wasn't reading them. She was arranging them by size like puzzle pieces.

"Find anything?" she asked without looking up.

"Maybe." Eli opened the journal carefully. The binding cracked. A pressed flower fell out — something small and brown that might have been yellow once. He turned past pages of sketches and soil charts until he found the entry. Abuelo's tight handwriting, slanting to the right the way it always did when he was excited. Avaris. Tell el-Dab'a. A foreign vizier.

The front door banged.

"It's just me," Jordan called from the hallway. His footsteps came up the stairs, and then he was in the doorway of the study, hoodie pulled up despite the July heat. He leaned against the frame and crossed his arms. "So. Are we doing this again or what?"

"Doing what?" Elena said, grinning at him.

"You know what." Jordan pushed his glasses up. "The whole impossible-time-travel-into-ancient-history thing. Because the last two times were, I mean, they were —"

"Amazing?" Elena offered.

"I was going to say deeply concerning. But sure."

Eli set the journal down and looked at the Pathfinder. It sat on its leather cloth on the desk, bronze and heavy and patient. Two symbols glowed faintly in the afternoon light — the stone and the branch, sharp and polished, each filled with color like enamel inlay. The other ten remained rough scratches, barely visible. Waiting.

The hum started low.

Eli felt it first in his chest, the way he always did — that resonant vibration just below hearing, like a note played on a string too thick to make a sound you could name. It rose slowly. The two completed symbols brightened, pulsing in rhythm with something that felt like a heartbeat.

Elena's head snapped up. "It's warm."

"It's activating," Eli said.

Jordan took a half step back. "Already? We didn't even —"

The light came.

Not from the window. Not from the lamp on the desk. From the Pathfinder itself, rising from the bronze like heat from summer pavement, golden and total, flooding the small room until the maps and shelves and journals dissolved into brightness. Eli felt Elena grab his wrist. He heard Jordan say something sharp and short. The hum climbed until it wasn't a hum anymore but two clear notes ringing together — the stone's note and the branch's note — harmonizing into something that vibrated in Eli's teeth.

Then the study was gone.

---

Heat. Not the dry heat of the Valley of Elah or the heavy heat before the flood. This was a different kind — open, grassy, with a wind that smelled like sheep and wild herbs and cook smoke from somewhere nearby.

Eli opened his eyes.

They stood on a hillside. The land rolled away below them in long, gentle slopes of brown and green — scrubby pastureland dotted with clusters of dark-leafed trees. In the distance, a camp of goatskin tents sat in the shade of a wide oak. Sheep moved in a loose herd on the far slope, white against the brown grass.

Their clothes had changed. Rough linen tunics again, belted at the waist. Elena's hair was covered. Jordan looked down at himself and sighed.

"Burlap. Again."

"It's linen," Eli said.

"It feels like burlap."

Elena was already scanning the landscape, her eyes quick and sharp. "Where are we? This doesn't look like Egypt."

"It's not," Eli said slowly. He recognized the terrain from Abuelo's photographs — the rolling hills, the scattered oaks, the limestone outcrops breaking through the thin soil. "I think this is Canaan. The hill country."

From the camp below, voices rose — sharp, angry voices, the sound of an argument carried uphill by the wind. Then a different voice, younger, cut through the others. Not arguing. Telling.

Elena looked at Eli. "Come on."

They moved down the slope toward the tents. As they got closer, Eli counted the figures. A lot of them — young men, mostly, rangy and weather-darkened from outdoor work. Brothers. He could tell by the way they argued. Only family fought like that — with the kind of anger that comes from knowing exactly where to push.

And standing apart from the group, talking to an older man whose back was turned, was a boy.

He looked about seventeen. Taller than some of his brothers, leaner than others. His hair was dark and longer than theirs, and he stood differently — straighter, more certain, the way someone stands when they know they're about to say something important.

The older man turned, and Eli saw his face — lined and weathered, with a long grey beard and eyes that were tired but kind. The man put his hand on the boy's shoulder. The boy smiled.

Not one of the brothers smiled back.

"That's Joseph," Eli whispered. "And that's Jacob. His father."

Jordan was watching the brothers. His face had gone still. "They hate him," he said quietly. "Look at their faces."

Eli looked. Jordan was right. It wasn't just annoyance or sibling rivalry. It was something uglier — a resentment that had curdled into something cold and fixed. The brothers watched Joseph and their father with eyes that had stopped seeing a brother a long time ago.

Elena shivered. "This isn't going to end well."

## Chapter 2: The Coat

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 12, 2017 — Avaris, Tell el-Dab'a, Egypt*
*Dr. Nassar showed me a textile fragment today — a scrap of woven fabric, multicolored, the dyes still faintly visible after millennia. Red and blue and yellow, interlocking patterns. A luxury item. Something you'd give to someone you wanted to honor. Or someone you loved more than you should have shown. I held it under the light and thought about what a gift like that costs — not in money, but in the jealousy it creates. A beautiful coat given to a favorite son. How many family wounds start with something that was meant to be love?*

---

It happened the next morning.

The kids had slept at the edge of the camp, near a storage tent where no one paid them any attention. The Pathfinder's effect held — the three of them moved through the camp like they belonged there. Part of the household, unremarkable, invisible the way children are invisible when adults have bigger things on their minds.

Eli woke to shouting.

Not the angry kind from the day before. This was different — excited, almost festive. He shook Elena awake and found Jordan already sitting up, squinting toward the center of camp.

Jacob stood outside his tent. In his arms, draped over both hands like something precious, was a coat.

Even from a distance, Eli could see the colors. Red and blue and deep yellow, woven in intricate patterns that caught the early sunlight and threw it back in flashes. The fabric was finer than anything else in the camp — smoother, brighter, the kind of thing that didn't belong in a shepherd's household. It looked like something for a prince.

Jacob called Joseph's name.

The boy came out of his tent, rubbing sleep from his eyes. He saw the coat. His face opened — not with greed or pride, but with the simple, startled joy of a son who knows his father loves him. He reached out and touched the fabric.

Jacob set the coat on Joseph's shoulders.

It fit him like it had been made for him — which, Eli realized, it had. The colors blazed against the brown tents and brown grass and brown hills. Joseph turned slowly, feeling the weight of it, and smiled at his father.

"Gracias," Eli heard himself whisper. The word came out in Spanish without thinking, the way it did when something hit him deeper than English could reach.

"It's beautiful," Elena said.

It was. And it was a disaster.

Jordan saw it first. He was watching the brothers, not Joseph. "Look," he said.

The brothers had gathered near the cook fire. They weren't watching their father or their brother. They were looking at each other. And the looks that passed between them were the most unsettling thing Eli had seen since they'd arrived — worse than the open anger from the day before. This was colder. This was the moment when resentment stopped being a feeling and became a decision.

One of the older brothers — tall, broad-shouldered, with a hard jaw — spat into the fire. Another turned away and walked toward the sheep without a word. A third stared at Joseph with an expression so flat and empty it made Eli's stomach drop.

"He doesn't see it," Jordan said. His voice was low, careful. "Joseph. He's standing there in that coat and he doesn't see what's happening."

"Maybe he sees it and doesn't care," Elena said.

"No. He doesn't see it. Look at his face."

Eli looked. Jordan was right again. Joseph was holding the edge of the coat up to the light, turning it, watching the colors shift. He was happy. Just happy. A kid with a gift from his father, not thinking about what it cost.

The cold crept in around the edges of the morning.

Elena rubbed her arms. "Why is it — it was warm a second ago."

Eli felt the hum. Low and steady, a pressure in his chest that had nothing to do with the altitude or the thin air. He looked toward the hills. At the far edge of his vision, where the pastureland met the rocky ground, the light looked thinner. Flatter. Like someone had taken an eraser to the horizon and smudged the color out.

Jordan blinked hard and adjusted his glasses. "Is it just me, or does everything look a little —"

"Grey," Eli said. "At the edges."

"Yeah."

They stood in silence and watched Joseph walk through the camp in his coat of many colors, bright as a flame in a room full of people who wanted to put it out.

That evening, Joseph told his brothers about a dream.

The kids were close enough to hear — sitting on the ground behind a tent, eating flatbread and dried figs that someone had left near the communting fire. Joseph's voice carried easily. He wasn't whispering. He wasn't bragging, either, but he wasn't being careful, and with this audience, careful was the only thing that might have helped.

"I dreamed we were binding sheaves of grain in the field," Joseph said. "And my sheaf rose up and stood straight, and all of your sheaves gathered around mine and bowed down."

Silence.

A terrible, loaded silence that stretched across the firelight like a wire pulled tight.

"Your sheaf," the broad-shouldered brother said. "Bowed down to yours."

"I'm just telling you what I saw," Joseph said. And Eli could hear it — the honesty in his voice, the guilelessness. Joseph wasn't gloating. He was sharing something that felt true, the way Eli might tell Elena about a dream that seemed important.

But truth and timing are different things. And this was the worst possible time.

The brothers didn't explode. That was what made it worse. They just looked at each other across the fire with those cold, decided eyes, and the oldest one stood up and walked away without a word.

Joseph sat by the fire, alone in his beautiful coat, and didn't understand what he'd just done.

## Chapter 3: The Pit

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 3, 2018 — Home office, late*
*I've been reading Genesis 37 again, and the detail that stops me every time is verse 25: "Then they sat down to eat." After they stripped their brother's coat. After they threw him into a pit. After he screamed for help — the text doesn't say he screamed, but of course he screamed. After all of that, they sat down and ate lunch. I've studied violence and cruelty across a dozen ancient civilizations, and nothing chills me like the ordinariness of that sentence. The banality of betrayal. They didn't rage. They didn't agonize. They ate. How does guilt disappear that fast? What kind of cold makes people forget what they just did to someone they grew up with?*

---

Three days later, Jacob sent Joseph to check on his brothers.

They'd taken the flocks to graze near Shechem, and Jacob wanted to know if the pasture was good and the sheep were healthy. Joseph left camp wearing the coat — of course he was wearing the coat — and the kids followed at a distance, moving through the scrubby hills in a loose line.

Elena walked fast, the way she always did, but her mouth was tight. "We know what happens next, right?"

"Yes," Eli said.

"And we can't do anything about it."

"The story happens how the story happens."

"That's a terrible rule." She kicked a stone hard enough to send it skipping down the slope. "I hate that rule."

Jordan said nothing. He walked with his hands shoved deep in his pockets, his eyes fixed on Joseph's bright figure moving through the brown landscape ahead of them. The rubber band on his wrist was stretched tight between his fingers.

The brothers saw Joseph coming from a long way off. The coat made him visible — a splash of color against the dull hills, impossible to miss. Like a signal fire.

The kids watched from behind a limestone outcrop as the brothers gathered. Eli couldn't hear their words, but he could read their bodies — the way they leaned in toward each other, the sharp gestures, the quick nods. A plan forming in real time. One of them pointed at Joseph. Another made a cutting motion with his hand.

"Reuben," Eli whispered, watching the oldest brother. "He tries to stop them. He tells them not to kill him."

"Not to *kill* him?" Elena's voice climbed. "That's the best he can do?"

"He suggests the pit instead. To buy time. He plans to come back for Joseph later."

"Does he?"

Eli didn't answer. The story was already moving.

Joseph reached his brothers with a wave and a smile. For one second, the scene looked ordinary — a younger brother catching up to the group, ready to help, glad to see them.

Then the closest brother grabbed the coat.

He didn't ask for it. Didn't threaten. Just seized the fabric at the shoulder and yanked. Joseph stumbled. Another brother caught his arm. A third grabbed the other side of the coat and pulled. The fabric tore with a sound that Eli felt in his chest — a ripping, a shredding, threads separating from threads.

Joseph tried to speak. "What are you — wait, I —"

They didn't wait.

They stripped the coat from his body in rough, jerking pulls, tearing it at the seams. Joseph fought them — not with fists, not with weapons, but the way a person fights when the thing they love is being destroyed, reaching for the pieces, trying to hold on. His brothers were bigger. Stronger. There were ten of them.

The coat came off in shreds.

Joseph stood barefoot in the dirt, his under-tunic torn, his face white with shock. He looked at his brothers — each one, face by face — searching for something. An explanation. A reason. Mercy.

He didn't find it.

They dragged him to the pit.

It was a dry cistern — a hole cut into the limestone, deep and narrow, with smooth sides that offered no grip. Joseph screamed as they lowered him. Not words. Just the raw sound of someone who can't believe what's happening, who keeps expecting this to stop, who is waiting for the moment when his brothers remember who he is and pull him back up.

The moment didn't come.

They dropped him into the dark.

Elena was crying. She'd turned away, her face pressed against the outcrop, her shoulders shaking. She didn't make any sound.

Jordan stood perfectly still. His glasses reflected the bright sky. His hands were at his sides, the rubber band motionless. He watched the brothers walk away from the pit — walk away from their brother's voice calling up to them from the dark — and his face did something Eli had never seen before. It closed. Every expression left it at once, like a door shutting.

"Jordan?" Eli said.

Jordan didn't answer.

The brothers sat down in the shade of an oak tree. Someone pulled bread from a bag. Someone else uncorked a skin of water and drank. They ate. Thirty feet from a pit where their brother was crying for help, they ate lunch.

And the cold rolled in.

Not gradually this time. It came like a wave, sudden and heavy, pressing down on the hillside. The colors leached from the grass, the sky, the rocks. The air turned thick and numb. Joseph's voice, calling from the pit, seemed to thin — not quieter, but emptier, as if the words were losing their meaning before they reached anyone's ears. The brothers' faces went slack. Not guilty, not defiant — just blank. As if they'd already forgotten what they'd done. As if the memory was being drained out of them while they chewed.

Elena gripped Eli's arm. Her fingers were ice.

"They don't even care," she whispered. "They just *don't care*."

Eli looked at the pit. Joseph's voice had gone quieter now — ragged, tired, still calling. The hum in Eli's chest had turned sharp and wrong, like a note bent out of tune. He wanted to run down the hill and pull Joseph out. He wanted to scream at the brothers until they remembered they were supposed to love him.

He couldn't do either.

Jordan finally spoke. His voice was flat and distant. "My dad left on a Tuesday. He packed two bags and put them in the trunk and told me he'd call when he got settled." He paused. "He didn't call for three weeks."

Eli looked at him. Jordan wasn't looking back. He was staring at the brothers eating their lunch, and his eyes were dry and far away.

"He kept eating," Jordan said. "Not right then. But after. He just went on with his life. Like we were — like I was just a thing that happened to him, and now it was over."

Nobody spoke. The wind moved through the dry grass. From the pit, Joseph's voice had gone silent.

## Chapter 4: Sold

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*October 5, 2018 — Home office*
*The Midianite trade routes ran from Gilead to Egypt — a well-established caravan corridor. Ishmaelite, Midianite — the text uses both terms, which suggests a mixed caravan, common for the period. Twenty pieces of silver. The going rate for a young male slave. His brothers knew the price. They'd thought about this before today. What breaks me is the coat. After the sale, they took what was left of it and dipped it in goat's blood. They carried it home to their father and said, "We found this. Examine it. Is it your son's coat?" They didn't even say his name.*

---

The caravan appeared on the eastern road an hour later.

Eli saw the dust first — a tan smudge rising above the hills, spreading in the still air. Then the sound: the creak of wooden wheels, the clink of harness bells, the low calls of men driving loaded camels. Midianite traders, coming down from Gilead, heading south toward Egypt.

The brothers saw the caravan too.

Judah stood up from the oak's shade and looked from the approaching traders to the pit where Joseph had gone silent. Something passed across his face — not guilt, not mercy, but calculation. A deal taking shape.

"What good is it if we kill him?" Judah said to the others. "Let's sell him to these traders. After all, he's our brother."

Even the mercy was ugly. *Let's not kill him. Let's profit from him instead.* The brothers nodded like it was reasonable. Like they were solving a problem instead of selling a person.

The caravan rolled closer. The traders were dark-faced men with weathered hands and shrewd eyes, riding camels loaded with spices and balm and myrrh — luxury goods heading for Egyptian markets. They stopped when the brothers flagged them down, and the bartering was quick and businesslike.

Twenty pieces of silver.

Joseph came up from the pit blinking in the harsh sunlight, his face streaked with dirt and dried tears. He looked at the traders. He looked at his brothers. He looked at the silver changing hands. And Eli watched understanding hit him — not all at once, but in stages, like a series of doors closing. His brothers had sold him. His brothers. These men he'd grown up with, slept beside, worked alongside, shared meals and stories and a father.

Sold him.

They tied a rope around Joseph's wrists. He didn't fight. Eli thought that was the worst part — the way the fight had gone out of him, replaced by a stunned stillness that was harder to watch than any struggle. Joseph turned once, looking back at his brothers as the caravan began to move. His mouth opened.

No one met his eyes.

Elena was rigid beside Eli, her fists clenched so tight her knuckles had gone white. "Do something," she hissed. Not to Eli, not to anyone. Just the words themselves, forced through her teeth. "Someone do something."

No one did.

The caravan pulled away. Joseph walked among the camels and trade goods, a boy who had woken up that morning loved by his father and was going to sleep that night as property, headed for a country he'd never seen.

Jordan watched until Joseph was a speck on the road. Then he watched the brothers.

They moved with practiced efficiency. Two of them killed a goat — not one from the flock, a scrub goat from the brush — and slit the blood into a clay bowl. The oldest one gathered the torn pieces of Joseph's coat. The colors were still bright, even ripped and dirtied — red and blue and yellow, catching the light.

They dipped the coat in the blood.

Piece by piece, they pressed the fabric into the dark liquid until the colors drowned. Then they wrung it out and folded it carefully, like men handling laundry, and placed it in a sack.

"They're going to tell their father he's dead," Jordan said. His voice was very quiet and very precise, the way a person speaks when they're holding something fragile and don't want to drop it. "Their own father. They're going to stand in front of him and show him his son's bloody coat and watch him break."

"Jordan —" Eli started.

"And he'll believe them. Because why wouldn't he? They're his sons. You trust your family."

The words landed hard. Not because of what they said about Joseph's story. Because of what they said about Jordan's.

The hum changed.

Eli felt it before he heard it — a shift in the vibration, the two notes pulling apart and recombining in a way that felt like preparation. Like gathering breath before a leap. The Pathfinder was still miles and centuries away, sitting on Abuelo's desk, but the connection was real and immediate and insistent.

The air around them began to glow.

"It's pulling us forward," Eli said. He could feel the direction of it — not backward, not home, but *forward* in this same world. Ahead in time. The hills around them were already blurring, the outlines of the brothers and the camp going soft and indistinct.

"Where?" Elena said.

The light answered.

It rushed in from every direction — warm, golden, total — and the hills and the camp and the blood-soaked coat dissolved. In their place, slowly, like a painting emerging from fog: flat land. A wide river. Enormous buildings of white stone rising against a sky so blue it hurt. The smell of water and incense and something baking in an oven somewhere far below.

Egypt.

The light settled. The hum steadied. They were standing on a stone terrace overlooking a city that went on forever.

"Okay," Jordan said, blinking hard. "That's new."

## Chapter 5: The Vizier

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 15, 2017 — Avaris, Tell el-Dab'a, Egypt*
*We found the administrative seal today — stamped in clay, clear as the day it was pressed. A signet ring impression belonging to a high official, a vizier of the court. The inscription is damaged but the title is unmistakable: Overseer of the Granaries of Upper and Lower Egypt. This is someone who controlled the food supply of the entire kingdom. And the name, partially legible, is Semitic. Not Egyptian. A foreigner at the pinnacle of power. I sat in the trench with the clay tablet in my hands and did the math. From slave to prisoner to second in command of the most powerful nation on earth. What happened to this man in between? What kind of suffering builds that instead of destroying it?*

---

The city was Memphis. Eli was almost sure.

He recognized the layout from Abuelo's maps — the river cutting wide and silver through the flat Delta, the temple complexes with their massive stone columns, the administrative quarter rising on a low hill above the marketplaces. Years had passed. How many, Eli couldn't tell. The air felt different, not just the geography but the time — heavier, more established, like a world that had settled into its own weight.

"This is Egypt," Elena said. "Like, actual ancient Egypt."

"The Delta," Eli said. "Near the capital."

"How many years ahead are we?"

"Maybe twenty. Maybe more."

Jordan was staring at the city. Sunlight glanced off the stone buildings and the slow river and the irrigation channels that threaded through green fields like veins. Boats moved in the distance. People swarmed through the streets below in a constant, colorful press. The air smelled nothing like Canaan — it was thick with river water and lotus flowers and something sharp like natron salt.

"If this is twenty years later," Jordan said carefully, "then Joseph is —"

"Down there somewhere," Eli said. "If he survived."

"He survived." Eli said it with a certainty that came from knowing the story. But standing here, looking at this enormous, foreign, indifferent city, the certainty felt thin. A boy sold by his brothers, dragged to a country where he knew no one and nothing. The distance between that pit in Canaan and this gleaming capital was more than miles.

Elena turned in a slow circle, taking in the stone terrace, the painted columns, the carved reliefs of pharaohs and gods and processions of workers bearing grain. "This is the fanciest place we've been. By a lot."

"It's one of the wealthiest civilizations in the ancient world," Eli said.

"So if Joseph ended up here, as a slave, with nothing —"

"He had to start at the very bottom."

They found their way down from the terrace through a narrow stairway cut into the stone. The steps were worn smooth by centuries of feet, and the walls on either side were painted with faded images — birds, reeds, a man standing in a field of grain with his arms raised.

The streets were packed and loud — merchants calling from stalls, donkeys laden with grain pushing through the crowd, officials in white linen striding past with servants in their wake. A group of children chased a dog through an alley, laughing. An old woman sat in a doorway grinding grain between two flat stones, her hands moving in a rhythm so practiced it looked like breathing. The smell was different from Canaan — denser, sweeter, layered with river mud and perfume and the sharp tang of beer being brewed.

Nobody noticed three kids in rough tunics. The Pathfinder's effect held even here, even decades later. They moved through the crowd like water through fingers.

The administrative quarter was on the hill. Larger buildings here, with guards at the doors and scribes hurrying between offices, carrying scrolls and clay tablets. The whole area hummed with efficiency — the business of running an empire, organized and relentless.

And in the center of it, in a courtyard surrounded by columns of polished stone, stood a man giving orders.

He was tall — taller than most of the Egyptians around him. His head was shaved in the Egyptian style, and he wore fine linen and a collar of gold and lapis lazuli. A signet ring gleamed on his right hand. His voice was calm, measured, the kind of voice that expected to be obeyed and usually was. Scribes flanked him, writing as he spoke. Soldiers stood at the courtyard's edges.

Eli studied his face. The lines of it. The jaw. The way he held himself — straight-backed, composed, controlled.

"That's Joseph," Eli breathed.

"No way," Elena said. She was staring. "He looks — he looks *Egyptian*."

He did. The shaved head, the clothes, the bearing — everything about him had been reshaped by twenty years in this country. He'd been sold as a slave and now he commanded the grain supply of the most powerful kingdom in the world. Pharaoh's right hand. Second only to the throne.

But Eli saw something the others might have missed. Joseph's composure was real, but it was also armor. The way he held his jaw — slightly tight, even when he was calm. The way his eyes moved — scanning, assessing, always aware of exits and angles. This was a man who had learned to survive by never being caught off guard again. The pit had taught him that. The years in prison after Potiphar's wife lied about him had taught him that. Every betrayal, every injustice, every year of waiting had been pressed into him like a blade being hammered into shape.

He wasn't broken. But he was marked.

"He's in charge of the food," Eli said quietly. "There's a famine coming — it's already started. Joseph interpreted Pharaoh's dream about seven years of plenty followed by seven years of famine. Pharaoh put him in charge of storing grain."

"So everyone comes to him for food," Jordan said.

"Everyone."

Jordan's eyes narrowed behind his glasses. "Including his brothers."

The cold touched Eli's neck. Just a brush — like a draft from a window left open. Elena's hands went to her arms. Jordan blinked and rubbed his eyes.

At the edges of the courtyard, the shadows seemed longer than they should have been. The sunlight on the polished stone looked a shade thinner. Not enough to notice if you weren't looking. But they were looking.

Something was here. Something that had followed them through the time jump or been waiting when they arrived. It crept along the margins of the bright Egyptian afternoon like ink spreading through water, patient and purposeful and aimed at the man in the center of the courtyard.

Joseph finished giving his orders. The scribes departed. The soldiers shifted. For one moment, Joseph stood alone between the columns, and Eli saw his face without the armor — tired, distant, the face of a man carrying something that twenty years of power hadn't made lighter.

Then the mask slid back into place, and the vizier of Egypt turned and walked into the shadows of the palace.

## Chapter 6: The Brothers Arrive

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*April 18, 2017 — Avaris, Tell el-Dab'a, Egypt*
*The famine records are consistent across sources. Seven lean years following seven years of abundance — the cycle shows up in Nile sediment cores, in harvest records from multiple dynasties. It was real. And if the famine was real, then the migration was real too. Families from Canaan streaming south into Egypt, desperate for grain. Imagine being one of those families. Imagine walking into the court of the most powerful man in the kingdom and not knowing he's the brother you sold into slavery twenty years ago. Now imagine being him. Looking down from that throne at the faces of the men who threw you in a hole and ate lunch while you screamed. What do you do with that kind of power over the people who destroyed you?*

---

They came on a Tuesday.

Eli didn't know how he knew it was Tuesday, but the rhythm of the grain distribution had a weekly cycle, and the largest crowds came on the second and fifth days. The courtyard was packed — families from the Delta, traders from the south, and foreigners. Lots of foreigners. The famine had spread beyond Egypt, and people came from every direction, crossing deserts and borders they wouldn't have crossed in better times. Eli could see it on their faces — the hollow look of people who had watched their fields turn to dust and their children grow thin.

The kids had spent days watching the grain distributions. Joseph ran them with cold efficiency — fair, organized, impersonal. He didn't linger. He didn't engage. He counted and measured and recorded and moved to the next supplicant. The mask never slipped. Sometimes, late in the day, Eli caught Joseph standing alone in the courtyard after the crowds had gone, staring at nothing. His hands would be still at his sides. His face would be blank. Then a scribe would approach with a question, and the vizier would snap back into place like a door pulled shut by a spring.

But the cold hadn't left. It hung at the edges of everything, thickening slowly. The shadows in the courtyard stayed too long. The stone columns looked greyer than they had on the first day. Something was here. It was patient.

Elena noticed the brothers first.

"Eli." She grabbed his arm and pointed. "Look."

A group of men stood at the back of the line. Ten of them. Road-worn and dusty, with the look of people who had traveled a long way and hadn't eaten well in longer. Their clothes were Canaanite — rougher than the Egyptian linens around them, patched and sun-bleached. They carried empty sacks for grain.

Eli's heart slammed.

He recognized them. Not their faces — twenty years had changed those, adding beards and lines and grey to their hair. But he recognized the way they stood together. The formation. The dynamic. The oldest leading, the youngest hanging back, the middle ones clustered in a tight knot. Brothers.

Joseph's brothers. Ten of them — Benjamin wasn't there. Joseph's full brother, the youngest, had been kept home by their father. These were the ones who had torn the coat and sold the boy and dipped the fabric in blood.

"They don't know it's him," Jordan said. He'd seen it too. "They don't recognize him."

"Of course they don't. He's Egyptian now. Shaved head, Egyptian clothes, speaks Egyptian. They're looking at the vizier of Egypt. They'd never think —"

"But he knows them."

Eli watched Joseph.

The brothers shuffled forward in the line. Joseph was seated on a raised stone platform, scribes on either side, soldiers at the perimeters. He processed each group with the same impersonal efficiency — eyes down, hand moving, voice flat.

Then the brothers reached the front.

They bowed. All ten of them, faces to the ground, the way everyone bowed before the vizier. Just like the dream of the sheaves. Eli felt his breath catch.

Joseph went still.

It was subtle — a pause so small that anyone who wasn't watching for it would have missed it. His hand stopped moving. His chin lifted. His eyes found the men on the ground before him, and something passed through those eyes like lightning through dark water — a flash of recognition so raw and immediate that Eli felt it in his own chest.

Joseph knew them.

His jaw tightened. The tendons in his neck stood out. His hand — the one with the signet ring — curled into a fist on the armrest of his chair.

And the cold surged.

It hit Eli like a wall. The temperature in the courtyard dropped ten degrees in a second. Elena gasped and wrapped her arms around herself. Jordan's vision blurred — Eli could tell because Jordan grabbed the nearest column and squeezed his eyes shut.

The shadows at the edges of the courtyard thickened. The stone under their feet felt like ice. The air went flat and dead, all the noise of the marketplace muffled down to a grey whisper. And in the center of it, Joseph sat on his throne with his fist clenched and his brother's faces at his feet, and something was pressing in on him — pressing against the composure, pressing against the years, pressing against the carefully built walls of his Egyptian life — whispering in the language of old wounds.

*They deserve this.*

Eli heard it. Not as words — the Fade didn't speak. But as a feeling, a current of thought that pushed through the cold air like a tide. A bitter, justified, undeniable truth wrapped in poison: these men had destroyed his life. They had ripped away his father's love and thrown him into darkness. They had sold him for the price of a slave and gone home and eaten dinner. They deserved whatever he gave them.

*Make them pay.*

Joseph's face was a mask of stone. He spoke — harsh, clipped Egyptian, translated by an interpreter though Eli knew Joseph understood every word of Hebrew his brothers spoke. He accused them of being spies. He demanded to know where they'd come from. He interrogated them about their family — how many brothers, was their father alive, was there a youngest son.

The brothers fell over themselves answering. Ten men who had once been strong enough to overpower their own brother now cowered before a stranger in gold. Their voices cracked. Their hands shook. They named their family, their home, their father. They told this Egyptian vizier things they'd never told anyone — laying their whole lives bare on the courtyard stones, desperate and confused.

And they never once looked up at the shaved head and golden collar and saw the boy they'd thrown in a pit.

"He's testing them," Eli whispered.

"He's hurting them," Jordan said.

Both things were true.

Joseph ordered Simeon — one of the brothers who'd been cruelest to him — bound and held as a hostage. The others were to return to Canaan with grain, but they had to bring Benjamin. The youngest brother. Joseph's only full sibling. The one Jacob had kept close because he couldn't bear to lose another son.

The brothers staggered out of the courtyard, clutching their grain sacks, terrified and confused. They hadn't recognized Joseph. But guilt has its own memory. As they passed the column where the kids stood, Eli heard one of them say to the others, in a voice that shook: "This is happening because of what we did to our brother. We saw his anguish when he pleaded with us, and we would not listen."

Twenty years. And they still remembered his face in the pit.

Jordan heard it too. His expression didn't change, but his hand found the rubber band on his wrist and twisted it, once, hard.

## Chapter 7: The Choice

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*December 19, 2018 — Home office, midnight*
*I keep trying to write about forgiveness as an archaeologist — to ground it in evidence and context and rational analysis. But forgiveness isn't rational. It's the most irrational act in scripture. Joseph had power. Joseph had justification. Joseph had twenty years of suffering that any reasonable person would say earned him the right to vengeance. And he chose to weep instead. "I am Joseph. Is my father still alive?" That's the first thing he says. Not "how dare you," not "do you know what you did to me." His first thought was his father. His first question was about love. I don't have an archaeological framework for that. I'm not sure I have any framework at all.*

---

The brothers came back.

It took weeks. Eli lost count of the days — the Pathfinder held them in this time and place, and the days blurred into a rhythm of watching and waiting. The cold didn't leave. It settled into the city like a second climate, pressing against every bright surface, dulling every color. Joseph went about his work with mechanical precision, and the pain underneath got closer to the surface with every day. Eli saw him once, walking alone through the granary at dusk, running his hand along the jars of stored wheat with an expression that wasn't pride or satisfaction. It was remembrance. A man touching the evidence of God's provision and still not sure if it was enough to heal what had been taken from him.

Elena spent the waiting days pacing. She couldn't sit still in this city, couldn't just watch and do nothing while the cold pressed in. "I hate waiting," she said, more than once. "Waiting is the worst part of every adventure."

Jordan spent the days quietly. He asked Eli questions about the story — not skeptical questions, not "but how is this possible" questions. Real ones. "What happened to Joseph between the pit and the palace? How long was he in prison? Did he think God had forgotten him?" Eli told him what he knew, and Jordan listened with the stillness of someone collecting pieces to a puzzle he couldn't see the edges of yet.

Then one morning, Elena shook Eli awake. "They're here. And Benjamin's with them."

The courtyard was different this time. Joseph had arranged a meal — a feast, actually. Long tables set with Egyptian dishes, wine, bread, fruit. The brothers entered confused and frightened, expecting punishment, and found a banquet instead.

Benjamin stood among them. Younger than the others, slim, with Joseph's eyes and something of Joseph's way of standing. The brother Joseph shared a mother with. The last piece of the family Joseph had lost.

Joseph entered the room.

Eli watched his eyes go straight to Benjamin. For one second — one heartbeat — the mask cracked. Joseph's face opened with a raw, desperate tenderness that was almost too private to witness. He looked at Benjamin the way a drowning man looks at the surface.

Then the mask closed again. Joseph spoke through his interpreter, formal and distant. He directed Benjamin to a seat of honor. He served the brothers food. He watched them eat.

The cold was brutal now. Elena couldn't stop shivering. Jordan's vision had narrowed to a tunnel — Eli could see him turning his head constantly, trying to track things at the edges that kept going grey. The hum in Eli's chest was sharp and dissonant, the two notes grinding against each other like gears misaligned.

Joseph stood abruptly and left the room.

The kids followed.

He went to a small chamber off the main hall — a storeroom, private, unguarded. The door was barely closed before the composure shattered. Joseph pressed his back against the wall and slid down to the floor. His hands covered his face. His shoulders shook.

He was weeping.

Not quietly. Not with dignity. With the great, heaving, gasping sobs of a man who has held something inside for twenty years and can't hold it for one more second. The sound filled the small room and spilled into the corridor where three kids from the future stood perfectly still and listened to a heart breaking open.

Elena's eyes were streaming. She pressed her hand over her mouth.

Jordan stood against the far wall. He wasn't crying. His face had that closed look again — the door shut, everything locked away. But his hands were shaking. The rubber band on his wrist was stretched to its limit.

Eli didn't know what to do. He didn't know what to feel. He stood in the corridor of an Egyptian palace and listened to the most powerful man in the kingdom cry like a lost child, and something in him understood that this was the center of the story. Not the pit. Not the slavery. Not the rise to power. This — this moment of a man alone with his grief, deciding what to do with it.

The cold pressed in harder. The air in the corridor went thick and numb. The hum turned ugly — a grinding, discordant buzz that pushed against Eli's ribs like a fist.

*They don't deserve forgiveness. They never even asked for it. They don't even know who you are. Walk away. Send them home. Keep the mask. It's safer behind the mask.*

Eli could feel the pull of it — the bitter logic, the justified rage. It made sense. It was fair. Justice said these men should pay.

Joseph lifted his head. His face was wrecked — swollen, tear-streaked, red. But his eyes were clear. Clearer than Eli had seen them since they'd arrived in Egypt. Like the weeping had burned something away, and what was left underneath was older than the pain.

He stood up. He washed his face in a basin by the door. He straightened his collar and his rings. Then he walked back into the banquet hall.

The brothers looked up from their food. Joseph stood at the head of the table and looked at them. Each face. One by one. Twenty years of distance. Twenty years of betrayal and slavery and prison and power and this moment, right now, this choice that no amount of suffering had prepared him for.

"Everyone leave," Joseph said. His voice was thick and rough. "Everyone except these men."

The servants and guards withdrew. The doors closed. The room was suddenly very quiet — just Joseph and his brothers and three kids hidden in the shadows near the wall.

Joseph opened his mouth. The mask fell away for the last time.

"I am Joseph," he said. In Hebrew. His own language, his own voice, the voice of the boy they'd thrown in a pit. "Is my father still alive?"

The brothers didn't move. They stared at him — at the shaved head and the Egyptian clothes and the gold and the power — and slowly, horribly, recognition dawned. The color drained from their faces. One of them took a step backward. Another dropped to his knees. The broad-shouldered brother — the one who'd spat into the fire the day Jacob gave Joseph the coat — made a sound like he'd been hit.

They were terrified.

"Come closer," Joseph said. His voice broke. "Please. Come closer to me."

They came. Stumbling, shaking, some of them weeping.

"I am Joseph, your brother," he said. "The one you sold into Egypt." He paused, and Eli watched him fight for every word. "Don't be angry with yourselves for selling me here. God sent me ahead of you to save lives. The famine will last five more years. God sent me here to preserve you — to preserve our family."

He was crying again. Not the broken, private sobs from the storeroom. These tears were different — open, unstoppable, falling down his face while he spoke. And he reached for them. He pulled the nearest brother into his arms and held on with the grip of a man who had been holding himself together for twenty years and was finally, finally letting go.

The cold shattered.

Eli felt it happen — a crack that ran through the numbness like a fault line splitting, and then a rush of warmth that poured through the room like sunlight through broken clouds. The grey at the edges of the room burned away. Colors flooded back — the rich reds of the wall hangings, the gold of the dishes, the warm brown of Benjamin's eyes as he stared at his brother. Jordan's peripheral vision snapped back into focus. Elena gasped as the cold left her bones.

Joseph held his brothers and wept, and the room was bright.

And at Joseph's feet, on the stone floor where his tears had fallen, something glowed.

It was small. So small Eli almost missed it. A single thread — not a rope, not a strip of fabric, but one thread, barely as long as Eli's hand, caught in a crack in the stone. It was multicolored — red and blue and yellow, woven together so tightly that the colors blurred into each other at the edges. A thread from a coat that had been torn apart decades ago in a field in Canaan.

It glowed with a warm, steady light. Not amber like the stone. Not green like the branch. Something in between — a rich, shifting shimmer that held every color the coat had ever been, condensed into a single strand.

The hum surged. The two notes in Eli's chest found a third — high and clear and piercing, like a bell struck at dawn. Three notes, harmonizing, climbing.

"There," Eli whispered. "The thread."

Elena saw it. Jordan saw it.

Joseph was still holding his brothers. He wasn't looking at the floor. He wasn't looking at anything but the faces he hadn't seen in twenty years.

Eli knelt. The thread lay in the crack between two stones, glowing like a vein of gold in rock. He reached down and lifted it gently between his fingers.

It was warm. The same alive warmth as the stone from the creek, the same heartbeat-pulse. But this was different too — softer, more layered. The warmth of the stone had been clear and singular, like one voice singing. This warmth was complex, braided, the way forgiveness is complex — not a single act but a thousand small choices woven together.

Eli understood.

The thread wasn't from the moment the coat was torn. It was from this moment — the moment Joseph chose to reach for his brothers instead of pushing them away. The thread had survived the tearing and the blood and the decades because forgiveness doesn't undo the hurt. It carries the hurt forward into something new. The coat was destroyed. The thread remained. The family was broken. The love remained.

The light began to build.

"Hold on," Eli said.

Elena grabbed his arm. Jordan moved closer — not reluctantly this time, not standing apart. He pressed his shoulder against Eli's, and Eli felt him trembling.

The three notes climbed. The light rose from the thread and spread outward, golden and warm, filling the banquet hall. Joseph's voice faded. The stone walls softened. The last thing Eli saw was Joseph's face — open, raw, wrecked with grief and lit with something that had outlasted every year of darkness.

Then Egypt dissolved, and they were falling through light.

## Chapter 8: The Third Note

*From the journal of Professor Hector Castillo:*
*December 25, 2018 — Home office, Christmas morning*
*Elena called this morning. She asked me what the hardest part of forgiveness is. I said it depends on who hurt you. She said, "No, Abuelo. What's the hardest part?" I think she was testing me. I told her the truth: the hardest part is that it doesn't feel fair. It shouldn't work. The person who hurt you doesn't deserve it, and you don't feel strong enough to give it, and the whole thing is upside down and backwards from what justice demands. But that's the point. If forgiveness only happened when it was deserved, it wouldn't be forgiveness. It would be a transaction. And God doesn't deal in transactions. He deals in grace. I told her that, and she was quiet for a long time. Then she said, "I think I'm going to need to remember that." I wonder what she meant.*

---

Old books and cinnamon.

Eli opened his eyes. The study. The desk. The maps on the walls, the journals stacked in columns, the late afternoon light slanting through the window at exactly the same angle. The same moment. The same July afternoon. No time at all.

His right hand was open. The thread was gone.

But the warmth lingered — not just in his palm, this time, but in his chest, in the space behind his ribs where the hum lived. Three notes still resonated, fading slowly like the last ring of a bell.

Elena stood beside him, blinking, her arms wrapped around herself out of habit. The cold was gone, but her body hadn't caught up yet. She looked at her hands. She looked at Eli. She looked at the Pathfinder.

"Three," she whispered.

The Pathfinder sat on its leather cloth, unchanged in every way except one. The third symbol — a faint scratch that had once been barely visible, a rough suggestion of a thread — had transformed. The lines were sharp now, precise, each twist of the thread rendered in miniature detail. The surface within the engraving was polished smooth, and color filled it like enamel — shifting color, red-blue-yellow, woven and luminous. A glow pulsed from within, steady and warm.

Three symbols complete. The stone, the branch, the thread. Three notes humming in quiet harmony — low, middle, high — almost a chord. Almost music.

Nine rough scratches remained, faint and waiting.

Elena reached out and almost touched the Pathfinder, then pulled her hand back. "It's beautiful."

"It's growing," Eli said. He could feel it — not just the visual change but something deeper, a sense that the Pathfinder was becoming more itself with each relic. Like a song gaining verses.

Jordan stood by the window.

He hadn't spoken since they'd returned. He was staring out at the street — Abuelo's yard with its overgrown wildflowers, the stone birdbath listing to one side, the sidewalk where a kid was riding a bike in the late afternoon heat. His hoodie was pulled up. His arms were crossed. His glasses reflected the window glass, hiding his eyes.

"Jordan?" Elena said.

He didn't turn around.

"He forgave them," Jordan said. His voice was quiet and very careful, like he was assembling the words one at a time and checking each one before he let it go. "After everything. After the pit and the slavery and the prison. After twenty years. He forgave them."

The room was still.

"How?" Jordan said. "How does someone do that?"

Eli thought for a long time. The hum of the Pathfinder was soft in the silence — three notes, patient, unhurried.

"I don't think it was easy," Eli said finally. "Did you see his face? When he left the room? He wasn't choosing forgiveness because it felt good. He was choosing it because — because the alternative was worse. Holding on to it was killing him."

"But they didn't deserve it," Jordan said. "They didn't even ask for it. They didn't apologize. They didn't even know who he was until he told them."

"No. They didn't deserve it."

"Then why?"

"Maybe that's the point," Eli said slowly. "Maybe forgiveness isn't about what they deserve. Maybe it's about what you carry. Joseph could have punished them. He had the power. He had the right. But he would have had to keep carrying all of it — the anger, the bitterness, the memory of the pit. Forever. He chose to put it down."

Jordan was quiet. His finger traced a line on the window glass.

Elena, for once, didn't fill the silence. She sat on the edge of the desk and waited, watching Jordan with an expression Eli had never seen on his sister's face before — not impatience, not frustration, but a kind of fierce, protective gentleness. Like she understood that something was happening in the silence and she wasn't going to rush it.

"My dad sent a birthday card last month," Jordan said. He said it to the window, not to them. "It was two weeks late. He spelled my name wrong on the envelope. He wrote 'Jordon.' With an O."

Nobody spoke.

"It's not the same thing," Jordan said quickly. "I know it's not the same as being sold into slavery. It's stupid. It's a birthday card."

"It's not stupid," Eli said.

"It is, though. Joseph's brothers threw him in a pit. My dad just — he just left. He packed his bags and drove away and started a new life, and sometimes he remembers I exist and sometimes he doesn't, and I'm supposed to be fine with that because at least nobody sold me to a caravan."

His voice was steady. Too steady. The kind of steady that takes effort.

"Jordan," Elena said. Just his name. Nothing else.

Jordan's jaw worked. The rubber band on his wrist was stretched between his thumb and forefinger, trembling.

"I don't know if I can do what Joseph did," Jordan said. So quietly that Eli barely heard it. "I don't know if I'm — I don't know."

"You don't have to know right now," Eli said.

Jordan turned from the window. His face was open — not guarded, not skeptical, not hidden behind the dry humor and the crossed arms. Just open. A kid standing in front of something too big to understand, not running from it, not pretending it wasn't there.

He looked at the Pathfinder. Three symbols glowing. Three notes humming.

"What's next?" he asked.

Eli picked up Abuelo's journal from the desk — the one he'd been reading when the Pathfinder activated that morning. He turned past the entry about Avaris, past sketches of pottery fragments and trade route maps, to a page near the back. A drawing in quick pencil strokes: a long, straight staff. Notes in the margins in Abuelo's tight handwriting: *Exodus 4:2. "What is that in your hand?" A shepherd's rod. An ordinary tool transformed by obedience. The staff that split the sea.* Below the sketch, circled twice in faded ink: *A pharaoh. A deliverer. A man who ran from his calling.*

Elena leaned over his shoulder. "Moses?"

"I think so."

"Let's go," she said. And then caught herself, looking at Jordan. "I mean — whenever. Whenever you're ready."

Jordan pushed his glasses up. The corner of his mouth twitched — not quite a smile, but the memory of one. "I mean, technically we don't get to choose when the magical bronze disc activates, so 'whenever we're ready' is kind of irrelevant."

"There he is," Elena said.

The Pathfinder hummed its three-note chord in the fading light, patient as a prayer. Outside, the evening was just beginning. Crickets tuned up in Abuelo's garden. The wind chimes on the porch made their soft, key-shaped music.

Eli closed the journal and set it on the desk. Three promises preserved. Three moments of God's faithfulness carved into bronze. Nine more waiting.

He looked at his sister, who was already reaching for another notebook. He looked at Jordan, who was leaning against the doorframe with his hands in his pockets and an expression on his face that was no longer skepticism. It was something newer than that. Something that didn't have a name yet but was growing — slowly, the way real things grow — in the space between questions and answers.

The thread was gone. The forgiveness remained.

And somewhere, on a desk in a hidden study in a house that smelled like old books and cinnamon, three notes harmonized in the dark, waiting for the fourth.
