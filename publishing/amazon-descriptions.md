## Book 1: The Stone of Courage

<p><b>Eli already knew the story of David and Goliath. He just never expected to be standing in the valley when it happened.</b></p>

<p>Twelve-year-old Eli Castillo and his ten-year-old sister Elena are spending the summer at their grandfather's house — a creaky Victorian stuffed with ancient artifacts, maps in six languages, and more secrets than any kid could explore in a lifetime. But when Abuelo lands in the hospital, the summer takes a sharp turn.</p>

<p>Behind a hidden bookcase, the kids and their skeptical neighbor Jordan Park discover a mysterious bronze device called the Pathfinder. The moment Eli touches it, the three of them are pulled backward through time — <i>three thousand years backward</i> — into the Valley of Elah, where a terrified army faces a giant no one will fight.</p>

<p>A strange fog creeps along the edges of the valley. Colors fade. Soldiers who were brave yesterday can barely lift their eyes. Something is <i>erasing</i> this moment from memory — and if the kids can't find the ancient relic hidden in this story, the courage that changed history might be lost forever.</p>

<p><b>But here's what Sunday School never told them:</b> standing fifty feet from a nine-foot giant is nothing like hearing about it in a classroom.</p>

<p>The Stone of Courage is the first book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — an action-packed adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. With vivid time travel, an unforgettable trio of heroes, and the kind of danger that makes you hold your breath, this is the series that makes ancient stories feel like they're happening <i>right now</i>.</p>

The only strange thing is the enormous wooden structure on the hill and the old man building it while his neighbors laugh.</p>

<p>Noah has been working for years. His sons hammer in grim silence. The whole town thinks he's lost his mind. And the sky? The sky has never been clearer.</p>

<p>Jordan, who doesn't believe in much of anything, finds himself face to face with a man who gave up <i>everything</i> for something he believed. And for the first time, Jordan doesn't have a comeback.</p>

<p><b>The Branch of Promise</b> is the second book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. When the dove finally returns, what it carries will change everything.</p>

<p>The Pathfinder pulls Eli, Elena, and Jordan into the dusty hills of Canaan — straight into a family that's about to tear itself apart. A father's favorite son. A coat that marks him as special. And a circle of brothers whose jealousy has curdled into something dangerous.</p>

<p>When Joseph is betrayed, sold to slave traders, and dragged away from everything he knows, the kids witness something that will haunt them long after they return home. The brothers dip the beautiful coat in blood. They carry it to their father. They watch him weep.</p>

<p>Then the Pathfinder does something it's never done before: it jumps <i>forward</i>. Years forward. To Egypt, where a powerful vizier holds the fate of nations in his hands — and the brothers who destroyed his life are kneeling at his feet, begging for food.</p>

<p>The Fade has a new weapon. Not fog. Not doubt. <i>Bitterness.</i> It presses against Joseph's old wound, whispering: <i>They deserve to suffer. Make them pay.</i></p>

<p>For Jordan — whose own father left without looking back — this story cuts deep. Forgiveness isn't supposed to be possible after something like this. Is it?</p>

<p><b>The Thread of Forgiveness</b> is the third book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. In a story about betrayal, slavery, and impossible forgiveness, one thread from a torn coat holds the answer to a question every kid has asked: <i>How do you forgive someone who doesn't deserve it?</i></p>

## Book 4: The Staff of Deliverance

<p><b>"Send someone else." Moses said it to God. Eli has been thinking it his whole life.</b></p>

<p>When the Pathfinder drops the three kids into the desert beside Moses, Eli sees something he recognizes instantly: a person who knows the right answers but freezes when it's time to act. Moses doesn't want this job. He's not brave enough, not eloquent enough, not <i>enough</i>. And Eli — the kid who always knows what to say in his head but can't get the words out when it matters — understands that feeling in his bones.</p>

<p>But the Fade has embedded itself in Egypt like a second empire. It's the crushing weight of Pharaoh's certainty, the impossibility of freedom, the voice that says <i>nothing will ever change</i>. The plagues escalate. Pharaoh's heart hardens. And on the darkest night in Egypt's history, the kids learn what deliverance really costs.</p>

<p>Then comes the sea.</p>

<p>An army behind them. Water ahead. Hundreds of thousands of people with nowhere to go. And a man holding a wooden staff — just a stick, really — who has finally stopped asking <i>why me</i> and started trusting the One who called him.</p>

<p>Meanwhile, back in the present, the phone rings. Abuelo's voice, thin but clear: <i>"The staff was never magic, mijo. It was just a stick. Remember that."</i></p>

<p><b>The Staff of Deliverance</b> is the fourth book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. God doesn't call the qualified. He qualifies the called.</p>

<p>For Elena Castillo — the girl who found the hidden room first, who wanted to fight Goliath herself, who has never once in her life been accused of patience — this is torture.</p>

<p>And the Fade knows it.</p>

The people of Jericho mock from the walls. And Elena, feet bleeding from the endless marching, nearly breaks rank on Day Six — triggering the biggest fight she and Eli have ever had.</p>

<p><b>The Horn of Obedience</b> is the fifth book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. The walls are about to fall. But first, you have to trust the silence.</p>

<p><b>Daniel's enemies searched his entire life for a flaw. The only thing they found was his faithfulness.</b></p>

Daniel has risen to the highest levels of the Persian empire, and the officials who can't outperform him have found another way to destroy him: make his greatest strength illegal.</p>

<p>A new decree: no prayer to any god except the king. For thirty days. The penalty is the lions' den. And everyone in the court knows exactly who this law is designed to trap.</p>

<p>The Fade arrives wearing a new disguise — the voice of reason. <i>Just close the curtains. Pray silently. God will understand. It's only thirty days.</i> The whisper is so sensible, so practical, that even the kids feel its pull. Why risk everything over a habit? Why die for something no one else would even notice?</p>

<p>Daniel opens his window. He kneels toward Jerusalem. He prays out loud. As he always has.</p>

<p>In the terrible night that follows — Daniel in the den, King Darius pacing in anguish, the kids huddled in darkness listening for sounds they don't want to hear — the three Covenant Keepers face their most unsettling realization yet: the Fade isn't just atmospheric anymore. It's putting thoughts directly into their heads.</p>

<p>Something has shifted. The enemy is learning. And the next six adventures just got a lot more dangerous.</p>

<p><b>The Seal of Faithfulness</b> is the sixth book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. Integrity isn't what you do when the world is watching. It's who you are when the lions are waiting.</p>

## Book 7: The Altar Stone

<p><b>Four hundred and fifty prophets called on their god for hours. Nothing answered. Not a whisper. Not a spark.</b></p>

<p>The Pathfinder hurls Eli, Elena, and Jordan onto Mount Carmel — straight into the most lopsided showdown in sacred history. On one side: four hundred and fifty prophets of Baal, backed by a corrupt king and queen who've turned an entire nation away from its God. On the other side: one exhausted, afraid prophet named Elijah who hasn't slept in days and is running on nothing but stubborn faith.</p>

<p>The odds are absurd. Elena loves it. Jordan finds it reckless.</p>

<p>But here's what none of them expected: the real tension isn't the fire. It's the <i>silence</i>. Hours of prophets screaming at the sky, begging their god to answer. Cutting themselves. Dancing. Pleading. And the silence that answers back is the loudest thing the kids have ever heard.</p>

<p>Then Elijah builds an altar. Twelve stones. He drenches it in water — three times, until the trench overflows. And the Fade rolls in thick, because <i>this</i> is the moment it's been waiting for. If it can't erase the fire, it will rewrite what the fire means. <i>Lightning strike. Weather anomaly. Coincidence.</i></p>

<p>When Elijah prays — simple, direct, no theatrics — fire from heaven consumes everything. Altar. Stones. Water. All of it.</p>

<p>For Eli, this adventure is personal. He's the thinker, the careful one, the kid who's never been the "stand alone" type. But this story demands something new from him: the courage to choose truth when the entire crowd disagrees.</p>

<p><b>The Altar Stone</b> is the seventh book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. When every voice says one thing and the truth says another, which side do you stand on?</p>

<p><b>Five symbols remain. The Fade is rewriting the meaning of miracles. And standing alone has never felt so costly.</b></p>

## Book 8: The Coin of Second Chances

<p><b>Jonah ran from God. Jordan has been running from faith. Neither of them saw the storm coming.</b></p>

<p>The Pathfinder activates at the halfway point — seven relics found, five still waiting — and drops the kids into a sun-bleached harbor at Joppa. A prophet named Jonah is buying a one-way ticket in the wrong direction. He knows exactly what God asked him to do. He's doing the opposite.</p>

<p>And Jordan Park, the skeptic who's been questioning everything since Book 1, has never identified with anyone more.</p>

<p>When the storm hits, it's not metaphorical. The ship pitches in waves that shouldn't exist on a clear day. Sailors who've weathered a hundred voyages are screaming prayers to every god they know. Jonah — the only man who knows the truth — is asleep below deck.</p>

<p>Then comes the great fish. Yes, the kids experience the inside. Dark. Claustrophobic. Awful. Jonah's prayer from the belly of the deep is raw and terrified and <i>real</i>.</p>

<p>But the whale isn't the real story. Nineveh is.</p>

<p>When the most wicked city in the ancient world repents — every last person, from the king to the cattle — it should feel like a miracle. Instead, Jonah is <i>furious</i>. He wanted them destroyed. They were enemies. They didn't deserve mercy. And the Fade feeds that rage like oxygen to a flame: <i>They don't deserve forgiveness.</i></p>

<p>It's one of the most insidious lies the kids have ever encountered. Because part of them — especially Jordan — wonders if Jonah might be right.</p>

<p><b>The Coin of Second Chances</b> is the eighth book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. The relic doesn't appear where Jonah arrived. It appears where he <i>ran</i>. Because second chances begin at the moment you admit you're running.</p>

<p><b>Four symbols remain. The pattern is emerging. And Jordan just said something that surprised everyone — including himself.</b></p>

## Book 9: The Gleaning Sheaf

<p><b>No army. No giant. No fire from heaven. Just a widow, a foreigner, and a field. This might be the bravest story yet.</b></p>

<p>After seven adventures full of giants and floods and furnaces, the Pathfinder sends Eli, Elena, and Jordan to the quietest place they've ever been: a barley field outside Bethlehem. Two widows have just arrived with nothing — no money, no status, no future. One of them, a foreign woman named Ruth, bends down and begins to pick up the leftover grain behind the harvesters.</p>

<p>Elena wants to scream. <i>Where's the battle? When does the exciting part start?</i></p>

<p>The Fade knows exactly how to exploit this. Its strategy changes completely — no fog, no fear, no creeping cold. Instead, it whispers. <i>This story doesn't matter. It's too small. Too ordinary. Not worth remembering.</i> And for the first time, Elena almost believes it.</p>

<p>But day after day, Ruth shows up. Backbreaking work. The other gleaners avoid her — she's a foreigner, a Moabite, an outsider. She bends, gathers, carries. Again and again. Not because it's dramatic, but because the person she loves needs her to.</p>

<p>When a landowner named Boaz notices her and quietly tells his workers to leave extra grain in her path, Elena sees something she didn't expect: bravery that doesn't look like bravery. Kindness that costs something. Loyalty so fierce it doesn't need a sword.</p>

<p><b>The Gleaning Sheaf</b> is the ninth book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. The relic appears at the moment of ordinary faithfulness, not dramatic triumph. Because sometimes the hardest kind of courage is simply showing up.</p>

<p><b>Three symbols remain. The Fade has arrived before the kids now. And the quiet stories might be the ones that matter most.</b></p>

## Book 10: The Crown of Courage

<p><b>"If I perish, I perish." Four words. One queen. The fate of an entire people hanging on whether she's brave enough to speak.</b></p>

<p>The Pathfinder pulls harder than ever, dragging the kids into the most dangerous setting they've encountered: the Persian palace of King Xerxes, where gold drips from the ceiling and one wrong word means death.</p>

<p>A young queen named Esther has been hiding a secret. She's Jewish — and a court official named Haman has just signed a decree to annihilate her people. Every Jewish man, woman, and child in the empire. The date is set. The orders are sealed.</p>

<p>Only one person can stop it. And approaching the king uninvited is punishable by death.</p>

<p>The Fade doesn't need to be supernatural here. The political reality is terrifying enough. It saturates the palace, pressing against Esther's every rational instinct: <i>Stay quiet. Stay safe. Someone else will speak up. You're just one person.</i></p>

<p>Mordecai's words cut through the fog like a blade: <i>"Who knows whether you have come to the kingdom for such a time as this?"</i></p>

<p>For Eli and Elena — both tested on what it means to use their voice when silence is safer — this adventure strikes close to home. The Fade is now openly aggressive, targeting stories before the kids arrive, racing to erase them. The endgame is approaching, and the Pathfinder hums almost constantly.</p>

<p>When Esther takes the longest walk of her life — down the corridor toward a king who holds her life in his hands — a jewel from her crown catches the light with a glow the kids have come to recognize.</p>

<p><b>The Crown of Courage</b> is the tenth book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. The relic appears when Esther chooses to speak — not when her people are saved. Because the courage is the point, not the outcome.</p>

<p><b>Two symbols remain. The Fade is hunting. And the Pathfinder knows what's at stake.</b></p>

## Book 11: The Fourth in the Fire

<p><b>"Our God is able to deliver us. But even if He does not — we will not bow."</b></p>

<p>Ten slots filled on the Pathfinder. Its hum is constant now, almost a melody. And the Fade has stopped waiting for the kids to arrive — it's already there when they land in Babylon, saturating an empire that demands total allegiance.</p>

<p>Nebuchadnezzar's golden statue towers over the plain of Dura. The decree is absolute: when the music plays, everyone bows. <i>Everyone.</i> The political machinery is terrifying — not a villain, but a system. An empire that crushes individual conscience under the weight of conformity.</p>

<p>Three young men refuse to kneel. While an entire plain of people bows, they stand. Elena, who has spent the series redefining what bravery means, whispers: <i>"That's the bravest thing I've ever seen."</i></p>

<p>Their declaration shakes Jordan to his core: <i>We believe our God can save us. But even if He doesn't, we won't bow.</i> Faith that doesn't require rescue. Faith that holds without a guarantee. Jordan's entire framework — <i>show me proof and I'll believe</i> — has no answer for people who believe regardless of outcome.</p>

<p>The furnace is heated seven times hotter. The soldiers who throw them in don't survive the heat. And then Nebuchadnezzar leaps to his feet: <i>"Did we not throw three men in? I see four — and the fourth is like a son of the gods."</i></p>

<p>The kids see the fourth figure too. Not rescuing from outside. <i>Present inside.</i> Walking with them through the fire. It's the most peaceful scene in the entire series — and the most mysterious. Jordan sees just enough to know that something fundamental has shifted in him. He can't name it. He doesn't try.</p>

<p><b>The Fourth in the Fire</b> is the eleventh book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. God doesn't always prevent the fire. Sometimes He walks through it with you.</p>

<p><b>One symbol remains. The melody is almost complete. And the last adventure will cost more than all the others combined.</b></p>

## Book 12: The Thorn of Promise

<p><b>A father. A son. A mountain. A knife. And a question that echoes across all of history: where is the lamb?</b></p>

<p>The final slot on the Pathfinder is empty. The pull is almost violent. Abuelo, home at last, watches the three kids prepare for their last adventure with an expression they've never seen on his face — not worry, not pride, but something older. Like a man watching a promise he's waited fifty years to see fulfilled.</p>

<p>They land on a dry, scrubby mountainside. A father and a teenage boy walk uphill in silence. The boy carries wood on his back. The father carries fire and a knife. There is no lamb.</p>

<p>The kids recognize the story immediately. Elena goes pale. Even Jordan knows this one.</p>

<p>Then the Fade arrives — and for the first time in twelve adventures, it doesn't target the Biblical story. It targets <i>them</i>.</p>

<p>Jordan hears it in his deepest wound: <i>Fathers leave. Fathers sacrifice their children for their own reasons. This God is no different.</i> Elena feels an exhaustion deeper than she's ever known: <i>This will never end. There will always be another test.</i> Eli's careful mind is overwhelmed: <i>You'll never have all the answers.</i></p>

<p>The Fade has saved its cruelest weapon for last — truths adjacent to lies, aimed at real pain.</p>

<p>But when the angel calls out, when the ram appears caught in the thicket, when Abraham names the place <i>Jehovah Jireh</i> — "The Lord <i>Will</i> Provide," future tense, a promise still being kept — the completed Pathfinder unfolds into something none of them expected. Not a weapon. Not an answer. A <i>map</i>. Twelve points of light, twelve promises preserved, all converging on one mountain where the center is still empty. Still waiting.</p>

<p>The melody resolves into a sustained note — beautiful, complete, and unmistakably the first phrase of a longer song.</p>

<p><b>The Thorn of Promise</b> is the twelfth and final book in the <b>Covenant Keepers</b> series — a time-travel adventure through sacred history for readers ages 8-12. Every promise has been preserved. Every character has been tested. And the map of God's faithfulness points to something that hasn't arrived yet — but will.</p>

<p><b>Twelve symbols. Twelve promises. One story that's bigger than all of them.</b></p>
